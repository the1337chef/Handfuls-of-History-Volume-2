Haladie

Circa 1850

The warriors of the Hindu states who survived through the era of the Mughal Empire used a unique and diverse array of weapons. The multi-bladed haladie are among the more rare and most unique.

Most Indian haladie are believed to have come from the Rajput states of northwestern India. At the beginning of the twentieth century there were about 20 "princely states" in the region known as Rajputana. These were incorporated into the Republic of India in 1949. Some haladie have only two blades, and the earliest in this form are thought to have evolved from Fakir horns, which linked two antelope horns. The early Fakir weapons were made from just the actual horns, presumably as a ritual observance of a prohibition against metal weapons. However, later weapons had metal tips or were made entirely of steel in the shape of horns. To improve its usefulness as a defensive parrying weapon the madu form incorporated a small shield in the center.

The haladie makes no pretense of being from animal horns, but the original style of the weapon's use may have some origins in the operation of the madu. Most haladie have a sort of knuckle guard over the center grip and when this is present there is also most often a third blade for using the weapon as a punch dagger, like a katar. There are also related parrying weapons that have just a defensive bar, knuckle guard, and punch blade. These are known as sainti. Haladie like this example, however, trump all the other related forms with two robust recurved double edged dagger blades which must have allowed for a variety of complex attacks, especially considering that it was still probably most often used as a left-hand weapon, with a sword, axe, or spear carried in the right. It is hard to imagine mastering such a complicated weapon with four slashing edges and three piercing points.

The double-fullered blades are very sharp and made of quality high-carbon steel. The tips are slightly thickened for strength. The central grip is ivory. The leather of the scabbard is possibly original and certainly older than the decorative silver tips. I am at something of a loss for how a weapon, which would have needed three separate scabbards, would have been carried or brought into service. It perhaps not surprising that the scabbard for the punch blade has been lost.

Note that some simple double bladed knives coming out of the Sudan are also referred to as haladie.
