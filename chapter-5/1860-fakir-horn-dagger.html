Circa 1860

Fakir is a name applied to Hindu or Sufi Muslim ascetics who might at first glance appear more like street beggars than holy men. Traditionally, as part of their denial of earthly goods, they are forbidden to use metal weapons. Apparently, though even for impoverished holy men, the world is not always a safe place. The weapons most commonly associated with the fakir are paired antelope, "fakir horns." However, as often as not, fakir horns are found with metal tips on the end of the horns, belying a legitimate connection to the Fakir prohibition on metal weapons and instead connecting them the large variety of Indian weapons, particularly parrying weapons such as the madu.

This dagger, carved out of a bull's horn, is another Fakir weapon, one that clearly does not violate the prohibition on metal weapons. It is strictly a thrusting weapon without sharp edges. One side is highly polished horn while the other side shows the grain of the horn. The original shape of the horn likely determined the shape of the dagger. These daggers are rare, and as on this example, are usually inscribed with decorative circles. Only one other example is in Artzi's archive, and Google search fails to find others. However, there are three similar examples in Oxford's Pitt Rivers museum, which were acquired in the mid 1800s.
