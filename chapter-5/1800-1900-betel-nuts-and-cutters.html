Circa1800-1900

"Bloody Mary's chewing betel nuts, she is always chewing betel nuts...." For many Westerners, these lines from Rogers and Hammerstein's musical is the extent of their knowledge of the fourth-most common drug habit in the world, after alcohol, nicotine, and caffeine. The term "betel nuts," however, is something of a misnomer, as the psychoactive agents associated with this habit come from the seed of the Areca catechu palm, prepared in combination with other elements into packets known as betel quids. For the peoples of South Asia, the habitual use of areca nut products remains common in many social contexts, although not so universally as it was a hundred years ago. Much of the history and culture of South Asia involves the use of areca nuts, from hallucinogenic carvings of the Mandau swords of Dyak head hunter, to the elegant accoutrements for royal use of betel quids in Asian courts. The first encounters of European merchants and dignitaries with Asian nobility often required the sometimes uncomfortable visitor to share betel quid with the monarch, occasionally from the monarch's own mouth. In 1998 Queen Elizabeth II was presented with a silver betel nut box by the Sultan of Brunei. On the seedier side of betel nut use, it is still common to see sidewalks and walls besmirched with crimson betel spittle in poor neighborhoods of India and parts of Asia.

The association between areca nuts and "betel" comes from the common use of the leaves of the vine Piper betle to wrap slices of areca nut, along with slaked lime, and often other ingredients, into the packet known as a quid (see photos). The lime, often produced from burning seashells or coral, has the effect of alkalinizing the oral cavity and thus improving the absorption of the plant's alkaloids, similar to the native customs for chewing coca leaves in Peru. The details of preferred areca preparations vary significantly across Asia, and in recent decades, have included some form of tobacco for about 50 percent of the users, although the key ingredient is always areca nut.

The preparation of betel quid requires chopping the nuts into slivers suitable for mixing with the other ingredients of the quid. The ripe nuts are hard and difficult, even dangerous, to cut with an ordinary knife, so special cutters are employed which are safer to use and provide leverage for cutting. All of the cutters in this small group came from India, but betel use is common throughout all of South Asia and the cutters used in different areas have distinct styles of design, while all are functionally equivalent.

The first three cutters shown were purchased together as a lot from an American vendor, the rest were purchased through eBay from vendors in India. All but one are brass with steel blades.

The holes on the largest of the first three probably once held small bells or other decorative baubles. There is a maker's mark in the form of a dagger on the smaller curved one and an inscription on the straight bladed cutter. The inscription is in Gujarati, a language spoken in the northwest region of India. The first two letters can be the name of the shop or the name of the person who made it. The third word is a little unclear. The last word is the name of the city, Jamngar, which is in the region of Gujarat. It says: Ka-sa-ra Ji-va-ra-j Shro-pa-ta Jaa-ma-na-ga-r, phonetically: (C)ut-(Sa)sha-D(ra)ft (Je)ans-(Ve)rse-D(ra)ft-(J)ump (Same as it sounds) (Jaa)-(ma)-Win(ne)r-Dig(ger).

An areca nut is shown in the pictures, once cut, the nut shows veins of red pigment in its interior. One of the active molecules in the nut is arecoline, which we have studied in my laboratory. It stimulates receptors of the parasympathetic nervous system for several types of effects, including the copious production of saliva, which picks up a red color from the nut's pigments. Areca is not a controlled substance and not only can whole nuts be easily purchased, but also foil packets of prepared "pan" can be purchased on the internet. The packets, known as pan masala, are popular with Asian immigrants to the United Kingdom and elsewhere, as well as in more urban areas of India. In one picture the areca pieces are separated from the fennel seed and other ingredients used to flavor this brand of pan. An even more insidiously addictive preparation is Guthka, which includes tobacco.

Animal shapes are popular designs for betel cutters and there are several shown that have the shape of fanciful birds, probably peacocks. One cutter has a double-edged blade of a diamond shape. This is sometimes referred to as a katar style cutter, as the handles can be folded back and the blade used as a weapon. This type of cutter is usually from the Deccan, the plateau region of Southern India.

There is an example of a decorative guillotine-style cutter, probably from Rajasthan. This one has many of the jingles still attached. There is also another larger guillotine-style cutter, which has a bird's head projection. This piece is solid iron and large enough that it may have been used to cut areca in large quantity by a vendor of prepared quids. It is likely from Bengal or Rajasthan.

Betel was commonly considered as aphrodisiac, and the second to last cutter shown is in the form of a husband and wife (lovers). As is often the case with this style of cutter, the male is disproportionately larger than the female. There is an extra hinge on the female piece so that it can be moved to take different poses, distancing her, or putting her in an erotic embrace with her mate. The actual blade on this cutter is small and it would not have been useful to cut whole nuts but could have been used for the final preparation of a quid.

The next cutter shown is from Burma (Myanmar), while all the others are from India. It is vaguely anthropomorphic and has the unusual feature of having a face looking out from the hinge, but with the character's hand hiding the face, as if in shame. This cutter came with the large, beautifully hand-painted lacquer betel nut box, with nesting compartments for storing nuts, pann (betel vine leaves), and other items for the quid. This box appears to be relatively new, showing little wear, and no evidence of the effects of the caustic lime used in typical betel quids.

The second betel nut box shown is a type known in the Philippines as a lutuan. Such items were common and sometimes very elegant accessories, as was certainly the case with the silver betel box the Sultan of Brunei presented Queen Elizabeth II. The one shown comes from a museum collection and has been attributed to the Philippine island of Mindanao. The museum card that came with the box is dated 1967 and supports the Philippines as the site of origin. The style of the inlays is Islamic. The author cited on the card suggests that a woman would have used this form of box. There are three compartments in the heavy silver-inlaid bronze box, presumably for the three principle ingredients of a betel quid, the areca nut, betel leaves, and the slaked lime (calcium hydroxide). Some betel leaves are shown in the pictures but since these are perishable, I have not given over a storage compartment for them. Rather, I show whole areca nuts in the left compartment and sliced nuts in the central compartment. The right compartment certainly at one time stored the lime since it shows signs of corrosion from that caustic material. I will continue to use that side to store lime, but will keep the lime in a glass vial to prevent further corrosion.

The last cutter shown is also from Burma (Myanmar), or at least it was shipped from Myanmar by my colleague, Sam Glatman, who is developing oral health products for low-income betel quid users. Its style is basically similar to the cutters from India, although it is solid brass while the ones from India typically have an iron or steel blade. Sam also sent the smaller lacquer betel nut box. The basic design of this box, with nesting compartments, is similar to the larger one that I bought on eBay. While the painting on the larger box is very decorative, incorporating floral motifs and fanciful creatures, the paintings on this box are more directly evocative of Hindu or Buddhist mythology. They are similar to the scenes from the Ramayana that are on the lontar scroll. Demons appear to chase a warrior woman and a male figure that is not quite human, since it appears to have a tail. The significance of the two-headed chicken is also a mystery to me.

Unfortunately, it is now appreciated that betel use increases the risk of cancer and other oral diseases. While in the past, betel use was the habit of Asian royalty; in India it is now most common among those with low education and income. In other parts of Asia, such as Myanmar and Taiwan, betel use has increased in recent decades, perhaps associated with industrialization and popularity with an urban working class. In Taiwan scantly clad women often sell betel from roadside kiosks. Many of their customers are truck divers who consider it a necessary stimulant to keep them alert on long trips.
