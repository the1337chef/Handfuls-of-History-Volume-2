Double-Bladed Bichawa

Circa 1780

The bichawa is an old type of South Asian weapon, with a history going back to medieval India. Its origins were probably in the Hindu kingdoms of southern India and they are often associated with the tribes known as the Marathas. The name comes from the Hindi word for scorpion. This one dates to the time of the Mughal Empire, although it is likely that it originated in one of the client Hindu kingdoms of the Empire.

The dagger has a nice solid cast brass handle that might have even served as sort of brass knuckles. Bichawa daggers had a reputation for use in sort of down-and-dirty fighting or as the chosen weapons of assassins. This example has a double blade, which is sometimes seen in Indian weapons, although this style of blade might have actually originated in Arabia. The sword that belonged to Ali, son-in-law of Mohammed was called Zulfiqar, and legend has it that it was also double bladed. No one knows for sure what Zulfiqar looked like, but at least in Persian and Indian Islamic art its blade often resembles that of this dagger. Images of the Zulfiqar on the historical flags of Shiite nations vary, sometimes showing swords that are just split at the tip and others showing curious V-shaped double blades.

The construction of a bichawa is basically rather simple, especially compared to some other Hindu daggers such as the khanjarli, chillanum, or a tiger tooth dagger. With a cast handle and a double blade, this dagger is a bit more elegant than the simplest of bichawas. In their simplest form they are no more than a blade riveted to a loop of steel, which serves as the handle. This results with the blade being perpendicular to the plane of the grip, an uncommon feature found only on other sorts of loop dagger such as those used by the TIV people of Africa. On this dagger, the blade is parallel to the grip, which would make it perpendicular to the palm of the hand, as is usual on other sorts of daggers, but less common on a bichawa. Blades mounted in this fashion are most common on bichawas with cast brass handles.
