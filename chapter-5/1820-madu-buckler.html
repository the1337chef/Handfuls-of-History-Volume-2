1820

The madu (sometimes maru or maduvu) is a parrying weapon that was popular among the Marathas in the early 19th century and would be used in combination with an offensive weapon such as a sword. The weapons most likely originated with the Dravidians of south India. The name comes from the Hindu name for the black buck deer, which were the source of the horns. The horns are linked by cross bars where they form a handle. To increase the effectiveness of the horns as weapons they were often steel tipped.

Fakir horns are a related weapon, lacking the central shield, which adds to the madu's effectiveness in combat as a sort of left-hand weapon. The term Fakir refers to Muslim Sufi holy men who typically were itinerate and living on alms. They renounced relationships and earthly possessions, especially refined objects such as metal weapons. Presumably weapons made of horn were more permissible. Such horn weapons were also sometime associated with Hindu Jogi (Yogi).

Unlike the Fakir horns, the madu was a true weapon. In some parts of India there were similar weapons with the horns made entirely of steel. The horns on this piece are 25 inches tip-to-tip, ending with steel points that are made for piercing armor, not cutting flesh. The central shield protecting the hand is six inches in diameter with four raised bosses that might have served to deflect blows. The caps on the ends of the horns are firmly attached.
