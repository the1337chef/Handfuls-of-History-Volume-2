Circa 1825

India has a rich history of great civilizations and has also been one of the key points where European and Asian cultures have confronted each other and exchanged influences, from the time of Alexander to the British Empire. With all this great drama some peoples were yet able to exist on the sidelines, aboriginal peoples whose cultures were rooted before the arrival of the Aryans, and whose ancient beliefs shaped the evolution of the Hindu faiths. One such aboriginal people were the Orissa (alternatively, Kurukh or Oraons) of the Chota Nagpur Plateau, which is in the far east of India, near Bengal and Bangladesh.

The Kurukh people maintain a distinct culture, following the Sarna religion, which, while of Hindu style in some regards, is more strongly rooted in animist philosophy. It is likely that their culture is related to the lost people of the early Indus civilization. The Orissa society was primarily agricultural, and their remote location kept them largely off the path of the great empire builders in India.

This style of war axe, or tabar, known as the bullova, is uniquely associated with the Orissa people. It is from the early 19th century and can be identified by the curious moustache shaped blade. While it lacks the sophistication and elegance Mughal or Rajput weapons it would have served a simple farmer to defend his home and family.
