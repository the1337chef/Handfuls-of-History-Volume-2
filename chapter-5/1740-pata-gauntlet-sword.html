Circa 1740

The push dagger or katar is a very old style of weapon unique to India, made to be gripped in the palm of the hand and pushed forward with the force of the arm. The blade of a katar is usually wedge shaped and often was thickened at the tip for the purpose of being able to pierce armor. Because if its design, the katar was probably a weapon that was best used for a single rapid strike rather than for extended sword play. A traditional katar also provides relatively little protection for the user's hand, aside from the top and bottom bars of the grip, which might also serve to hold it rigid on the forearm. This deficiency in hand protection was improved on "hooded" katars that incorporated a cup type hand guard. The ultimate improvement on this line was the gauntlet sword or pata, which had a full form-fitting metal glove surrounding the grip and blade mounting

The pata developed and was most widely used during the time of the Mughal Empire. However, use of the pata was primarily by the non-Muslim peoples of India, such as the Marathas and Rajput. The weapon is especially associated with the Marathas and in Marathi it was known as a "dandpatta." Often the Maratha warriors would be equipped with two pata, presenting a fearsome image as they would advance with whirling blades.

This pata has a 37-inch, double-edge, thin and very flexible blade. The gauntlet is made of brass with small bells and rattlers. Its total length is 49.5 inches. It is overall in good condition. There are a few holes in the handle, the purpose of which is not very clear, but perhaps they once served to attach a cloth liner. There are some marks on the blade of unknown significance.
