Circa 1690

This is a very old style of dagger associated with the Kafirs of a region known as the Hindu Kush on the northern boarder of Afghanistan and modern day Pakistan. The region, formerly known as Kafiristan, is now called Nuristan. The Kafirs are an ancient people, ethnically distinct from the Pashtun tribes of Afghanistan and the people of India. They have features more in common with people of the Caucuses. It is an oft-told legend that they are descents of the Greek soldiers of Alexander.

For many centuries, the Kafirs resisted conversion to Islam and retained the practice of an old Indo-European animistic faith, some aspects of which even predated Hinduism. Islam was finally forced upon them in 1895 by the Afghani king Abdur Rahman Khan, by and large they are now enthusiastic and fervent Shiites. Those who did not convert were forced to disperse, probably most finding India more hospitable than the neighboring Islamic countries.

Isolated geographically and culturally, the Kafirs were an almost mythical people. Kafiristan was the inspiration for the Kipling's story "The Man Who Would be King." To the dominant Muslim tribes, the Kafirs were seen as devil worshipers, or at the very least as infidels. The region was renamed Nuristan, meaning the land of the enlightened suggesting the redemption of the people by their conversion. Nonetheless, even today to call someone in Pakistan or Afghanistan a Kafir is an insult.

This dagger has some elements that are reminiscent of the Hindu chillanum, being of solid steel construction. However, while there is an elegant curvilinear style to a chillanum, this dagger is functional an in a way minimalistic. The name "Jamdhar" reportedly comes from the Sanskrit words "Yama Daunstra" meaning tooth of Yama, the God of death. Katari is probably just an add-on intended to imply a sort of dagger.

This is a dagger intended for use more than for display and it is stylistically unique from other Persian, Arab, or Hindu weapons. It is rather unadorned, the steel having no apparent finish or even patina although it is clearly old. If it was polished, at least the steel does not show signs of abrasion.
