Circa 1700

Elephants were a very special part of ancient Indian culture, with significance that persists to the present day. As the Vedic people spread through India, their religion was blended with the ancient animistic faiths of the earlier tribal people and thus the Hindu faith evolved. Tribal gods often survived as avatars of Vishnu, Shiva, or other Vedic divinities. One of the most important avatars originating from tribal faiths was Ganesh, the elephant-headed god. Ganesh was important because the elephant itself was so important, both a symbol and basis for the power of Indian rulers.

The use of domesticated elephants as beasts of burden and for war was not unique to India. The army of Carthage led by Hannibal, famously brought war elephants from Africa, through Spain, across the Alps, and into Italy. However, the use of elephants in war persisted longer and was more common in India than elsewhere. Elephants were also used for conveyance of the rich or powerful. Installed on the elephant's back was a platform for the passengers, be they warriors or maharajas, called a howdah. Fanciful representations of howdahs in European art took on the appearance and magnitude of castles. In reality, two to four passengers would be the normal load for anything but a very largest war elephants. The passengers in the howdah sat too high up and far back on the elephant to control the beast; that was the job of the "mahout" who sat on the elephant's neck, just behind the ears. To be a mahout was a life's occupation. A boy in training to be a mahout would be assigned a young elephant and the two remained bonded throughout their lives. Of course, this special relationship was no guarantee that the elephant would automatically understand what the mahout wanted, or that it would be agreeable with those wishes. A mahout therefore had to have a means to commutate with the elephant, or prod it to do as was wished. The tool for this was the ankus, with hook and spear points on a handle. The mahout could use the ankus to jab into the elephant's various tender bits around the mouth and inner ear, which he could reach from his riding position. The ankus became one of the Hindu symbols of royal authority. Ganesh was often represented holding an ankus.

This is an Ankus, late 17th or early 18th century. It is a small size but of a very good quality. Its total length is 14.5 inches. The top steel blade is 4 inches with thickened tip and the hook shaped blade is also 4 inches, both are perforated along the edges. The handle is mounted with ivory grips riveted to the handle tang. The size might suggest that it was intended for a young mahout with a relatively young beast.
