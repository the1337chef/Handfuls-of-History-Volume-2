Circa 1785

The shamshir is a sort of saber or scimitar most often associated with Persia, but is common throughout the Islamic world. It was also the inspiration for European and American military sabers such as the Marine Corps Mamluk swords. The quintessential shamshirs of legend were those used by the Muslims during the crusades. Many were produced in Damascus and made of watered steel. From these swords came the misconceptions of "Damascus steel," which in the West was only counterfeited by pattern welding until recent years. The actual origins or the watered steel were the crucible forges of India where true Damascus steel (wootz) was produced. The secrets of wootz production lived on in India and wootz was used for making high quality weapons produced domestically (e.g. see mogul khanjar, tiger tooth dagger, soussan pata) and also exported to various Arab countries, as it was during the crusades (e.g. see Kurdish jambiya, khyber sword).

This shamshir was most likely produced for a well-off subject of the Indian Mogul Empire. The wootz blade is in the classic pattern of Persian style but the hilt is of a distinctly Indian pattern, as seen also on the soussan pata and tulwar. The hilt is decorated with silver koftgari inlay. The blade shows both its age and signs of use but it is a very solid and satisfying sword to wield.
