Sri Lanka (Ceylon) is an island nation whose history and culture reflect both its proximity to, and separation from, mainland India. Sri Lanka was off the path of Islamic conquests but, like India, Ceylon was in the path European commercial and colonial expansion. In 1803 Ceylon became part of the British colonial Empire and gained independence only after World War II.

The Sinhalese are the majority people on of Sri Lanka. Their language is of the Indo-Aryan group, and the Sinhalese most likely originated in Bengal region of India. The legendary founder of the first Sinhalese kingdom was Prince Vijaya (Sinhga) who was exiled from his homeland in India along with his 700 followers in around 500 BCE. His arrival on the island was said to have occurred on the date of the death of the Buddha. Hindu mythology shades much of the early legendary history of the Sinhalese, but the auspicious association with the passing of the Buddha was a foreshadowing of the conversion of the Sinhalese people to Buddhism about 300 years later (or a post-hoc revision of the legend).

The Emperor Ashoka, of the Mauryan Empire on mainland India, was active at promoting the spread of the teaching and practice of Buddhism. Ashoka's son, Mahinda, traveled to Sri Lanka where he met and converted the Sinhalese King Devanampiyatissa. This set the Sinhalese people on their own path, distinct from that of most peoples on the mainland. The Sinhalese, who represent approximately 75 percent of the population on Sri Lanka, are still predominantly Buddhist.

The second major ethnic group on Sri Lanka are the Tamils, believed to be related to Tamils of mainland India, but having a distinct history on Sri Lanka going back almost as far as the Sinhalese. The Tamils are Hindu and mostly located in the northern part of the island and along the eastern coast. A Tamil nationalist movement began after Sri Lanka gained independence from Britain. This led to a series of conflicts with the Sinhalese when the Tamil failed to be granted a separate national state (Tamil Eelam) on the island. The country was subsequently racked with civil war and terrorist activity for a period of over 25 years, finally ending with the defeat of the Rebel Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam in 2009.

The piha kahetta is the traditional knife of the Sinhalese people. The kastane is their symbolic national sword. They are typically very attractive and ornate and of a size and form that make them practical to use. This example has an eight-inch blade inlaid with engraved brass side plates and decorative bolsters. The grips are carved from ivory and the scabbard is carved wood with an embossed silver collar. Traditional pieces like this are hard to date with any real confidence. I am guessing that it is from at least as far back as the early 19th century, but it could be older.
