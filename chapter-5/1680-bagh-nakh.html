Bagh Nakh

Circa 1680

When the Marathas leader Sivaji used a bichawa to murder Afzal Khan, the commander of the armies of the Islamic Sultanate of Bijapur, he reportedly had a bagh nakh concealed in his left hand to assist in his treacherous attack. The bagh nakh, (tiger claws, sometimes spelled wagh nakh), is quite literally an underhanded weapon, as it is concealed in a closed fist and would deliver painful deep slashes if used to strike with an open palm. They were never considered an honorable weapon and so are quite rare. Claws of a similar sort were sometimes combined onto the grip of bladed or even multibladed weapon, constituting a bichawa bagh nakh. There may have been some variants where the blade protruded through the finger, or were designed to be worn across the back of the hand, but the most common form, like this example, is only practical to attach inside the palm.

The bagh nakh is most commonly associated with the Marathas of India, but usually with thieves or assassins rather than with generals. There is a similar tiger claw weapon sometimes associated with Japanese ninjas. During the Mughal era, a particularly bloody form of exhibition wrestling was sometime put on with the fighters each wearing bagh nakh and ripping each other apart for the entertainment of the spectators.
