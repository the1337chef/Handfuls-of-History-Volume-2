Circa 1770

Firangi is the term applied in India to swords that carried European style blades set with an Indian style hilt, similar to those used for the Indian khanda type swords. The term may have come from the Arabic term for the Franks who fought during the era of the crusades and in India came to mean simply "foreigner." The blades are usually 34 to 38 inches long, single edged and suitable for cut or thrust attacks. Unlike a khanda they are usually thought of as primarily cavalry weapons. The basket hilt provided good grip and hand protection. The curved spike at the back of the grip allows for the use of the second hand if a more forceful blow is desired, although this might have been impractical on horse back.

The quest for trade with India was a European dream for centuries, probably since the fall of the Roman Empire, and was revitalized by people like Marco Polo and his family. The dream became a reality after the Portuguese established a sea route to India in 1498. Other European nations soon followed, looking for spices and raw materials in exchange for European manufactured goods. Sword blades might have come both in the hands of the European soldiers, as well as in the holds of the merchant ships.

India was a land of many large and small princely states and sultanates, and by the 18th century the eminence of the Mughal Empire was fading. The Europeans, especially Britain, in the form of the East India Company, took advantage of the situation by playing one state against another and always getting something in the bargain. European swords would certainly have become common objects and it is interesting that while some Europeans came to adopt shamshir-like mamluk swords, some of the Indians also adopted firangi swords.

Many Indian firangi swords have blades that were made in Europe, either imported or scavenged from captured weapons. These blades often have maker's marks attesting to their history. Firangi swords also usually have fullers, as is typical on European sabers. However, the style of sword became sufficiently popular that blades were also made locally in Indian in the European style. This sword came from Oriental Arms and the vendor thought it was likely that the blade was European made. However, with no markings or fullers I suspect that it was made in India. The hilt is of very classical Indian style, fully inlaid with silver decoration and koftigari of floral design.

The firangi sword is most often associated with the Marathas, who were renown for their cavalry, but they also became popular with the Mughals and various Rajputs, making it difficult to ascribe this sword to a particular people. It most likely dates to the late 18th century.
