Circa 1856

The katar, or push dagger, is uniquely associated with India and likely originated in Tamil Nadu in the early middle ages. Its original name in Tamil was kattari. It is unusual in that the force delivered is not from a ballistic (swinging) motion of the arm, but rather a thrusting motion, as if striking with a closed fist. There are numerous variations and other weapons derived from the katar, including the hooded form which has a hand guard, ones which have spring-actuated triple blades, and the pata gauntlet sword. Sometimes broken sword blades (in many cases of European origin) were adapted (riveted) to katar hilts, but more authentically the blades were forged in one piece with the long bars that constitute the outside of the handle.

Some katars were quite ornate, with gold inlays or other decorations, but many are relatively plain, as in the case of this example. While simple in appearance, the quality of the steel and construction of the weapon is excellent. It is likely that the steel is wootz, although the watered pattern is not evident under the patina. There is faint engraving, which might once have had the light gold inlay known as koftgari. The blade has a thickened tip which strengthens it and hypothetically made it strong enough to punch through mail or other armor without breaking. Not unusually for an Indian weapon, the grip seems as if it were made for someone with a smaller hand than mine.
