Circa 1780

The chillanum is a rather rare form of dagger in the Indian arsenal, but at the same time representative of the exotic styles favored by the Hindu peoples with similarities to the khanjarli and the jamdhar katari. The earliest reference in Western literature to the chillanum (sometimes chillanum) as a distinct style dates to 1570. The chillanum style of dagger was often associated with the Maharattas but they were use by others, including the Mughals. This particular piece probably comes from one of the Rajput states of Rajasthan.

One feature often associated with chillanum daggers is that they are forged from steel in one piece with the hilt and grip of the same casting as the blade, as on this dagger. This is sometimes the case with a jamdhar katari but never the case with a khanjarli. This has led me to wonder whether some pieces identified as chillanums might in fact be more correctly identified as khanjarli, especially pieces with a large lunette pommel.

On this example the blade is slender, 12 inches long, double edged, and recurving. To me it is a piece of remarkable beauty. It is inscribed with piercings through the steel. I suspect that the etched areas were once filled with gold koftgari. The blade is likely to be wootz but the present patina prevents the watered pattern from showing clearly.

This is one of my most favorite pieces from India, one for which I wish I could speak an incantation and make it tell me its story. There were many princely states in Rajasthan, such as Mewar, Jodhpur, and Bharatpur, to name but a few of the more than 20 which survived into the 20th century. These Rajput states were constantly at war with one another and with the Mughal Empire. By 1800, France and England entered the game as power brokers, playing one state against another for their own advantage. For members of the warrior caste there was no other honorable occupation than war.

This dagger seems to me something more likely to have been worn at court than carried to war. A khanda would have been the sword to carry to battle. Nonetheless, the intrigues at court were probably plentiful and this dagger would have been an elegant statement of defense or assassination.
