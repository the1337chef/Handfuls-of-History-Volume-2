Circa 1820

A bit of an anomaly for this book, this is a "left hand" piece of history, defense rather than attack. Unlike the Madu or a left-hand dagger, this shield makes no pretence of being a weapon. It is however, a testimony to the elegance and flamboyance of the Indian caste-born warrior. There have been times when soldiers of a western army were flamboyant. Even in the American Civil War, some units, in both the Northern and Southern armies, fought as Zouaves, with baggy trousers and colorful caps, in imitation of North African native soldiers. Our modern images of armies are of rank upon rank of soldiers in uniforms, if not always dull in color, anonymous in their sameness to one another. Eastern armies were not so.

There was seldom peace among all the small states in India or between the small sultanates and Rajputs and the larger Empires. The warrior castes were kept busy by war. War was their life, but not all that there was to their lifestyle. The more aesthetic aspects of their characters were also something they took with them to war, something which is evident in the diversity and beauty in many of the pieces in this chapter. This is also evident in the paintings on this hide shield. It is most probably from Rajasthan, early 19th century. It is entirely hand painted in black, dark green, red, and gold colors with various, largely floral, designs both on the outside and inside for the bearer of the shield to appreciate. It is about 16 inches in diameter and 3 inches in height, so the sort of shield that would have allowed free use of a right-hand weapon. There are four brass/ silver bosses that would have served to deflect attacks. Most of the wear and damage on this shield are due to aging of the perishable materials from which it is constructed, however there are dints on the bosses which might have come from use in the field.
