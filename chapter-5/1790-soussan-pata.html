Circa 1790

India has a complex and variegated history, a montage of many peoples, faiths and states. The native Hindu faith is a rich and complicated one, and India was also the birthplace of Buddhism. After the year 720, Islam was also brought to the subcontinent, primarily with a sword. Although there were from time to time large kingdoms or empires in India, like that of the Hindu Gupta (c. 320-550 CE), and Vijayanagar empires (1336-1646), and the Islamic Mughal Empire (1526-18570), India was also a mosaic of small kingdoms, frequently at war with one another. When India and Pakistan were at last consolidated into modern nations in 1949, they were assembled from 17 British provinces and 562 semiautonomous princely states.

Although we sometimes have an image of India today as a peaceful land of mystic contemplation, as noted above, historically it was a land of frequent warfare among the many large and small states. For the many gods of the Hindu faith, there were many rituals and sacred weapons, so there were many types of swords and daggers with unique and distinctive forms. This sword is probably from central or south India. It is usually referred to as a sousson pata or sometimes a sailaba. It can be distinguished from typical Indian tulwar swords by the downward curving blade. The shape of the blade is similar to a Turkish yatagan blade, but the tulwar-style handle identifies it as Indian. The shape therefore suggests a fusion of Indian and Islamic influences, and the piece is not as uniquely Indian as something like a katar dagger.

The sword has a 30-inch blade of good Indian crystalline wootz (true "Damascus") steel, with an 8-inch back edge and a 2-inch ricasso. The handle is plated with heavy silver and retains a few tracings of old gilding. It is a well-balanced sword, although it must have been a special art to be expert with the downward curving blade. My original incentive for purchasing this piece was for the blade, to help illustrate the story of true Damascus steel that originated in India. The modern American concept of Damascus steels as many folded layers of pattern-welded steel comes from the attempts of medieval European blacksmiths to counterfeit the appearance of Islamic swords made of Indian wootz that had been imported to the Arab nations at the time of the crusades.
