Circa 1740

In the early 13th century, the armies of Genghis Khan cut a great swath across Asia, the Middle East, and into Europe. After the death of the great Khan, the contraction of the Mongol forces back into Mongolia and China created a power vacuum that was eventually filled by three Islamic states collectively known as the "Gunpowder Empires," the Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal. The Mughal Empire at its greatest extent around 1700 covered most of the Indian subcontinent. It was founded in 1526 by a dynasty which claimed direct decent from Genghis Khan. The period of its decline corresponded with the rise of British power in India, although it was always at odds with the native Indian states of the Marathas, Rajputs, and Sikhs. The arts of the Mughal Empire blended influences of India and Persia with Islam. The culmination of the Mughal architecture was achieved during the reign of Shah Jahan, who constructed the Taj Mahal in 1632.

This is a Mughal-style khanjar (dagger) acquired from Oriental Arms, a respected dealer based in Israel. I actually picked this dagger out when I visited Artsi Yarom, owner of Oriental Arms at his home in Haifa. Artsi dates it as from the early- to mid-18th century, so near the peak of the Mughal era. As noted above, the Mughal Empire suffered a slow decline as the British East India Company played one faction in India against another, and by the early 1800s the Mughal ruler was mostly just a figurehead. When the last Mughal Emperor, Bahadur Shah II, voiced his support of the Indian Rebellion of 1857 that occurred as native troops mutinied against the British, he sealed the fate of the Mughal state. When the mutiny failed, the Emperor was captured and exiled. His sons were executed, and Queen Victoria added "Empress of India" to her titles.

The blade on the dagger is made of true "Damascus" steel, or more correctly wootz, a form of crucible steel invented in India and exported to the Near East where it was used with great skill by sword makers in Damascus. The handle is ivory and shows an old repair.
