Circa 1780

This dagger is a pesh-kabz, a member of a family of weapons that include the karud and the khyber knife, but with a re-curving blade. This style of dagger originated in Persia and spread throughout the Indo-Persian world. The name means "fore-grip" in Persian, which refers to how it was traditionally carried in Persia, in the front of the belt, rather that at the side.

Like the khyber knife, the blade has a pronounced "T" spine, reinforced edge, but with a thickened armor-piercing tip. The full tang construction and grip style is also similar to the khyber knife. The grips are of elephant ivory; the grip strap and bolsters are steel. The total length is 14.5 inches. The blade of this particular knife is forged from wootz (true Damascus) steel. However, the wootz pattern is imperfect, and not visible in several areas.

The recurved blade is common in Indian weapons, seen in examples such as the bichwa, khanjarli, chillanum, the dual blades of the haladie, and some khanjar. Perhaps the inspiration for these recurved blades comes from the shape of the antlers of Fakir horns and the madu. In contrast, knives like the Indian tiger tooth dagger and Islamic jambiyas, are curved but not curved twice. Of course, some weapons, like Indonesian kris, have wavy blades with many curves.

What advantage, if any, does a recurved blade give a knife? One possibility is that it creates more cutting surface on a shorter blade, and that might be valuable for knives used for cutting rather than thrusting. However, daggers like the pesh-kabz and bichwa are really intended for thrusting attacks, in which case, the purpose of the recurve might be to widen the cut as the blade is thrust deeper. This certainly could be the case with the pesh-kabz blade, which is first narrowed and reinforced to improve an initial penetration between links of mail.

Although the armor piecing function has lost its importance, the pesh-kabz remains a popular style of dagger in Afghanistan, Pakistan, and parts of western India, primarily among Islamic tribesmen.
