Circa 1860

The Ayda Katti is the traditional sword of the Coorg of Malabar, in the South West coastal area of India. The name "Coorg" is anglicized from the native name Kodagu (Kodava Nadu). The Coorg have a rich tradition of independence and military prowess. They are non-conventional Hindus, celebrating special festivals with the veneration of weapons. An Ayda Katti is prominent on the flag of the Coorg people, along with a pichangeti dagger and a rifle. Because of the use of weapons in their worship they are the only people in India who are exempt from the Indian Arms Act and may own guns without special permits. Most Coorg consider themselves of the Kshatriyas (warrior) caste and many generals in the Indian army have been of Coorg decent. However, Coorg society is not heavily stratified by the caste system, as is the case in other parts of India.

The Coorgs are a distinct people, probably of pre-Aryan tribal origin although their language is related to other languages of South India, Dravidian origin, a mixture of Kannada, Tamil, and Malayalam dialects. Their non-conventional form of Hinduism places a special importance on the veneration of ancestors, even above the usual gods of the Hindu pantheon. Their sacred river is the Cauvery rather than the Ganges. The Coorgs can also be identified by their distinct ceremonial dress, which for men, is typified by long black coats with short sleeves, and a pichangeti dagger is usually worn with this attire. The wearing of the sari by Coorg women is also different from other India peoples in how it is draped.

While the pichangeti is more of a fashion accessory than a weapon, the ayda katti is a very serious weapon indeed, although its origins may have once had an agricultural association. The very heavily weighted blade is sharp on the inside concave edge like the Nepalese kora and kukris. The broad flat pommel will allow it to be held firmly while delivering blows with great force. The ayda katti were traditionally carried without a scabbard, mounted through a ring, worn in a harness on the back. Unlike some Indian weapons, Ayda Katti swords were not made to be especially beautiful, and this example has just the usual sorts of modest decorations.
