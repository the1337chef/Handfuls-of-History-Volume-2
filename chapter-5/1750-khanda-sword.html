Circa 1750

The khanda was the sacred sword of the Rajput warriors who controlled the princely states of north central India for many centuries. They warred constantly amongst themselves and with Mughal and other earlier Islamic Empires of India, sometimes subjugated and sometimes independent. Many of the Rajput states were allied to the British during the mutiny of 1858 and remained at least nominally independent up until the consolidation of the modern Indian nation in 1947. In 1947 there were still 562 princely or native states of varying sizes within what were to become the modern nations of India and Pakistan.

From the seventh century onward, the majority of the Indian princely and native states were ruled by Rajput dynasties, others were ruled by Marathas, or native peoples like the Coorg (see pichangeti and ayda katti), who retained distinct cultural identities going back thousands of years. Of course, throughout the entire history of India, beginning with the poorly understood pre-Vedic era of the Indus civilization, there were conflicts between large Empires that rose and fell and small nations or kingdoms, which did their best to endure. This rich history accounts for the splendid legacy of diverse and elegant weapons sampled in this chapter.

The khanda was to the Rajputs what the basket-hilted broadsword was to the highland Scots, and in several ways, they are similar weapons, the khanda in fact being a basket-hilted broadsword. The khanda blade is double edged, but on typical examples, one edge is covered with decorative reinforcement for most of its length, making it effectively single edged. However, while the Scottish sword was a cut or thrust weapon, the khanda blade would have not been effective for stabbing attacks. Khanda blades often widen slightly, rather than taper, and they have rather blunt tips. As on this example, there is usually a spike off the back of the handgrip which might be used for a back-handed attack, or more commonly, to allow for the warrior to use a two-handed grip.

As is common with Indian weapons, there is a great deal of elegance in the design and details of this sword. The gold inlay is of the sort known as koftgari. The inlay is decorative, rather than inscriptional. The texture in the blade is that of pattern-welded steel, rather than wootz (true Damascus), and the blade shows signs of rough polishing in a few places. The velvet covering of the scabbard and lining of the hand guard are correct for this style of weapon but may not be as old as the blade itself. The sword was probably in use for a 150 years or more, and these fittings might have been restored at some point, but they do not appear to be strictly modern replacements.
