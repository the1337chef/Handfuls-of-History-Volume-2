Circa 1850

India has seen the rise and fall of many Empires, most recently of course, the British, and before that, the Mughal Empire. However, thoughout its history, prior its independence in 1947, India has never been a unified nation, even during the periods of domination by the Mughals or the English. At the time when Great Britain granted independence to the nations of India and Pakistan, more than 560 "Princely States" were recognized in the new unions. The hereditary rulers of these domains retained their titles and limited regional authority in the new nations. In India some of the old aristocrats continued to receive government stipends from the "privy purse" until 1971. Many still would like to lay claim to their family's titles; Maharaja Nizam, Raja, Raje, Rana, Rao, Rawat, Rawal, Thakur, Thakores, or Sultan. Along with this veneration of the ancient kingdoms of India, members of these noble families retain other traditions of custom and dress. A dagger was a common part of traditional stately garb throughout India. In the Sikh states, wearing a dagger is a requirement of the faith.

Numerous elegant types of daggers come from the many states of India, such as the jamdhar katari from Kafiristan in the north and the distinctive pieces like chillanum, khanjarli, and bichwas from the Rajaput and Maratha states. Tiger tooth daggers were popular in northern India and in the Sikh states, while katar type daggers were used throughout India. Each of these dagger types is of a unique and readily identifiable style. The term khanjar, originally from Arabic, came to be used more generically and has been applied to several different types of daggers depending on the geographic local. In Arabia it is applied to jambiya-like daggers, while in India, the term khanjar is often applied to daggers of Mughal Empire that have graceful recurved blades, similar to those of chillanum and khanjarli, but with an elegant, though less ornate, hilt sometimes referred to as a pistol grip style. Nonetheless, khanjar hilts are often of ivory or other precious material.

The engravings on the handle of this khanjar clearly place its origin in one of the Hindu states. As with many of the finest weapons from India, the blade is of wootz (true Damascus steel), though of a microcrystalline pattern, like that of the Chillanum, rather than the high contrast type on the Mughal kard and khanjar and the tiger tooth dagger. The green velvet-covered scabbard is newly made or recently restored. The blade is beautiful, though relatively simple, lacking the deep forged fullers present on the Mughal khanjar, the tiger tooth, and the khanjarli.

The really outstanding features on this khanjar are the engravings. While most of them are floral or geometric, there is a striking rendition of a Hindu deity on both sides of the handle. Although the Hindu faith recognizes a large pantheon of deities, a quantity amplified by their many avatars, most people of the Hindu faith have one, or at least a small selection of particular favorites. For many Hindus, Ganesh is the most revered god, while for others their patron may be the monkey god Hanuman. This dagger may be dedicated to Ma Kali, the goddess who is the destroyer of evil forces. Legend has it that Kali goes on a rampage killing all the evil in the three worlds earth, heaven, and underworld and couldn't be stopped until her consort Lord Shiva lies down in her path forcing her to stop as she literally steps on him. Traditionally, Kali has 10 arms, each armed with a weapon that she uses in specific circumstances. In the representations on this dagger only six arms are clearly visible. One hand holds a sword, another a serpent, a third perhaps a katar, two are crossed serenely, and it is hard to say what is in the sixth hand, perhaps a globe, a lotus flower, a chakra, or a human heart. In most representations of Kali there is a prostrate victim on his back at her feet, but this redition of her actually riding on Shiva (?) is unusual. Kali is often shown holding a freshly severed human head and wearing a necklace from many decapitations. Usually her tongue protrudes.

None of the fierce and bloody features typical of Kali are really evident in the representations on this dagger. So, considering those inconsistencies, this image might more likely be meant to represent Durga Maa, a mother goddess of creation rather than a destroyer. Kali is sometimes said to be an avatar of Durga. Durga is also usually shown having eight or ten arms, as well as three eyes, the third, being on the middle of the forehead, may not be visible in profile. Durga usually rides, or is accompanied by a lion. Although she also carries a variety of weapons, her countenance is far more benign than Kali's, her tongue does not stick out and she is usually smiling. Perhaps the figure being ridden by the deity on this dagger is an anthropomorphized lion, one can almost imaging whiskers.

It seems likely that this dagger was made with a specific person in mind, especially during the final stages of the engraving, since Hindu worship is often so individualized. It is even possible that the person beneath the deity is meant to represent the intended owner of the dagger, in which case it could symbolize his submission to the goddess.
