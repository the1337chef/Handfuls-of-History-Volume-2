Hooded Katar

Circa 1795

The katar (kattar) is certainly one of the most iconic of all Indian weapons, originating in South India and by the 18th century common to all of the Hindu states. The typical katar has a relatively short triangular blade, intended to deliver a strong blow when held in the closed fist. Blades were often thickened to be potentially armor piercing. The katar is usually strictly a thrusting weapon, although some are found with longer blades adapted from broken swords, or in rare cases, curved blades that would be suitable for slashing attacks. Although they do not offer much reach, I believe they were often relied on as primary (right-hand) weapons. The ordinary katar would have limited use as a left-hand dagger to parry attacks from a sword-wielding opponent for a couple of reasons. Firstly, they have no cross guards or other projections to deflect an attack, and secondly, although the bars of the handle offer some protection to the sides of the arm, the hand and other arm surfaces are exposed. One form of the katar that could be argued was better suited as a parrying weapon had a mechanism that opened the single blade into three, creating clefts between the blades that could snag an opponent's weapon. Similar devices were sometimes built into European left-hand daggers. Hooded katars, like this example, provide almost a basket to offer near-complete hand protection. The quillion-like finial at the back of the hood would also have a deflecting function. These features might also have made it suitable as a left-hand weapon.

The hooded katar seems to anticipate the pata gauntlet sword which became popular with the Maratha warriors. Except that the pata blades tend to be longer and more flexible than those of a hooded katar, which though usually longer than a typical katar blade, are still relatively wide and strongly reinforced with fullers.

The chiseled designs on the hood and langets of this example are beautifully executed floral patterns with no animal or human elements. The blade is wide and heavy with somewhat unusual wavy edges. The steel is a laminated form of simulated Damascus. The weapon is both beautiful and formidable, especially if it were paired with a long sword like a khanda or a soussan pata. However, as is often the case with Indian weapons, it was made for someone with a smaller hand than mine and I had a friend pose for the picture of the weapon in hand.
