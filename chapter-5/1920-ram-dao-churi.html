Animal sacrifice was a traditional part of Hindu worship, but is less common today than it was in earlier centuries. In ancient Hindu practice the installation of a new king often involved the sacrifice of a horse, a ritual so magnificent as to inspire epic poems. Festivals with large-scale animal sacrifice have been banned in India, but still occur in Nepal. Most notable is the three-day Gadhimai festival, held every five years, which attracts several million visitors from India. Hundreds of thousands of animals, including cows and goats, are sacrificed at the Gadhimai festival to placate the goddess Gadhimai. Special weapons are used for these rituals, which are designed to dispatch the offered animals with a single blow. One such weapon is the Nepalese kora, which historically was also used in battle. Oversized kukri are also commonly used. While the kora and kukri are weapons that are also used for animal sacrifice, the ram dao was a special sword made exclusively for this ritual purpose with particular significance to its shape and design, especially in regards to the god's seeing eye engraved onto the blade. The beast-like engraving also has spiritual significance. There is also a cartouche with an "S" within a diamond, which is probably a maker's mark

This piece was advertised as a ram dao, dao being just the Chinese word for sword. However, a true ram dao would be larger and of sufficient mass to deal the death blow to an ox or other comparably sized beast. This smaller type of blade, which shares the same ritual design elements as the ram dao, is more correctly called a churi or kartri*. It might be used to dispatch a goat or sheep. As with a kora or kukri, the blade is curved with the sharpened edge on the inner side to catch the animal's neck on a single downward blow.


* D. LaRocca "The Gods Of War: Sacred Imagery and the Decoration" Metropolitan Museum of Art Press.
