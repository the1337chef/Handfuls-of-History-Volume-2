Circa 1870

The Garo people live in parts of Meghalaya, Nagaland and Assam. They call themselves A·chik Mande ("hill people"). Although the men dominate the society, property ownership and inheritance is through the maternal line. As is common with many tribal cultures, Christian missionaries have converted many of the Garo, with only isolated rural pockets still following the traditional animist religion known as Songsarek. It is believed that the Garo originated in Tibet and only migrated to their present locales around the year 400 CE, and indeed an analysis of their language supports a connection to Tibet. They had very little contact with Europeans prior to 1800, and as with the Naga, they were viewed as bloodthirsty savages. It is certain that like the neighboring Naga, they had a cultural history as headhunters. In December 1872, the British sent forces from India to "pacify" the Garo. The Garo warriors, equipped only with swords and similar primitive weapons, were heavily outmatched and largely slaughtered. Togan Nengminja, the leader of the Garo warriors, fell along with many of his comrades.

For those Garo who still live in traditional villages, life is not very much changed from what it was a hundred years ago. They practice slash and burn agriculture, which they supplement with what the forest provides. They still build homes in traditional styles with forest materials, although many of their everyday garments are western style shorts and t-shirts complete with logos and slogans in English.

This type of two-handed sword, known as a mil-am, was the principle weapon of the Garo warrior. It is wrought of a single piece of steel, with a straight tang and spatulate pommel. The heavy blade is obliquely angled at the forte, then straight and double-edged, with a cusped tip. The guard is composed of a steel rod passed through a punched aperture in the tang. There probably were tufts of hair attached to the ends of the guard, but as is usually the case, these were lost over time. The overall length is 31 inches, which is somewhat short for a two-handed weapon. In their simple construction, but complex shape, they resemble some of the "throwing knives" of Africa. As with the African weapons, the shape is complex but very stereotyped, so that one mil-am is very much like another, with each element in the shape having perhaps some spiritual or ritual significance.
