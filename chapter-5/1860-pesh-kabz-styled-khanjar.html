Circa 1860

This dagger was advertised by Oriental Arms as a "Pesh-Kabz Style Dagger" and while there is some resemblance in the shape of the grip and the recurved blade, in other key features it bears no resemblance to a true pesh-kabz. A proper pesh-kabz would have a grip with a full tang and two scales riveted to either side, as well as a blade reinforced with at T-ridge along the back, like a Khyber knife or Afghan choora. The connection to a pesh-kabz is therefore rather weak, and it is probably better to just consider it a khanjar (dagger).

The blade is 7.5 inches long, forged from wootz (true Damascus) steel. The handle is steel, richly decorated with gold koftgari work. The dagger is almost certainly Indian, and Artzi suggested probably from Rajasthan, the region of old Hindu states in the northwest part of the sub-continent. While this is possible, the lack of Hindu iconography or figurative elements would lead me to think it is more likely from a Sultanate, or a remnant of the Mughal Empire, rather than from a Princely Rajput state. The elements of style suggestive of the pesh-kabz, would also to me be consistent with a relationship to the Islamic states in the west of India.

If, as I suspect, it dates from the early period of the British Raj, after the fall of the Mughul Empire, the failure of the Indian mutiny, and the dissolution of the British East India Company, then this might have simply been the courtly dagger of a lesser Indian noble. It would have been of little importance that it was not suitable to pierce chain mail like a true pesh-kabz.
