Fakir Horns

Circa 1950

Fakirs are ascetics who might at first glance appear more like street beggars than holy men. Most are Muslim Sufi but the name is also applied to Hindu ascetics. Traditionally, Fakirs were forbidden to use metal weapons, so when they found reason to defend themselves they used weapons, such as daggers made of animal horn. One such weapon, made from paired horns of black buck deer, came to be known as Fakir horns. Fakirs were also known to sometimes have daggers made just of carved horn. Such horn weapons might have served their purpose in time of need, but of course would have been inferior to metal weapons. Even ascetics had to be pragmatic from time-to-time, so some "horns" came to be fashioned with metal tips. In this form, combined with a small shield, they became useful as parry weapons known as madu, a weapon favored by Maratha warriors.

These particular horns are likely to be relatively modern. They have brass tips, which are more fancy and less weapon-like than steel tips. Some aspects of these look old but the bolts that join the two horns look relatively new, so perhaps they were restored or reconstructed. These are also interesting in that they have removable (albeit, at this time somewhat ill-fitting) caps on the ends of the horns. These provide places to stash items inside, a feature which could be a plus, especially for someone living on the streets.

It would be interesting to know if today's fakirs still carry such items at all, or if indeed there are many such old school mystics still on the streets in India. No doubt shops in India will continue to sell items like these as long as there is a market for them. These may be an old item that was rebuilt for sale to a modern tourist (or U.S. soldier). I purchased them rather cheaply at a gun show, and such shows are a common outlet for the souvenirs of American veterans.
