Three-Bladed Katar

Circa 1890

The katar is probably one of the most iconic, and uniquely Indian, of weapons from the sub-continent. They are primarily intended for thrusting attacks and, except for hooded katars, are not suited for parrying attacks, since much of the fist is exposed. The katar is believed to have originated in the Vijayanagara Empire of South India, probably during the 14th century.

This form of the katar, sometimes known as a scissors katar, would not have had much utility as an offensive weapon, but might conceivably be used as a sort of left-hand dagger, or sword catcher. European parrying daggers that had a similar ability to split into three blades probably inspired the design. One could imagine the "disarming" effect it might have had to see a weapon transform, and indeed it is possible that these katars were intended to appeal the imagination of a European collector more than to an Indian warrior. Most scissor katars date to the colonial period.

It takes a certain effort to hold this by the lower cross bar and push against the inner one with your knuckles to keep the enveloping outer blades in place so they can function as one. The outer blades are sharpened, but their thick edges make them seem less than lethal. The inner blade is sharper than the outer ones, but it does not extend beyond them, so that ordinarily it would not even make effective contact with an opponent. It seems the only thing this weapon might do is serve to catch and deflect another sword.

The mechanism for expanding the outer blades is engaged when the inner bar is pulled in, and if held tight enough, the three blades are relatively well fixed in position. However, especially with the age of this piece, they are not so rigid as would be desired. Indeed this piece has not aged very well. Not only is there a bit of looseness in the mechanism, but also most of the decorations have faded. When new, it was doubtless rather attractive, with what was probably kofgiri inlays on most of the surfaces.
