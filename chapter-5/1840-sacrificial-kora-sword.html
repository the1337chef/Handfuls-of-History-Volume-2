Circa 1840

The kora is a traditional sword of the Ghurka in Nepal. Prior to their association, and sometimes collaboration, with the British, the Ghurka would have used similar swords in battle. However, by the 18th century these swords were replaced as the Ghurkas's main battle weapons were British style muskets and rifles. These were either old models surplused from the British or copies of lesser quality made in Nepal. Of course, even to the present day, the Ghurka, who still fight as mercenaries for the British, will carry their traditional broad bladed kukri knives but the not koras. Koras are however, still used occasionally for their other traditional purpose, ritual animal sacrifice. Animal sacrifice is still an important part of the religious practice for some Hindu sects of India and Nepal. In India the special ritual swords for this purpose are the ram dao for the larger animals, and somewhat smaller churi, that might be used on goats or similar sized animals. In Nepal, koras and oversized kukris are used for such purposes.

Many Hindus in India believe that animal sacrifice is forbidden now in the present era, the Kali Yuga (age of Kali), and the Indian government takes the position of discouraging the practice, especially when conducted on a large scale. The most notorious event involving animal sacrifice on a massive scale is the three-day-long Gadhimai Festival in Nepal, which is held every five years. As many as 250,000 animals, including water buffaloes, pigs, goats, and various smaller animals, were sacrificed at this event in 2009. It was attended by five million people, approximately 80 percent of whom made the journey from India, where such a festival would be banned. The festival is believed to placate Gadhimai, the goddess of power. In 2014, the Indian government blockaded the border with Nepal to try to prevent the import of livestock for the festival.

Animal sacrifice is practiced on a small scale through all of India for the worship of goddesses such as Bhavani or Kali. It is particularly common in the Southern Indian states of Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, and Tamil Nadu. The most common and approved form of slaughter is Jhatka, decapitation with a single blow. Pious Hindus who eat meat will require that the animals be killed with the Jhatka method.

Older Koras, which might have been used in battle, were somewhat lighter and less broad than this piece. This piece, with the sharp inner edge and flat hooked end, was surely designed to land on an animal's neck and not slip off, improving the chances of a proper blow. The small floret on the blade is likely symbolic of the god's eye to look upon the sacrifice. Ram dao and churi swords that were dedicated to use for ritual sacrifice typically have elaborately engraved eye symbols.
