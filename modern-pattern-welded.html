As described in the essay by Dr. Bloom, another approach for creating steel with the appearance of true Damascus steel (wootz) is to forge metals of different qualities, often folding them into many layers so that they can be etched and show a pattern when finished. These are examples of modern pattern-welded “Damascus” knives. Dr Bloom made the first four knives.

The first two pictures are of the “star blade.” When Steve made the billet of steel for this blade he knew that the blade formed out of the center would have a repeating pattern of stars. I especially requested a knife made from that center piece. The grip is nicely decorated with silver castings from Japanese menuki in the form of spiny lobsters.

To make the second knife, Steve made a sort of jellyroll of steel and nickel. He then cut off slices from the roll and welded them onto the blade of a knife.

The third knife, one that gets a lot of service in our kitchen, is random-pattern steel made with 512 folded layers.

On the fourth knife, Steve folded the steel in such a way as to get a sort of chevron pattern. This knife also has a decorative “mokume” (Japanese, mokume-gane) patterned guard and pommel. In this case, the metal laminate was made from a billet formed from U.S. quarters. The composition of the coins was a sandwich of a cupronickel alloy with a copper core. Although they appear silver colored, more than 90% of the modern U.S. quarter is copper.

The fifth knife was made and marked by a smith named Peterson. It is also basically a random pattern. Reportedly one of the steels was from a meteorite, one of the oldest sources available for steel blades.

The last knife in this small selection is a little French-made kitchen knife. Pattern-welded knives are often handcrafted items like those made by Dr. Bloom and Mr. Peterson, but sometimes the methods are adapted to production pieces as well. I believe the laminations on this blade are essentially just decorative, especially as they run perpendicular to the cutting edge.
