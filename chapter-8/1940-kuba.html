Circa 1940

During the 17th century, the Kuba Kingdom arose as a conglomerate of several smaller Bushongo-speaking principalities in the center of what is now the Democratic Republic of the Congo. The kingdom flourished until the colonial era and still exists today as a princely state within the Republic. The kingdom includes 19 different ethnic groups. They recognize one high king as a hereditary "divine right" ruler and lesser sub-kings in the village communities. The current reigning monarch, Kot-a-Mbweeky III, has been on the throne since 1969. Kuba kings have been known to have as many as 800 wives.

This style of ceremonial knife was introduced by an early Kuba king, replacing traditional throwing knives, as a symbol of status and authority. It is believed that the first examples of these knives were obtained by the Kuba as trade items from Portuguese or Dutch merchants. Subsequently, Congolese blacksmiths learned to forge them locally. Most examples have had the steel blackened to prevent rust, as on this dagger. Like the Mangbetu Trumbash axe and the Yakoma short sword, these daggers appear poorly designed for use as weapons, consistent with their strictly ceremonial significance.
