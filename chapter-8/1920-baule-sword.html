Circa 1920

The Baule (Baoulé) people have an interesting history, at least if the legends are to be believed. They were an established nation as far back as the 14th century but at that time they lived in what is now Senegal and were dominated by the Wolof Empire. In the 17th century, the Baule fled the control of their current overlords the Ashanti and traveled west to what is now Côte d'Ivoire. They were led by Queen Pokou and were pursued by armies of the Ashanti. In desperation, when they reached the Komoe river they began discarding even their most valued possessions. It was believed that these sacrifices were required for their salvation. Legend has it that the Queen felt she had to surrender what was most precious to her and threw her son to his death in the river. After her sacrifice the hippopotami rose out of the river and helped them cross. The people took a new name from the laments of the queen, "baouli," which means "the child is dead."

The Baule resisted repeated attempts of Europeans to control their kingdom until finally in middle of the 19th century they were overpowered by the French and came under their colonial rule. The Baule constitute the largest ethnic minority in Côte d'Ivoire and account for 23 percent of the total population.

The Baule are agriculturists; yams and maize are the staples, with yams being the most important. Fish and game supplement these crops. Coffee, cocoa and kola nuts are also raised as cash crops.

Several styles of weapons have been associated with the Baule. Some are hook shaped, others have broad hour-glass shaped blades like the kuba sword. Ones like this example, with bar-shaped blades are more unusual. These are often decorated with large shells of the spondylus-family. On this example, the squared tipped blade is slightly curved, of a shallow diamond cross-section and 18 inches long. The handle is covered with leather and with a characteristic crown shaped pommel. The scabbard is wood, covered with hide and animal fur. The total length is 23.5 inches.

Today about 35 percent of the Baule are converted to Islam and about 55 percent are nominally Christian. However, about 11 percent of Baule the still retain their ethnic belief in three worlds, one of the gods, one of humans, animals, and other earthly things, and a third "beyond" which is where the spirits of the ancestors reside.
