Circa 1932

The Tiv are a large native group living mainly in Nigeria. They are primarily farmers, not generally known as warriors or for their weapons. Their first contact with Europeans was not until 1907. It is believed that they migrated to their present location in the Benue Valley from the Congo around 1800. Their language is related to Bantu. The traditional Tiv society was very egalitarian with little social hierarchy or competition among communities. They did not generally evoke the concept of private ownership of land, as it was generally believed that there was land enough for every family to farm. The most favored crop of the Tiv is yams.

Groups of Tiv communities, each led by a chosen elder, decided the issues that affected the communities through a democratic council. There was no single ruler for the groups until in 1946 when the British colonial administration required them to select a king, so that the British could deal with a single central ruler. The "kingship" of the Tiv has never become hereditary.

The Tiv are famous for storytelling and pleasant community life, even in difficult times. Laura Bohannan (writing as Elenore Smith Bowen) described life among the Tiv in her autobiographical novel, Return to Laughter. She begins her story as an aloof anthropologist, but eventually warms to the people as they cease to see her as a stranger.

This style of loop dagger is, I believe, the only distinctive weapon associated with the Tiv. An archer would have used it while hunting, since it could be kept on the hand while drawing a bow. Presumably, following a successful shot, it could be used to dispatch the prey or dress the carcass. While the Indian bichwa loop dagger is of similar design, the bichwa was considered as an assassin's weapon for covert attacks. The blades of Tiv loop daggers are typically spade or leaf shaped, although not usually as round as this example. In contrast, bichwa blades are thinner, recurved, often double, and altogether more sinister in appearance.
