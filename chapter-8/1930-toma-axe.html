Circa 1930

The Toma (also Loma and/or Buzi) are one of the Mande people, related to the Mandinka. They live in Guinea and Sierra Leone. They speak a unique language and still largely follow their traditional religious practices, of which relatively little is known. The total population of the tribe is approximately 225,000. The only weapons commonly associated with them are axes, and even those are rare. Artzi at Oriental Arms has only come upon three, which all came to him as a single lot. He posted two of them for sale. They were unusual but appeared frail and strictly ceremonial. One, in fact, had a brass rather than steel blade. When I inquired, he told me about this one which he had not yet listed for sale. It was the nicest of the three and the only one that might have actually been used effectively as a weapon.

One African specialty store on eBay has Toma masks for sale*. They have this to say about the tribe:

"The Toma, who number about 200,000, occupy high-altitude rain forests in widely-scattered villages in Guinea, Liberia, and Sierra Leone.

Like many cultures living in the area, their political and religious life is regulated by the men's "poro" society. This society is responsible for the initiation of young boys, among other things. These initiations take place in the forest, and masks known as "landai" are used to summon the young men to the forest for their month-long journey into manhood. These masks were also used to conclude the ordeal, symbolically "eating" the boys, leaving them as men, new members of adult society.

The Toma are known almost exclusively for abstract "landai" masks, these masks are characterized by a rather blank face, with round holes for eyes, and a dramatic horizontal brow. There is usually a simple, vertical nose and, frequently, no mouth. There are a few staffs and canes known, usually identified by the use of finials which feature a "landai" mask-like face.

A careful search of the literature yielded no useful information on Toma history."

I can concur that there is very little information available about the Toma. The websites supported by Christian missionaries indicate that although they have been "reached," they resist the best efforts of the missionaries and the teachers of Islam.
