Tubu Dagger with Cowries

Circa 1900

This is a dagger from the Tebu (Teda) people. The Tebu are of largely Arabic decent living mainly in Chad but also in Niger and Libya. They are primarily nomadic herdsman and generally distain the manual labor of agriculture. Through the 19th century they were known to raid the caravan routes and trade in slaves.

Tebu customs are based on Islamic law but with a particular bias toward violence and retribution. This culture of violence still impacts the contemporary life in Chad.

The blade markings are typical Tebu. The grip of this dagger is covered with material that is most likely cured gut. The scabbard is covered with lizard or snakeskin. The cowry shells are decorative but also are reminiscent of the times when such shells were used as currency or barter. When the first Europeans to explore the interior of Africa traveled up the Niger they carried chests of these shells. Originally, there were probably many more of these shells on the sheath of this dagger. The old lacing is falling apart and in just the short time I have owned this dagger, several of the cowry shells have come loose.
