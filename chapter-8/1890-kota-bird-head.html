Kota Bird Head Throwing Knife

Circa 1890

Items identified as "throwing knives" are a special category of African artifacts. Some, like the examples attributed the Zande, Matakam, and Mbanja tribes, are of complex irregular shape, perhaps with the idea that the more edges there are the better chance you will hit something if you do indeed throw it. This particular style of throwing knife has a large flat "blade," stylized in the shape of a bird's head with a triangular eye cut into the blade, to further suggest the image of a bird's head. Early forms of this style knife, associated with the Fang people, had shorter straight handles and could be considered true throwing knives. This example, with a similar head design but with a heavy wood handle bound with brass wires, is a style associated with the Kota people, neighbors of the Fang from whom they likely adopted this style of knife. It is likely that this knife/axe was intended more as a ceremonial item, or as an indicator of status, more than as an actual weapon. A good deal of effort was put into crafting this item, especially in the handle wrapping. Items of this quality were sometimes used as currency to pay tribute or toward bride prices.

The Kota (Bakota) people are a Bantu ethnic group living in the northeastern region of Gabon. Their current population is estimated at 75,000. It is believed that they migrated to their present homeland a few hundred years ago, possibly from the southern Sudan. They live in rainforest and are slash and burn agriculturalists. The Kota are noted for having produced copper and brass reliquary guardian figures. These were key to their native religion of ancestor worship known as Bwete.
