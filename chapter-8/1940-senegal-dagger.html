Senegal Dagger

Circa 1940

This dagger comes from West Africa. A similar dagger in the British Museum is described as coming from the Bissagos (Bijagos) Islands, a group of islands off the coast of Guinea, Bissau, but the attribution to these islands is questionable. The Bissagos islands are quite sparsely populated and have been dominated by the Portuguese for several centuries. It is unlikely that the indigenous peoples had the metallurgical skills locally to produce the brass work of this dagger and scabbard, let alone the steel. I showed the piece a friend who has spent many years in various African nations. He suggested Morocco or some other North African origin. Perhaps the British Museum acquired their daggers in the Bissagos islands but they originated elsewhere. Other sources identify the dagger as from Senegal, including an annotation to the picture from the British Museum.

The curved blade is 7.5 inches long, of flat diamond cross section with a 1-inch ricasso of decorative brass work. The handle is of black horn and bone and the scabbard of etched brass and copper. The total length is 12.5 inches. The decorative "ears" above the blade are a defining feature on this type of dagger, but they are too abstract to say for certain what they are meant to represent (if anything). They are sometimes referred to as elephants, but they look almost more lizard-like to me.

Although a relatively small country, there are numerous ethnic groups in Senegal and several languages are widely spoken. The Wolof are the largest single ethnic group, followed by the Fula, Toucouleur, and Serer with lesser numbers of Jola, Mandinka, Maures, Soninke, and Bassari. I have not been able to pin down this dagger type to any specific group. Similar daggers are sometime attributed to the Tuareg, but I believe this is incorrect.
