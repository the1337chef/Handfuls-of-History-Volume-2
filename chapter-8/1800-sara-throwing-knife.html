Circa 1800

The throwing knives used by the Sara are of the oldest and most simple form, flattened iron in the form of the letter F. The Sara warrior might carry a bundle three or four of these, with the lower arm of the F resting on his shoulder.

The Sara are one of the Kirdi (non-Muslim) peoples of Chad and the Central African Republic. They are primarily agriculturalists and, in recent history, cotton has become one of their main crops. Their society has a rigid hierarchy favoring the male elders. In the traditional Sara society, younger males must endure a difficult and sometimes painful period of initiation known as the yoindo. In recent decades there has been increased conversion of the Sara to Christianity, which has spared some of the men from the yoindo.

Scarification was a common practice among the Sara and women increased their bride value by forcing their flesh to accept large plates in both upper and lower lips. The lower lip plates were sometimes eight inches or more in diameter. It has been claimed that the Sara originated in the Nile valley and the name refers to them as the "sons of Ra." They migrated further into the interior of Africa to escape the slave raids of northern Arabs and the Islamic Sudanese Empires.

Many of the Sara benefited from the educational opportunities provided by the French in the colonial period. However, the Sara were also treated roughly by the French, who made them targets for forced labor and military recruitments. Today the Sara are one of the dominant peoples in modern Chad, making up 27 percent of the population. François Tombalbaye, the first President of Chad was of the Sara tribe. As part of a program of Africanization, he attempted to reestablish the yoindo as a requirement for advancement in the government or military. In 1975, after 15 years in office, Tombalbaye was assassinated during a military coup.
