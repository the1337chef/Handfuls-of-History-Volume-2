Ingessana Throwing Knife

Circa 1920

This rare form of throwing knife known as a muder (scorpion) is associated with the Ingessana (Gaahmg) of the Tabi Hills in the Blue Nile province of Sudan. The same peoples are also known to use a similar knife called a "sat" (serpent), which, as the name implies, has a sinewy shape and snake-like head. Collectively, the two styles of Ingessana throwing knives are called koleth. These knives are considered very early styles of the African throwing knife, and are derived from the simple types used by the Sara people. Stylized animals are included in the engraving to reinforce the animal allegory, inherent in the shape of these knives. On the muder, the scorpion symbol (deit) is on one side while an insect called a fil is on the other. The fil is a water insect, and although it may bite, its sting is mild compared to that of a scorpion. The presence of these symbolic creatures is invariant on this type of Ingessana koleth, although details of the designs will vary. The Ingessana describe their koleth in terms that would apply to the female body; head, neck, breasts (the crossbar), and loins. Jedri reports (Ingessana Throwing Knives (Sudan), Anthropos, Bd. 70, H. 1./2. (1975), pp. 42-48)), "The shank and hilt of each variety are engraved with either pairs of small incisions (representing the footprints of a small deer, mofor) or parallel zig-zag lines called 'the millipede' (dongole) and sometimes combinations of both.... On the head are designs that are fixed, on the loins are designs that are optional, the former are derived from harmful creatures and the latter from the harmless."

The Ingessana are primarily subsistence agriculturalists and have a reputation for being hostile to strangers as well as neighboring tribes and Arab traders. They remain a relatively cohesive but isolated people. Many Ingessana still practice an ancient form of sun worship, believing that the sun is their god, Tel, who is the creator of life. Reportedly, when an Ingessana dies he or she should be buried in the place where they die. Therefore, it is very important for an extremely ill Ingessana man to try to return to their home village and a woman who is close to death will leave her husband and family to try to return to the house of her natal family.

The Ingessana koleth are important to the tribal identity but less significant as weapons of war than traditional spears and smaller throwing sticks. This muder is a long (27 inches) and heavy weapon and probably not really intended to be thrown. The koleths are more likely to be wielded like a sword or ax, if used as a weapon at all. They are highly symbolic of a man's membership in the tribe, but not necessarily of status within the tribe since as noted above, the form, even to the level of the inscribed creatures, is invariant.
