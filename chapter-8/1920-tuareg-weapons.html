Tuareg Weapons

The Tuareg are nomadic Islamic people of Berber origin who live in the Sahara and central Sahel. They are sometimes known as the "blue people," since the men traditionally shroud themselves in indigo dyed wrappings, exposing only their eyes. Atypical of Islamic people, Tuareg women are less often veiled than the men.

The Tuareg claim "ownership" of large portions of the North African deserts and associated oases, which they maintain through intermittent occupation and intimidation of the other native people who they often consider as nothing more than slaves. The Tuareg society itself is stratified, with the various confederations (drum groups) headed by clans deemed to be nobility, while the lesser clans within the group are mere vassals. The noble clans are the "Imajaghan," (The Proud and Free) and the Takouba is the sword of these Imajaghan. The tellak is their traditional arm dagger. The Imajaghan have always prided themselves on their warrior skills, living a nomadic life and leaving the husbandry of goats, cattle and camel to the lesser vassal clans known as the Imghad. The Imajaghan controlled much of caravan trade across the Sahara and supplemented the tribute received from their vassals with raids on other people.

The Imajaghan claim the ownership of the fertile areas where irrigation agriculture is possible and permit cultivation of those areas by the people living there in return for a shared of the produce. The Tuareg overlords, for their part, are obligated to pay for the upkeep of the irrigation wells and canals and provide rudimentary agricultural implements.

Other castes important to Tuareg society were the clerics, known as Ineslemen or marabouts, and the blacksmith "Inadan" client caste. While the smiths and craftsmen were considered a lower class, they were also essential for the existence of the society. It is often hypothesized that Inadan were originally of a different ethnicity from the Tuareg, an idea supported by the fact that they spoke among themselves in a special dialect or secret language.

It is unlikely that there has even been a Tuareg "city," rather the tent is central to their lifestyle. The pastoral clans that husband large herds of goats or cattle might sometime settle for longer stays at sites of particularly good pasture, and on such occasions build huts of adobe, but even these were never intended to be permanent dwellings.

The Tuareg are a scattered and nomadic people with significant populations in at least five different countries. Wikipedia ascribes the largest population (1,600,000) to Niger where they would account for less than 10 percent of the total population. Likewise, the 850,000 living in Mali would account for 10 percent of the total population of that country.

Although the scattered tribes of the Tuareg share a cultural identity, the idea that they were ever a single nation has only the weakest foundation in the legend of an ancient queen, Tin Hinan, assumed to have lived in the 4th or 5th century, long before the coming of Islam to Africa. Legend has it that she was queen of Hoggar, in what is now southern Algeria. According to legend she united tribes and became the "mother of the tribe" and "Queen of the tents." A monument believed to be her tomb is located in the Sahara at Abalessa and was found to contain the skeleton of woman of a Mediterranean race with rich grave offerings. The tomb may have been built upon the remains of an earlier Roman fort.

The modern world has little to offer the Tuareg. However, living as they do on the edge of what most people would consider the habitable world, they persist.

For hundreds of years the Tuareg were the masters of the Sahara and the central plain to the south known as the Sahel, and the takouba was symbolic of their power. The long sword of the people to the east in the Sudan was the Kaskara, and to the west the Mandinka people carried their own distinctive long swords. Takouba blades are straight and double edged, tapering gradually towards the tip, unlike the blades of kaskara, which are of uniform width for most of their length. Usually, takouba blades are fullered along their lengths. The handles and cross guards are wrapped in leather, since it is said that the Nobel Tuareg have an aversion to touching iron. Scabbards are usually tooled leathers with baldric straps attached. The swords and scabbards were most often products of the Inadan caste, illustrating the debt the Imajaghan owed to a class of people they distained.

For the "Noble" Tuareg tribes, the companion piece to their takouba long swords was a telek arm dagger. As is typical with this type of weapon, this piece is really quite garish with lots of flash on what is unfortunately a rather poor quality weapon. A telek would be worn on the forearm with the hilt toward the wrist. However, the strap on the scabbard of this dagger is quite rigid and far too small for me to put my hand through.

Shown are two nicely made examples of Tuareg daggers. The grip styles and the brass bolsters are typical. With double-edged blades, they could be serious weapons, but they might also serve other useful purposes. Unlike the takouba swords and telek arm daggers, these daggers might have been used by Tuareg vassals as well as by the tribes considered to be of the noble lineages.
