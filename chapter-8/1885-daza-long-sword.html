Daza Long Sword

1885

This sword came out of Chad. The majority of the native people in Chad are of the Tebu (or Toubou) tribes. There are two main sub-groups, the Teda (Toubou) and Daza, with the Teda more in the North. When I acquired this sword, my friend Rodney Stubina was working in Chad. He sent pictures to an anthropologist friend of his who showed the pictures to natives in northern Chad, the former Kanem empire. They were of the opinion it was not Toubou. His e-mail said, "It is traditional Gorand from Bourku." The Daza are known as the Goran in Chad.

The scabbard is not actually leather but processed gut, probably goat or mutton. Also, the lace that wraps the handle is made from strips of the same material. This sword is unusually long for a typical Tebu weapon. It is made from a single piece of steel from the pommel to the unsharpened tip. The protrusion of the steel to form a "skull crusher" pommel is not uncommon for a Tebu weapon.

Rodney tells me that the Goran (or Gouran) are distinctively different from the Toubou, who are a bit further north. The Goran are generally lighter skinned, more Libyan in features, with less Bantu features. The concept that Teda and Daza are related, refers to the linguistic group more than anything. The Teda reside in the north, the Daza to the south.

Steve Bloom suggests the following regarding how the blade was formed:

"Start with a square piece - on the diamond - work it between two plates of steel - each with a grove aligned over the other - one 'corner' into the bottom and one (the opposite) into the top. Mash with a top & bottom tool to work it out."

The Goran are nomadic, but unlike the Tuareg who live on the edges of the dessert further west, they are not pastoral, since there is not much pastureland in Chad. Theirs is a culture of raids and marauding, weapons are therefore important to them.
