Circa 1925

Circa 1925: Approximately 1.6 million Tetela (Batetla in the plural) people live in the heart of the Democratic Republic of the Congo. They are among the group of tribes also known as Mongo. Like most of the tribes in the region, they supplement subsistence farming with hunting and fishing. Traditionally, they worshipped the god Motetela, whose name means either âhe who laughs notâ or âhe at whom one may not laugh.â Scarification is a common form of body adornment among the Tetela.

The weapons associated with the Tetela are distinctive, usually with pierced sheets of copper or other metals on the grip and menacing pommels that are themselves piercing weapons or are heavy and could be used for crushing blows. The blades are most often triangular like these, usually with etchings or engravings, but sometimes they are rounded at the end, like the sword attributed to the Teke people. Although these daggers and swords could, of course, be used as serious weapons, the Tetela are not especially renowned as a war-like people, so these pieces were also likely to have symbolic importance indicative of the ownersâ status within the tribal groups.

The Tetela speak a Bantu language and are one of the largest ethnic groups in the DRC. Many are fully integrated into the modern culture of the nation and no longer live traditional tribal lifestyles. They have, in large, also been reached by Christian missionaries and the majority are nominally Catholic.
