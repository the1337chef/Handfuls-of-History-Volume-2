Circa 1870

As discussed in the entry on the shotel, Ethiopia has the longest history as an independent nation in all of Africa. It was spared the Arab conquest that swept across North Africa and was never seriously threatened by the Turks. For the most part, it evaded European colonial imperialism as well, only yielding to an Italian invasion in the 1930s but recovering independence after World War II. Ethiopia also has a long and unique connection to Judaism and Christianity going back to the Queen of Sheba's visit to King Solomon in Jerusalem.

At some times in history, the strength of the Ethiopian throne was greater than at others, depending how effectively the current Emperor could unify and dominate the kings and tribal leaders in his domain. Probably the threats of European (i.e. Italian) intervention helped to bring the nation together under the successors to King Tewodros in the late 1800s.

The shotel is the oldest of the national swords of Ethiopia. The gurade type of saber was introduced in the 19th century. It is in the style of European sabers, sharpened only on the outside edge, while a shotel is sharp on both edges.

Gurade sabers often have European-made blades. This example has an elegantly curved 34-inch fullered blade, but no European proof marks are evident. The hilt is in the typical Ethiopian style, assembled from three pieces of rhino horn, the fibers of which are evident in the chip on the pommel. The scabbard is of tooled leather with faint patterning still visible. In the pictures the gurade is shown compared to the older shotel and the later Imperial saif.
