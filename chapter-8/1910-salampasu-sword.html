Circa 1910

The Salampasu are one of the many tribal groups living in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Their homeland is located on the southern border near Angola. They have a reputation as fierce warriors and their social structure is dominated by stratification based on proven warrior status among the warriors and chiefs. Their current population has been estimated between 60,000 and 120,000. The tribal name supposedly means "hunters of locusts" and historically, with a reputation as headhunters, they were widely feared by neighbor tribes.

The men concern themselves with warfare and hunting while the women of the tribe practice subsistence slash and burn farming. The people are famous for their ceremonial masks, which are often effective at evoking terror in the observers of the masquerades. These masks and ceremonies were key elements of their native religion which was rich is superstition and involved initiation/circumcision rituals for would-be warriors prior to their acceptance as adults.

Most Salampasu still live in rather primitive villages without schools, clinics, or churches. However, missionary groups report that most Salampasu are now Christian, and the modern governments have been successful at reducing intertribal warfare.

The swords of the Salampasu are quite unique and recognizable, distinctly different from the weapons of their many neighbors. The scabbard of this example is wood covered with leather and reinforced with three iron strips. The leather "ears" of the mouth of the scabbard are also typical of the Salampasu, as is the shape of the blade and handle. This example has an unusual leather pouch attached at the toe of the scabbard. It looks like it could be made of some sort of skin, and I have speculated, based on the reputation of the Salampasu as headhunters, whether the skin could have been human.
