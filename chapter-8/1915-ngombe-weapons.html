Ngombe Weapons

The Ngombe had a reputation as fierce warriors, headhunters and cannibals, but very little is really known about their history as a people. Today, roughly 150,000 Ngombe people live in villages close to the banks of the Congo River. A typical village has 30 to 100 huts. The Ngombe territory and village boundaries are fluid, as the village structures are impermanent. Hunting, and occasionally warfare, is the business of the Ngombe men, while the women fish and keep small gardens. Sometimes they will gather snails and other invertebrates to supplement their diets. The principle Ngombe form of body adornment is scarification, which is practiced on both men and women, usually on the face, but sometimes with the entire body as a canvas.

It is claimed that at present 98 percent of the Ngombe are Christian, predominately Roman Catholic. However, few villages are visited by a priest more than once or twice a year and so it is unlikely that they are very rigorous practitioners. It has also been reported that cannibalism has not been totally eradicated from the Congo.

There are several types of weapons associated with the Ngombe, including axes, throwing knives, and sickle swords (see below), but probably the most well-known type specifically associated with the tribe are so-called "executioner swords" like this one. However, it should not be taken literally that these were specifically used for judicial punishments, but rather that they impart an impression of power over life or death. Their flamboyant design is reminiscent of some of the more elaborate throwing knives, such as the Zande form, but they are far too heavy and unwieldy to be thrown effectively. The wrapped ricasso above the large multidisc pommel provides the only practical place to grip the weapon, although the pommel itself could be used to get a two-hand grip if necessary. Other examples of this type of weapon are generally of the same form, although varying in overall size. This is a relatively large example. There is also a second variant that ends with two semicircular blades, a decidedly unnecessary elaboration for the practical accomplishment of executions. It is safe to assume that, at least since the time of European intervention in Africa, these swords have mainly been for ceremonial use or as symbols of status. In earlier days of course, such intimidating weapons may have served different purposes.

While the large Ngombe swords referred to as the Execution type described above are impressive and imposing, they are rather too large to be practical for carrying. This sword is of a simpler style with a shape reminiscent of Abyssinian shotel. While it also has the appearance of a sickle, it is unlikely that it was used for agricultural purposes since the Ngombe did not as a rule raise grain crops that would be harvested with such a tool. Moreover, the blade on this sword is sharpened on both the inner and outer edges, while a true sickle would only be sharpened on the inner edge. The 20-inch blade has a flat diamond cross section and is lightly engraved with a hardwood handle bound with copper strips.
