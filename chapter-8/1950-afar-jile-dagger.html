Circa 1950

The Afar (Danakil) people reside in the Horn of Africa, the modern East African countries of Djibouti, Ethiopia, and Eritrea. They prefer to be known as the Afar, since the Arabic word "danakil" is offensive to them. They claim to be descendants of Ham (Noah's son). This is a classic example of the Afar "jile" dagger, also known as a qolxad. The blade is 11 inches long, curved and of flat diamond cross section, widest at its center. The hilt is made of dark wood with cone shaped brass pommel. The hide scabbard has a large brass tip. The bent asymmetric blade reminds me of the Somali billao daggers.

There are two subgroups of the Afar, the more prestigious and powerful Asaemara ("red ones"), who live primarily in the area of Assayita, and the poorer Adaemara ("white ones"), who live in the desert areas of the Afar Plain and in the Danakil Depression, a vast plain of salt pans and active volcanoes.

Most of the Asaemara are nomads, herding sheep, goats, cattle, and camels. They live in camps surrounded by thorn barricades, to protect themselves from the attacks of wild animals or enemy tribesmen. Their huts are made of palm mats and can be quickly taken down and reassembled when the tribal group moves with the herds.

Some of the poorest Adaemara live by prying loose slabs of solid salt in the Danakil Depression during the dry season. They then sell the ready-to-use salt in the form of crude blocks.

The major components of the Afar diet are meat and milk, with the offering of warm fresh milk to a guest having a very special social significance and implying a pledge of protection, as if the guest has been accepted as a family member.

The Afar are Islamic, but not Arab. Although their jile daggers have for them the same significance as the Arab jambiyas, in regards to being symbols of virility and personal identity, they are very important as practical tools and weapons.
