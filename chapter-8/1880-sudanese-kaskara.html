Circa 1880

Before Europeans were able to probe the interior of Africa from its western coast in the 1500s, Islamic people of empires based in the Sudan sent caravans and armies into central Africa for trade, conquest, and the acquisition of slaves. The power of these people relied on their cavalry, spears, and long swords. The kaskara was the iconic sword of the warriors of the Sudan and although in the present day they are not so much weapons of war as they remain symbols of the Sudanese warrior heritage.

"Sudan" can of course refer to the modern nation located to the south of Egypt and north of Ethiopia, but historically it has also been used to refer to the broad stretch of Africa between the Sahara desert and the savannahs north of the African interior, a region also sometime referred to as the Sahel. In this sense, the Sudan spans from the Atlantic to the Red Sea. Three great swords of the Sahel (compared in the pictures) are the kaskara of the eastern Sudan, the takouba of Tuareg tribes in the central Sahel, and the swords of the Mandinka in the West. Both the kaskara and the takouba bear a resemblance to cross hilt "Crusader swords." In the case of the kaskara, the type is believed to have originated in the 16th century.

The Islamic Empires of the Sudan that controlled the caravan routes to the interior of Africa for several hundred years grew weak, and in 1820, armies of the Ottoman Empire conquered the northern Sudan. In the early1880s, a religious leader named Muhammad Ahmad, declaring himself the Mahdi ("guided one"), began a movement that reunified the tribes of the Sudan against the Ottomans and newly arrived Europeans. In January 1885, the Mahdists conquered Khartoum, massacring many of the inhabitants, including General Charles George Gordon, the representative of the British crown, which was developing colonial aspirations for the Sudan. The British reacted by sending an army under the command of Lord Kitchener to take control of the territory. The British subdued the Mahdists after the death of Muhammad Ahmad and subsequently the Sudan became a British protectorate until 1955. As a result of the Mahdist war, many kaskara swords came to Britain as trophies and subsequently entered the western antique markets.

A kaskara was a treasured possession for Islamic men of warrior class throughout the entire eastern Sahel, from the Red Sea through Darfur and Chad. Some kaskara have blades that originated in Europe, while others have blades of local manufacture, with the native-made blades often imitating European blades in construction and their markings. A kaskara was worn either horizontally across the back or between the upper arm and upper chest so that the hilt was accessible under the armpit.

This particular sword was acquired by Artzi of Oriental Arms in the early 1970s, and kept as part of his private collection for more than 40 years until he was so gracious to release it for sale to me when I asked if he could help locate a nice example of this classic type of sword. It seems likely that the sword has an heirloom blade that may date as far back as the 1700s. However, the manufacture of the hilt and the remarkable condition of the scabbard and leather harness suggest that the blade was rehilted in the late 19th or early 20th century. The crocodile leather at the tip of the scabbard suggests the older range. There are langets above and below the hilt, although the upper one is under the leather. The pommel is leather and wood and so not heavy enough to affect the balance of the weapon.

Some of the engravings resemble marks associated with the sword makers of Solingen Germany. The paired moons, and flowery/fiery suns were common talismanic signs. The serpents engraved and both sided near the hilt also suggest European origin, since Islamic art would not be so figurative.

The harness is indeed rather complicated. However I find I can loop the thinner strap over my neck and the thicker one around my left arm to the shoulder and this holds it in place for hilt to be in position under my left arm, as seen in illustrations from the period. The braided cords may be simply decorative.

I do not think that the spatulate shape of the lower scabbard serves a purpose, but it is an identifying feature of the kaskara. Interestingly, the scabbards of the Mandinka swords from the far side of the Sahel have the same shape, although the swords themselves are rather different.
