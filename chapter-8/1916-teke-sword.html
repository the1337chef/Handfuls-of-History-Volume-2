Circa 1916

Artzi of Oriental Arms, attributed this sword to the Teke (Bateke) people who live in Gabon and in Brazzaville region of the Congo. However, I have not been able to confirm that association. The most common Teke swords are hook shaped.

There was a Teke kingdom north of the Congo River as long ago as 1600, when it was part of the slave trade to the African coast. The name of the tribe in fact means "to buy." In 1880 their King Makoko signed a treaty with the French that shortly thereafter led to the Kingdom of the Bateke becoming part of French Middle Congo. The Teke are now mainly located in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Blacksmiths were highly regarded in Teke society and often chosen as kings; in spite of this, there are not very many iconic Teke weapons. The most well known Teke artifacts are their masks.

Teke who still live in traditional villages are subsistence farmers, mostly of root crops, such as yams, supplemented with traditional hunting and gathering. The majority of the Teke profess Christianity, but probably without much conviction. The Teke live within the tsetse fly infested regions of Africa so large domestic animals such as cows and horses cannot survive. Unusually though, according to Wikipedia, the Teke do keep domesticated dogs and cats. The dogs are used for hunting. I can't say what purpose the cats would serve in Teke society.

To me the blade of this sword resembles a steel planarian (flat worm) as much as anything. Clearly it is not intended for thrusting attacks, if indeed it is meant to be a weapon at all. I have seen similar blades associated with the Tetela, but the grips of Tetela weapons are usually made with pierced metal sheets and the majority of Tetela weapons do have pointed blades and either heavily weighted or pointed pommels.
