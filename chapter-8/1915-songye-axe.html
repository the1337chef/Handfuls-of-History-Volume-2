Circa 1915

The Songye, along with the Kuba, are among the peoples that Christopher Spring refers to as Blacksmith Kingdoms. They reside in Democratic republic of the Congo and are known to be masters in metal work. This type of axe is usually associated with someone of high status within the tribe, and the stylized heads that always appear on this sort of axe are thought to be symbolic of the domination of other peoples by the owner of the axe. This example has just a single head, but examples are known that have as many as a hundred heads on the arms supporting the blade. The 15-inch shaft is wood covered is copper and the blade is 8 inches. Interestingly, I saw a similar piece on a video from the antique road show which was appraised in 1999 at a "conservative" $2000-$3000. It was reappraised in 2014 at $1500-$2000. I think a more realistic value would be in the range of $400-$500. At the time of this writing, there were three similar axes on eBay with "buy it now" prices from $250-$800. The nicest one in my opinion had more than a dozen heads and lizard-skin-wrapped shaft for $529.

The Songye traditionally relied mostly on farming and hunting for subsistence. Rivers are sacred to them, so they generally did not fish. The men are known for their metal work and the women for their pottery and weaving. The Songye believed in Efile, a creator god, although much of their daily worship was of their familial ancestors to whom they would offer sacrifices. There are approximately two million Songye living in the DRC and the evangelical Christians claim that presently they are 90 percent Christian (although only 16 percent evangelical).
