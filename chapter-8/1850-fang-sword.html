Circa 1850

The Fang people reside in the border areas of Gabon and Cameroon. The first Europeans in these areas were the Portuguese, who brought trade and Christian missionaries by 1500. However, tribal rule prevailed until the German Empire claimed the territory as the colony of Kamerun in 1884. The German colonies reverted to French control in 1916 and the British took a share after World War I concluded.

The Fang are one of the Beti-Pahuin peoples. They were largely Christianized by the mid-20th century and much of their native culture is lost except in the form of various artifacts, including weapons such as their throwing knives and swords. As with most native Africans, their original faiths were animistic, although some of the Fang also practiced Bwiti, a spiritual discipline that included use of the psychedelic, dissociative root bark of Tabernanthe iboga. Ibogaine, the active agent in Tabernanthe iboga, is presently under informal investigation as an addiction therapy drug. This has been referred to as a "vast uncontrolled experiment." Look for it online, not at your pharmacist.

In 1993 it was estimated that there were 858,000 speakers of the Fang language in Cameroon, Gabon and Equatorial Guinea. They are the politically dominant people in Equatorial Guinea. The Fang are known for their native arts, which included masks and guardian figures which they attached to wooden ossuaries.

In pre-colonial days, the Fang were known as cannibals and feared by neighboring tribes. Slash and burn techniques are still the dominant form of agriculture practiced by the Fang.

This is an old sword that dates to the pre-colonial era. Handsomely designed, it has an 18-inch, double-edged blade with a flat central rib and is lightly engraved at its upper part. It would be a formidable weapon in the hands of a cannibal.
