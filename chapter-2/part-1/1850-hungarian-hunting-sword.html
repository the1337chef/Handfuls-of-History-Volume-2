Circa 1850

"Waste not, want not." I believe that the blade of this sword was cut down from some sort of European military sword. It is single-edged and at some point saw some rather hard use, as there are numerous nicks on the blade. I believe that once it was modified, it was intended to be used as a hunting sword. Both the rustic engravings and the antler handle are consistent with this idea. In the era of single-shot muzzle-loading rifles, it would have been customary to have an edged weapon along to dispatch an injured beast, and a good blade would not have been allowed to just go to waste.

Is this blade from a broken sword, or was it cut down on purpose? The former case seems most likely, especially given the other signs of wear. However, it is possible that a retired soldier might have had their own sword re-hilted for civilian use. The sword did come with what seemed to be an original scabbard, cut down to fit the modified blade. However, the scabbard was in poor condition and has since perished (or at least gone missing in my closet).

It seems likely that the engraving and re-hilting were done at the same time. I wish I could identify the original sword type that the blade came from, or even just the maker, but the marks that would have given clues would have been on the upper shaft that no longer exists. The blade is actually somewhat unusual, with a straight single-edged blade and double fullers for most of the length. I have not been able to find images of another blade like it. I refer to it as a "Hungarian" sword because of the simple fact that I purchased it from an antique shop in Budapest in 1999. It is possible the blade originally came from somewhere else.
