German Hunting Sword/ Plug Bayonet

Circa 1750

This is a hunting sword that was made to serve also as a "plug bayonet". Bayonets of course are usually associated with military weapons, long guns in particular. As the name suggests, plug bayonets were shoved down the bore of a musket or rifle, thus plugging them. Although these were probably the oldest type of bayonet to be used, they have an obvious limitation, especially in battle. Socket bayonets, that became popular in the 18th century, had the clear advantage that once mounted they did not disable the use of the weapon as a firearm. As we know from romances of authors like Alexandre Dumas, we expect the musketeer to have a proper sword at hand when it comes time for close-quarter fighting. The situation for a boar hunter, armed with a single shot flintlock, would have been different. A wounded animal might present a very clear and immediate danger that would have to have been dealt with sooner than the gun could be reloaded under duress. The gently tapered handle of the plug bayonet can be quickly and securely fit to convert a musket into a formidable spear.

The design of the hilt, and the clamshell guard in particular, makes it likely that this piece came from one of the German states. The hilt has a notable resemblance to the combination hunting hanger/ flintlock pistol from Mainz. The charming engravings show dogs in pursuit of game, on one side a boar and on the other side a stag, further confirming its intended use as a hunting sword. Unfortunately, the leather scabbard was beyond recovery. There is a faint mark on the handle showing how deep the bayonet would have been inserted. Based on this mark, we can estimate that this bayonet was inserted about three inches down the bore of a musket. At that point the diameter would correspond to .70 caliber, and at that depth there would be no more than 1 degree of wobble of the handle in the bore.

The design of this piece is clearly well suited for use as a bayonet, which is not the case for Albacete daggers that are often misidentified as plug bayonets (see the photo from a current Czerny auction). I provide a picture comparing this knife to the Albacete dagger. You could not put the handle of the Albacete even an inch down a .70 caliber bore, and you tried to do so, there would be at least 4 degrees of wobble.
