Circa 1855

The Bowie knife is an icon of the American frontier associated with stories about James "Jim" Bowie, a prominent figure in the Texas war for independence. The term is frequently applied to any of a variety of large fixed blade knives. There are many stories, true and fanciful about Jim Bowie, and one such story of the so-called 1827 Sandbar Fight features his use of a large knife in a brawl that ensued after a duel between Samuel Levi Wells, a friend of Bowie's and Thomas Harris Maddox who was a friend of Norris Wright. Although honor was settled in the duel without injury to either party, old animosities flared provoking a free-for-all between the supporters of the original duelists. Bowie took several serious injuries but ultimately defended himself from an attack by Wright, killing him with his knife.

The original Bowie knives were reported to have been made from designs Bowie presented to Arkansas blacksmith James Black. As a result of the fame achieved by Bowie after the Sandbar Fight, Black's knives became highly sought after.

Bowie and his knife, which was probably of American manufacture, became famous and inspired the production of many similar knives, and even schools of knife fighting, in the U.S. Subsequently, blade-making centers of Europe began producing Bowie knives for export to America. A "Bowie knife" of typical design has a blade 7 to 9 inches long and over an inch wide, with a curved or clip point and usually with a false edge toward the tip on the back. They have a cross-guard to help parry attacks and to protect the user's hands.

One European center of production for Bowie type knives was the steel- and sword-making region of Sheffield, England. The blades of these English knives tend to be thinner than American-made knives, perhaps reflecting the quality of the steel. The also tend to have shallower false edges. This knife was made by Slater Brothers, who were located at Arundel Street and Fitzwilliam Street in Sheffield. The Slater brothers made a variety of knives as well as straight razors, many of which were marked "Venture" like this knife. This knife is elegant in its simplicity-other Slater knives had more ornate cutlery-type handles and pretentious engravings such as "Never Draw Without Reason Nor Sheath Me Without Honor."
