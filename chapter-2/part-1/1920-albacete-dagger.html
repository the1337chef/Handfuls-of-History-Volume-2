Albacete Dagger

Circa 1920

As Toledo is associated with Spanish swords, the city of Albacete has a special association with a distinctive style of Spanish dagger. Albacete is in the southeast of the Iberian Peninsula, in the region known as the Meseta Central within the historic region of La Mancha. Some stylistic aspects of this dagger carry over with other pieces related to Spain, like the knife from the Canary Islands.

This is a relatively common style of Spanish knife, I have seen as many as 10 of them come up in a single auction. About half of them have the dumbbell shaped cutout, as on this knife. Many surviving knives with the cutout have a brass insert fit into the space. Together, they are called a lengueta. There has been a fair amount speculation about what the purpose might have been served by these features. Were they used to hold and deliver poison? Some people have imagined that this type of knife might have been carried by a hunter and used to deal a final injury to a prey. It was sometimes the case that in delivering such a blow there might be such a deep penetration that the blade would be difficult to extract. Hunting swords and spears sometimes had a cross bar in the middle of the blade to limit how far they could be inserted. If these cross bars could be folded flat inside the blade or rotated perpendicular, then they might have been used to limit penetration when folded out. However, I have never seen an example where the crossbar could be rotated, so this is certainly a myth. It seems most likely that the lengueta was just a curious decorative element.

On older Albacete daggers the inlays on the handle would have likely been bone or ivory. It is likely that on this piece, which I believe is early twentieth century, they may be synthetic. It was purchased in an antique store in Toledo Spain. I am unsure of the significance of either the number or the other mark stamped on the blade.

Albacete daggers bear a certain resemblance to plug bayonets that were first developed in the seventeenth century. We typically associate bayonets with military rifles, but early plug bayonets were probably developed for use with hunting rifles and muskets. They would have served as an alternative to a hunting sword for delivering the coup de gras to an injured animal. However, a side-by-side comparison of this knife with a true plug bayonet shows how unsuitable these knives would have been as plug bayonets. The bulbous spindle-shaped handle could not have been shoved very far down the bore of any reasonably sized musket. Moreover, the relatively steep taper of the handle would have allowed the weapon to wobble and easily fall out. Nonetheless, the similarity is enough for some people that it is not unusual to see these daggers misidentified as plug bayonets, as was the case in a recent catalog for the famous Czerny auction house.
