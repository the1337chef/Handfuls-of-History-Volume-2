Circa 1938

The term penknife calls up a genteel sort of image as a small folding knife, useful to cut string or perhaps clean out the bowl of a tobacco pipe. The term switchblade has altogether a different connotation, a weapon for stealthy attacks by thugs in dark alleys. The darkest image for a switchblade is one where the blade comes straight out from the handle with force, thrusting itself into a hapless victim. In many places these sorts of knives are illegal. Another sort of spring-operated knife are those on which the blade snaps out from the normal folded position, rapidly and without the usual two-handed effort required to use the thumbnail groove on the top of the blade. These types of knives are not normally illegal. This charming little knife is of the latter type with the blade under such strong spring tension it will surprise you when it opens.

The design was one of several patented by George Schrade in 1937. Other knives covered in the same patent had side mounted "safety push buttons." This type is operated by pulling back the ball on the end. These knives were made with a variety of latch ends-rings, dice, miniature eight balls, or beads of different colors. The scales (side plates) were most often opalescent white plastic, as on this knife, and sometimes were branded with advertising to be used as promotional items.

George Schrade and his brothers Louis and William formed the Schrade Cutlery Co. in Walden, Massachuetts, in 1904. They specialized in spring-loaded knives with up to four blades under tension. This particular model was manufactured for and sold by Remington as the "Quick Point."
