Navaja of Santa Cruz de Mudela

Circa 1780

"Sirvo y Defiendo a mi Señor Dueño Polo," "I serve and defend my owner, Señor Polo."

The Spanish navaja takes many forms, and this is a very nice example from the 18th century in the style typical of Santa Cruz de Mudela, a municipality of the Spanish province Ciudad Real in Castile-La Mancha. Santa Cruz de Mudela has a rich tradition of blade making, though is not so famous for it as Toledo.

The Spanish crown restricted the weapons allowed to common citizens, forbidding them the use of fixed-blade swords. This led to the evolution of this sort of navaja, effectively a folding knife, that when extended had nearly the functionality of a short sword. This is clearly a hand-made piece, which is not to say that it was unprofessionally made. The ratcheting lock and large exposed spring are of robust and very basic design. When opened, it sounds out with the classic "click-click-click" of the navaja, a startling and perhaps terrifying sound for someone walking the streets of a Spanish town at night.

As noted above, the navaja takes many forms, and although they remain to this day an iconic Spanish knife, their importance as weapons has, of course, changed over time, especially with the advent of pocket pistols and revolvers, large numbers of which were produced in Spain from the late 19th century on. Smaller navajas, many of which were made in France for the Spanish market, remained popular pocket knives. Larger, impractical, and ostentatious forms of the navaja were made in cities like Toledo, probably in the hopes that they might catch the eye of a tourist. This knife, however, was made at a time when a Spaniard might have truly appreciated the service and defense it offered. In 1807 the armies of Napoleon invaded Spain, and the Spanish civilians fighting the French famously became credited as the inventors of Guerrilla warfare. This knife, made for a Señor Polo (or possibly Solo or Soto), may have served the Señor or his descendants in that war. After the defeat of Napoleon, Spain remained in turmoil as the Spanish succession was contested through a series of conflicts known as the Carlist wars. An heirloom weapon like this, which clearly has seen considerable use, might even have still been in the hands of a citizen soldier of the Spanish Republic in the 1930s.

The individualized character of this knife is brought out in the engravings, highlighted by red paint, a fair amount of which still remains, as well as the inscription to the original owner. The engraving was clearly done freehand. There are several features that testify to its history of use, including most obviously, the chip in the blade's edge where I imagine someone used the blade to pry something open. There is also well-repaired damage on the handle. This truly is a remarkable handful of Spanish history.
