Laguiole Knives

Circa 1990

There are several brands of excellent French knives, and my scientific colleagues introduced me to Laguioles (la-gee-oles) when I was a post-Doc at the Salk Institute in San Diego. I think the smaller of the two folding knives shown was a present from a friend, brought back from a trip to France, and that I bought the larger of the two folding knives myself. The steak knives were a present from my wife some years later. The first knives to be known as Laguioles were made in a southern French town of that name around 1829 by Casimir-Antoine Moulin, and the first folding ones by Jean-Pierre Calmels in 1840. They have a certain similarity to Spanish Navaja knives, which were also sometimes made in southern France for the Spanish market. Laguioles however, can be identified by the "bee" symbol on the front of the back edge. The bee is sometimes claimed to be associated with "B" as in Bonaparte, but by 1829 that association would have been more nostalgic than contemporary. Moreover, close inspection might suggest more of a two-winged fly than a bee.

The Laguiole designs are certainly elegant, but it should be appreciated that the term is generic one and not restricted to any one maker. There are in fact several manufacturers, mostly in the region around Thiers. You will see from the pictures of the two single knives and the set, there are differences in the trademarks and even in the flies on the spines.

The blades on the two folding knives are high carbon steel, which really is the best choice for a blade but requires great attention if it is to be kept shiny as when new. The larger of the two folders saw quite a bit of use when it was new and also clearly was not always well taken care of. The blade was once allowed to stain badly and shows signs of pitting from when it was later polished. Also, it appears that water was allowed to seep in around the bolsters so that green tarnish can be seen emanating from the brass pins. The smaller folder is in better shape, but largely because it has been little used. The blades of the steak knives are stainless steel. The set is used frequently, but these knives have always been cleaned before they even leave the table.

Some Laguioles are marketed especially for sommeliers and are equipped with corkscrews. I had one of these once but I think it was an inferior sort and I have lost track of it, probably gave it away. Nowadays, it is possible that in an up-scale steak house in a small city like Gainesville Florida you might see what looks like Laguiole knives. Look closely and you will see that these are almost certainly Chinese copies.
