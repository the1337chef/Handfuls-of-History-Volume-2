Circa 1840

A gentleman can never be too careful-or too discrete. This sword cane is a weapon that shows both elegance and great discretion, a very effective concealed weapon from the era of relatively unreliable single-shot firearms.

Concealed weapons have always had a certain appeal, especially for members of what might only pretend to be "polite society." To paraphrase the Roman epigramist Martial, "A rich man worries when he walks at night, but a poor man whistles when he meets a thief."

The silver head of the cane is of comfortable size and nicely cast. The cane itself, which serves as a scabbard, is of a good length to be used as an aid for walking. The sword is not one that would be fenced with, but the 13-inch, triangular, stiletto-like blade could certainly be used with deadly purpose.

This piece is probably English or American-made, most likely the former, but perhaps made to be sold in America. There are some small punches on the cane head but I can't make out any details, so I can't say if they were intended to be proof marks. There also may be some crudely scratched initials on the head, but these could have been made at any time and clearly are not in keeping with the original quality of the piece. The blade is blued and has decorative gold etching, in the style of early 19th century military swords, such as the Pattern 1803 English saber. The gilt etchings are mostly floral motifs, and very pretty for a weapon intended strictly for concealment. One imagines that this blade would only have been drawn for personal defense in extremis, perhaps its beauty was somehow intended as an excuse for its lethality, or for carrying it in the first place.

Man is the animal that "walks on three legs" in the evening of its life. Would this third leg lessen the worry of a rich man who walked at night?
