Circa 1850

The iconic Spanish navaja folding knives were made in a variety of sizes, some with blades reportedly as large as three feet in length. However, most of the larger knives had blades in the range of six- to eight-inches long and were made for serious fighting. These knives were sometimes referred to as santólios, from the Spanish for holy oil, a reference to the sacrament of last rites, which might follow a deadly encounter with such a weapon.

The very largest navajas were more likely to have been made for the tourist trade, which was already a significant market as far back as the mid 1800s. Extremely oversized examples, with the blades as long as sabers, were more likely made as display pieces. These were known as navajas de muestra or navajas de exposición.

The blade of this knife is deathly sharp, but being nine and a half inches long, the knife was probably made more to catch the eye of a tourist than of a street fighter. The style of the ring pull, and blade locking mechanism, date the knife to around 1850, prior to the preferred use of ratcheting locks. It is marked "Navajas de Toledo," probably denoting a shop in the city of Toledo. A Google image search on "Navajas de Toledo" will discover several siblings to this knife, differing primarily in the decoration of the grip panels, and probably sold out of the same shop. When I purchased this knife from a British dealer, I in fact had a choice between this knife and one such sibling that had plain colored panels on the handle. Under strong magnification, the panels on the grip of this knife appear to be horn with bone inlay, sealed with a clear vanish overlay.

Other aspects of the grip style, including the brass fitting at the tip with a series of circles decreasing in size, are common on navajas by other makers, including the French knife maker Batisse. The pictures show these similarities in design as well as the differences in size between this knife and the French version. As the French were making practical weapons for sale in Spain, the Spanish were making impractical pieces for tourists to take back to England or perhaps France.
