1943

The U.S. Army began issuing Bolo knives in 1904 to serve purposes somewhat intermediate between a weapon and tool for cutting brush. The military would find purposes for such tools long after the military sword became relegated to merely ceremonial duties. This is a Marine Hospital Corps Knife of the type adopted in 1942. It was likely to have been used in the Pacific theater of World War II.

A common misconception is that such a knife was intended for field amputations, but even as far back as the American Civil War it was known that the best course when facing such a loss was to put on a tourniquet until a proper field hospital could be reached.

This knife was designed for field medical support, but more for making splits or building stretchers than for chopping limbs or performing surgery. It has a rounded tip that is sharp but not pointed. In this way, it is like the German Red Cross Hewer. Somewhat ironically, the German Hewer has a saw tooth edge, which would make it useful for cutting small limbs, while this is just a tool for hacking branches. But while this tool was intended for use in the field, the German Hewer was largely just a bit of Nazi foppery.

Perhaps 200,000 or more of these knives were made, a significantly larger number than the number of the Medical Corpsmen who served in the Pacific theater. More than anything else, it reminds me of a meat cleaver.
