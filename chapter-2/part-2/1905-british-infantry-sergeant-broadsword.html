British Staff Sergeant Broadsword

Circa 1905

This is a Wilkinson-made sword issued to a British staff sergeant during the reign of Edward VII, as clearly indicated by the royal cipher built into the guard. Albert Edward of the house of Winsor, who would latter be crowned Edward VII, had been the Prince of Wales for 60 years when he finally ascended the throne on January 22, 1901, as King of the United Kingdom and the British Dominions and Emperor of India. He ruled for less than ten years and died on the May 6, 1910. He held the position of Prince of Wales longer than any of his predecessors, although he has recently lost that distinction to the current Prince of Wales who was invested with the title in 1952, 61 years ago at the time of this writing. Edward was largely excluded from power while his mother was alive, filling only ceremonial and minor diplomatic roles in the final years of his mother's life. He was married to a Danish princess but had numerous and not-well-hidden affairs, gaining him a reputation as bon-vivant playboy. After he finally took the throne he came to be known as the uncle to all of Europe because he was in fact the uncle to, or cousin of, most of the crown heads. He reigned toward the end of the period known as the 40-Year Peace in Europe, although that peace owed little to either Edward's diplomacy or kinship to the other European rulers.

While this sword is more robust than other NCO swords, such as the Union NCO sword from the Civil War or the Prussian/Bavarian NCO sword, like those it is only sharpened at the point. It other ways, it also gives the impression of being more functional that decorative. There is a mark that identifies Wilkinson as the maker, along with the British broad arrow acceptance mark. There are also some unit marks which have been struck over. The leather-covered scabbard is better suited for the field than the parade ground.

Another interesting part of the story of this sword is that it came to the U.S. from the British army by way of Nepal. I found it among the other artifacts in the Windlass steel warehouse in Atlanta, part of the trove of "Treasures"* from the Royal armory of Nepal. Presumably, the unit numbers were over struck when the weapon was removed from British service after the death of King Edward or otherwise reassigned. Was the sword previously issued to a sergeant on the staff of the British "resident" in Kathmandu? Or was it perhaps acquired by Ghurka while in service with the British and then brought back to Nepal?


* For more information see Handfuls of History, Volume 1, Treasures of Nepal.
