1864

Throughout the American Civil War there were many changes in weapons technology. Some represented relatively radical advances, while others were made for the sake of economy or practicality. In 1862, the Union began to issue a new somewhat lighter cavalry saber to replace the Model 1840 saber that had gained the nickname "Old Wristbreaker."

The saber remains the most iconic of all cavalry weapons, but in reality by the 1860s they were becoming eclipsed by revolvers and the carbines. In the first engagement of the battle of Gettysburg, Union cavalry, led by General John Buford, encountered the vanguard of the Confederate forces. They dismounted, taking a tactically advantageous position, and due to the fact that they were armed with a variety of rapidly loaded carbines-Sharps, Burnsides, Merrills, Ballards, and Maynards-they were able to delay the Confederate advance long enough for regular Union army units to arrive and take up positions on the high ground outside of the city.

Two thirds of the three hundred thousand 1860 sabers made during the Civil War were made by the Ames Co. The second-largest producer was Roby. This saber was made by Emerson and Silver of Trenton, New Jersey, another known contractor who produced them in smaller numbers. It is missing the leather washer that should be between the blade and the hilt, and the grip has been restored. Model 1860 sabers were sometimes used by foot officers who could not afford to purchase the more ornate regulation Model 1850 Army Staff and Field Officer's Sword. The model continued to be used as the official U.S. cavalry sword through the Indian wars and the Spanish American War. Although it was officially superseded by the Model 1913 "Patton" saber, the more elegant Model 1860 is still sometimes used for military ceremonies.
