1918

One Sunday I took a ride to High Springs to check out the antique mall. There I found this World War I vintage Bolo Knife. On the way home the Harley, which was my chosen ride for the day, started to cut out and eventually quit all together, leaving me on the roadside many miles from home. I was savvy enough to guess that what broke was most likely the last thing I "fixed." I suspected a loose connection on the recently installed battery. Unfortunately, in recent years I had become so complacent that I wasn't carrying a toolkit, not even my many-featured Swiss Army knife. I did at least have the 1917 Bolo knife. Its broad tip was sufficient to unscrew the battery cover and, once I confirmed my hypothesis, I was able to tighten the loose battery terminal bolt and ride home. Clearly such a tool could be useful in many situations.

The U.S. Army's first issued Bolo Knife was the Model 1904. They were intended for Hospital Corps troops to be used to clear brush and cut poles-sort of like the French Artillery swords, which came to be known as cabbage choppers. Like the Artillery sword, when pressed they could be used as close-combat weapons. It was followed by the Model 1910 and then the Model 1917. Large numbers of the Model 1917 were produced in 1917 and 1918, Plumb (Philadelphia and New Orleans) and ACCO. During World War I, a few Bolos were issued each unit with their type and intended mission. They were issued to machine gun squads to be used for clearing their field of fire.

The pommel on this piece is damaged but the sheath is in relatively good shape (white paint splatter not withstanding). Both the knife and the sheath are dated 1918, making it unlikely that they were sent with the U.S. expeditionary force to France. With some difficulty the remnants of the word "Philadelphia" can be made out over the 1918 date.

It was certainly a useful purchase for me, but when I got home I made sure to store a Leatherman multi-tool in the Harley's saddlebag.
