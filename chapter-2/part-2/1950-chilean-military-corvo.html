Chilean Military Corvo

Circa 1950

The curved corvo is considered the national knife of Chile. The designs probably originated from the use of such knives on ranches or in the Chilean silver and copper mines. Originally civilian versions of the knife had only the inside (concave) surface sharpened.

Chile's first national call to arms was with the War of the Pacific against Peru and Bolivia (1879-1883), which was fought over the nitrate-rich, centuries-old deposits of Guano in the Atacama Desert and on the costal islands. The nitrates were used as fertilizer and to make saltpeter, a primary ingredient of gunpowder. The Chilean soldiers armed with the corvo knives they brought with them to war became renown as fighters. Reportedly, they would fire themselves up with Chupilca del diablo (The Devil's Chupilca), a mixture of gunpowder and aguardiente (firewater).

Chile was victorious in the war and gained territorial concessions from both Peru and Bolivia. Bolivia has forever since been a land-locked country. Following the war, Chile adopted a version of the corvo as their official military knife. Unlike the civilian versions, the military knives are double edged.

This is an Atacameño version (having a relatively mild curve to the blade) of the NCO model of the corvo. Knives for officers and commandos had shorter blades with a pronounced hook. It was made by FAMAE (Factory of Arsenals of the Army of Chile). The plastic sheath suggests relatively recent production.
