1842

Cutlass is a term most often associated with broad-bladed, single-edged swords used by sailors in ship-to-ship, close-quarters combat. It is a weapon with which to slash at an opponent or hack at a ship's rigging, and not the sort of weapon with which one would necessarily choose to fence or duel. The blade of the weapon is not unlike a machete, but a cutlass hilt typically has a guard to protect the user's hand from attacks. It is hard to imagine a more iconic naval cutlass than the French Model 1831. This example was made at the French Châtellerault armory in 1842. This was shortly before the establishment of the French Second Republic.

After the fall of Napoleon, the victorious European allies restored the Bourbon monarchy. However, the combined reigns of Louie XVI and his younger brother Charles X were only 16 years. The most notable accomplishment associated with the reign of Charles X was the conquest of Algeria, which occurred on the eve of another revolution, which resulted in the deposition of Charles in favor of King Louis-Philippe of the house of Orleans. Louis-Philippe was broadly supported by the bourgeois and his reign brought France closer to a constitutional monarchy. Although in some ways France thrived during the reign of Louis-Phillip, his government was never very stable, threatened by both the right and left parties, and attempted coups d'é·tat by surviving relatives of Napoleon. In 1848 Louis-Philippe abdicated, and the French Second Republic was proclaimed.

With the fall of Napoleon, France had lost most of its colonial empire, except for a few small territories in South America and West Africa. The conquest of Algeria marked the beginning of a second phase of French colonial expansion, necessarily supported by the French Navy, although much of that was to come decades later toward the end of the 19th century as France joined with the other powers in a scramble for territories in Africa and Asia.

This cutlass was purchased from an antique weapons dealer in the French Quarter of New Orleans, Louisiana, just at the time I began seriously collecting. It has a marvelous feel of weight and balance and a lovely patina.
