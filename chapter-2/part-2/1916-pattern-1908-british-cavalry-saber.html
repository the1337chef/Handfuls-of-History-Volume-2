1916

In 1908, the British army adapted a new sword design intended to reinvent cavalry tactics. For over a thousand years, the swords of light cavalry were intended to be used more for cutting than for thrusting, in the spirit of the Persian Shamshir. Beginning in around 1820, the British, followed by the French and later the Americans, began the process of straightening out their cavalry swords so they could be used for cut or thrust, inevitably making them not ideal for either purpose.

The Pattern 1908 sword was the ultimate commitment to the straight-arm charge, where the sword extends the locked arm into a sort of lance. It was in part inspired by the French Model 1896, but was more exaggerated in design. The blade has a thick "T" cross-section making it very rigid in spite of it stiletto-like narrowness and very sharp tip. The hand guard is large and bowl shaped, and the pistol grip on the hilt improves the alignment with the extended arm. The pommel is large and heavy to keep the balance point close to the hand guard. Unfortunately, this decision to reinvent cavalry warfare came a bit late in history to be of any military importance.

The movie "Warhorse" has a scene illustrating the intended image for a charge by cavalry with such sabers. They attack the back of a German camp and put the surprised troopers to flight. However, they pass through the camp almost unopposed and they reach the alerted line of machine guns on the other side of the camp. The gunners rotate their weapons on their mounts, and well, the horse, who is the star of the movie, survives. I saw the movie and then went shopping for this sword. The grip is etched PAB. The story that came with the sword attributes those initials to Paul Albert Burnet. The sword was made by Wilkinson and has all the appropriate inspector's marks. The Brown finish to the scabbard and hilt is original and correct.
