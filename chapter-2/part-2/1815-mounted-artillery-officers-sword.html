Circa 1815

This sword is difficult to place. There are features in common with both English and American swords of the early 1800s, contemporary to the War of 1812. The blue and gilt etching is a match to the pattern 1803 English saber, but the hilt is not quite right for that pattern; it is a bit too plain. American officers' swords of the era have similar etching but usually include the motto, "e' pluribus unum," and also usually with an eagle's head pommel rather than a plain one, as on this sword. The American sword closest to this design is the mounted artillery officer's sword. I'll hazard a guess that this sword crossed lines during the War of 1812, with the blade originally part of a captured English saber, then re-hilted in the style of a U.S. mounted artillery officer's saber. It is of an era when an officer's sword was more than a decorative uniform accessory. Curved sabers like this, intended to deliver slashing blows from horseback, remained important weapons through the end of the 19th century. Whether the hilt is the original or a replacement, it clearly has been damaged and repaired. There are also a few substantial notches on the edge suggesting serious use. It is interesting to speculate that this sword may have come from England and then changed hands to perhaps go as far as Mexico City in the service of an American officer.
