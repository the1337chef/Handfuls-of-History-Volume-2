Circa 1910

By World War I, the sword of a U.S. Army officer had become little more than accessory to their dress uniforms, but to a Prussian officer it was probably something more. Prussian military training retained a strong tradition of dueling and to display a scar from a dueling experience was honorable. Therefore a Prussian officer would have valued their sword as a weapon, although whether to be used against an enemy or a fellow officer might be another question.

This sword belonged to officer in the Imperial German Army during World War I. It was made by Weyersberg, Kirschbaum & Company (W K & C), the major manufacturer of edged weapons in Solingen probably sometime between 1910 and 1918. It has the royal cipher of Kaiser Wilhelm II on the grip and the folding guard has an image of the Prussian Imperial eagle. This style of straight bladed sword was called by the Prussians a "Degen," and was specified by regulations of 1889. The style of the scabbard however dates it to post 1906.

Officers typically purchased their own swords and although this one has a very nice hilt, it has no etching or engraving on the blade. However, the blade does feel as if it has been sharpened. There is an interesting contrast, shown in the pictures, between this and the U.S. 1902 "sword for all officers." The U.S. sword has a much plainer hilt but an etched yet unsharpened blade.
