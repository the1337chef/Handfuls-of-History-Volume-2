1917

With tanks, trenches, and airplanes, World War I was a different kind of war from any other fought previously. The U.S. entered the war late, sending troops to France only after the war had been ongoing for three years. The U.S. had the opportunity to learn at least a little bit from their allies about what to expect. Whether they got it right is another question.

It was decided that hand-to-hand fighting, if such was to occur in the trenches, would require a special sort of weapon, something that combined a stiletto with brass knuckles. This led to the design of the M1917 Trench Knife, and the U.S. government placed orders for such knives with several U.S. contractors. The inspiration for the Model 1917 reportedly came from weapons known as French nails, which were hand made from broken bayonets by soldiers in the trenches.

If the M1917 had a virtue, it was that it was relatively light. The triangular spike-like blade was good for thrusting attacks only and, with no cutting edge, it lacked the basic functionality of a kitchen knife. The knuckle guard was studded, presumably to make it a sort of fist weapon, but the grip was on the wooden handle and not solidly connected to the guard as on true brass knuckles. By 1918, the U.S. had designed a replacement, the Mark I trench knife, which had a double-edged blade and a cast hilt with separate rings for each finger, improving the grip if it were to be used as a fist weapon. However, the Mark I went into production too late for use in the First World War. In World War II, some Mark I knives were issued to airborne troops, Army Rangers, and Marine Raiders, but by 1943 they were replaced by the more standard-appearing M3 fighting knife.

This "knife" was made by Landers, Frary & Clark (L.F.&C.) of New Britain, Connecticut, who were also contracted to make Model 1913 Calvary sabers, another weapon of questionable utility.
