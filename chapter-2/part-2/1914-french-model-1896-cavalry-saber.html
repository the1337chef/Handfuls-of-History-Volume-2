1914

Throughout most of the 19th century the French were leaders in both firearm and sword design. In the later half of the century they were espousing the philosophy that for cavalry sabers it was the point, not the edge, that was important. The French Model 1822 Cavalry Officer's Sword, which set the pattern for the U.S. Model 1840 saber, had a long, gently curved blade reflecting the older philosophy, manifest in the design of the Middle Eastern shamshir, that in the melee of close combat important attacks were dealt with overhand slashing blows delivered with the tangent of a curved blade. With the advent of the revolver as a back-up weapon, the role of the sword attack in close combat was diminished, but the shock and awe of the initial cavalry charge was still considered important.

The French Model 1896 saber has, if anything, a more beautiful and elegant hilt than earlier models, with floral and mythological embellishments. Note the head of Medusa, harkening back to the Aegis of Athena. This sword was made at the French national armory of ChÃ¢tellerault and is dated 1914. It was certainly in the possession of a French officer during World War I, although it cannot be said if it was used in combat. It shows no sign of hard use.

The British, and later American, armies took inspiration from this French sword for the British Model 1908 and American Model 1913 "Patton" sabers, but they refined the hilt deigns to optimize what little utility they thought might still exist for a cavalry sword. The elegant swept hilt of the French sword was replaced with large sheet metal hand guards and the handles of the English and American swords were modified to have a more exaggerated pistol-grip-like shapes with thumb rests and heavily weighted pommels.

This sword was made at a time when, for most military swords, form was more important than function. Calvary sabers and Naval cutlasses were amongst the last swords of real importance as weapons. The Patton swords were designed for function, but likely were never used in combat. The French swords, although not optimized, would have been suitable for combat and of course since the war was much longer for France than it was for America, many did see use in combat. In any case, who would have known for sure in 1914 (or for that matter, in 1896) how far or how fast the role of cavalry in the war would decline in the next two decades? The blade of this sword is plain, with an unsharpened edge but purposeful point. The blade of this sword is about six inches longer than the American Model 1902 Army officer's sword, which with its etched, unsharpened, curved blade, was definitely intended more for show than for combat.
