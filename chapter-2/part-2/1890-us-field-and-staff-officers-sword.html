U.S. Field and Staff Officer's Sword

Circa 1890

In 1860, the U.S. Army introduced a new pattern sword for general staff and other high-ranking officers. The uniform regulations allowed such senior officers to carry this sword instead of the much heavier 1850 pattern sword. In 1872, this substitution became mandatory. One way in which the pattern 1860 sword was remarkable is that it was the first U.S. military sword with virtually no pretense toward being a serious weapon. The trend to move away from swords and lances as the main weapons of war is as old as the harquebus. However, even today, swords are retained as a symbol of military authority.

More than three dozen different companies are known to have produced this model of sword for private purchase by U.S. officers, they were never "government issued." This sword was made by the M.C. Lilley Company of Columbus, Ohio, which was founded in the mid 1860s and remained in business until 1933 when it was absorbed by Ames manufacturing, who carried the brand until 1966. The founder, Mitchell Campbell Lilley, died in 1882, and after that date the company's swords were marked "THE M.C. LILLEY & Co. COLUMBUS O."-meaning this sword can be dated as post-1882. In addition to producing military swords, the Lilley company was known for making presentation swords and other ceremonial regalia for fraternal orders such as the Knights of Columbus and the Masons.

In 1902 the U.S. Army introduced to the Model 1902 saber as the new sword for Officers, and the Lilley Company switched to producing that model. Therefore, this sword can be dated with confidence to sometime between 1882 and 1902. The only U.S. military conflict of note during that era was the Spanish-American War, and it would seem likely that the owner of this sword was at least enlisted during that time. It is quite unlikely that this sword would have been on any battlefield of that war, being more suited to a parade ground.

The sword is almost in the style of a European court sword and is a rather attractive and gracile piece. It was part of the official specifications that the blade should be etched and this was apparently a technology that was well developed by the time this sword was made. The cross guards of the sword are plain on the backsides providing ideal surfaces for engraving the name of the owner. However, this sword was never personalized.
