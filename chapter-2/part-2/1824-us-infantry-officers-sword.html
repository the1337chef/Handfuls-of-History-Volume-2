Circa 1824

It is relatively difficult to identify and classify the swords used by U.S. military officers prior to 1850. During the Revolution and the following two decades, there were no regulations, and since officers supplied their own weapons, many types of swords were used. George Washington reportedly carried his hunting sword during the war. Although by 1800 there were regulations that provided guidelines of a sort, there were still no identifiable single patterns for officers. Prior to 1821, the guidelines stipulated that swords should have curved blades, and in 1821 they were changed to stipulate straight blades for the swords of infantry officers. They were to have knuckle bows, often beaded, and typically had some sort of figurative pommel. Harold Peterson's book, The American Sword, illustrates several examples of officer's swords from the period between 1821 and 1840. One of his examples (plate 66) is nearly identical to this sword in the shape of the beaded knuckle bow, eagle-head pommel, and ivory grip. He remarks that it "is a good early example of the type undoubtedly made well before 1830." It seems likely that the same could be said for this piece. The sword pictured in Peterson's book has an etched blade, but there is no etching or maker's marks still evident on this piece. Like the one in Peterson's book, this sword is single-edged, with a broad fuller.

The date for this sword therefore falls between the War of 1812 and the Mexican-American War, a period sometimes known as the "thirty-year peace." America's standing army was reduced at this time to as few as 6000 men. The downsized army supported a certain amount of pioneering on the Western frontier and occasional conflict with Native American peoples, the most serious of which were the Seminole Wars in Florida. Pressure in both these areas had the American army scaled back up toward 12 to 15 thousand by 1836, when things were heating up on the frontier near Texas, which achieved its independence from Mexico in that year.

This sword has an element of rarity, being an officer's sword from a time when there were an unusually small number of American soldiers and fewer officers. However, if, as Peterson suggests, it was made before 1830, the officer who owned it might still have been serving in 1836, the time of the second Seminole War.
