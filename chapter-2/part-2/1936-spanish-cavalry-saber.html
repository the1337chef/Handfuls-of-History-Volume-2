Circa 1936

In 1907, the Spanish fell in with the trend toward the use of the point over the edge on a cavalry sword. This trend was most refined in the British Model 1908 and latter the American "Patton" saber. The Spanish 1907 Pattern Puerto-Seguro saber is clearly on the same path, but not so refined as a purposeful thrusting weapon since it lacks the pistol-style grip. The design of this sword is attributed to the Marquess of Puerto-Seguro, Luis Carvajal. As a Cavalry Captain he was a strong supporter of the point over the edge.

The Kingdom of Spain, which had been a dominant European power for several centuries, never really recovered from the Napoleonic conquest in 1808. The brief infusion of liberal thinking into Spanish politics during the period of French power undermined the subsequently restored Spanish monarchy, leading to a series of conflicts between supporters of absolute and constitutionally limited monarchy known as the Carlist wars. Not only was the monarchy insecure, the Spanish colonial empire was rapidly evaporating. In the 19th century, revolutions freed Mexico and South America, and by 1900, Puerto Rico and the Philippines were lost to emerging American imperialism.

In 1907 Spain was but a shadow of its former self, holding on to a few territories like the Canary Islands and a few colonies in Africa. Holding these colonies required a large enough army to provide General Francisco Franco his power base, with which he was able to oppose the Spanish Republic, which was declared in 1931.

Prior to the beginnings of the Spanish Republic, all Spanish 1907 cavalry sabers had the royal crest on the hilt. The absence of the Royal sigil identifies this as a sword made for the Republic (1931-1936). It was probably carried during the Spanish Civil War, but like most cavalry weapons of the twentieth century, never used in battle.
