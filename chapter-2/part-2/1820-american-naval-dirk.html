Circa 1820

Any sort of double-edged dagger can technically be called a dirk, but the term dirk has two most common associations. One is for the large daggers of the Scottish Highlands, which probably had their origins in the so-called "bullock daggers" of the Middle Ages. The other is with naval daggers, especially the small, graceful weapons of junior officers. There are such dirks attributed to the navies of major European countries and nations of the Americas, especially the U.S., as well as with the Japanese navy after Meiji restoration, when the Japanese began to adopt European military styles.

The European and American naval dirks of this type were weapons permitted to, and most closely associated with, midshipmen. The rank of midshipman during the Tudor and Stuart eras was assigned to senior seamen who in fact worked amidship. However, by the 1700s the term came to be associated with young apprentice officers who had served as volunteers on board for a period of at least three years. These were often boys from the gentry who accepted service as a cabin boy or a similar sort of on-board servant as a means to a career in the navy. This sort of on-the-job training was the standard path for young officers until later in the 19th century when the career path for naval officers shifted toward naval academies and a more academic education.

This dirk has a nine-inch blade and a three-inch handle. It would have been appropriate for a young midshipman in the typical age range of 13 to 16. It would have been worn with their best dress uniform. As was the norm for any officer's weapon at the time, it would have been privately purchased. For that reason naval dirks of this era can vary significantly in quality. This piece is certainly not of the highest quality, but not too far off of average. There are traces of the original etching on the blade, which when new were probably brought out with bluing, as on the contemporary mounted artillery officer's sword. The handle is ivory or, perhaps more likely, bone. The guard and other fittings are cast brass. While it can't be said conclusively, it seems most likely that this was American. The only other reasonable alternative would be that it was British. It probably dates to the period of 1800 to 1820, so it might have been shipboard during the war of 1812.
