Circa 1920

The 1872 decision to standardize the 1860 Staff and Field Officer's sword, as the sword for officers was not universally popular. It clearly converted the status of an officer's sword from weapon to regalia. In 1902, a new sword pattern was specified for officers that brought back at least the appearance of a serious weapon. The sword has roughly the dimensions and appearance of the 1860 cavalry saber, with a simpler grip and slightly straighter blade. Also, as was the custom for officers' swords, Model 1902 sabers have etched blades. Although they certainly have a more purposeful appearance than the Model 1860 swords, it was not really the expectation that they would have to serve as weapon, and so the blades are usually not sharpened. The model is still the current sword for U.S. Army officers, although their use is essentially ceremonial, equipping honor guards at weddings, memorials, or other official functions or parades.

This sword was made by the M.C. Lilley Company of Columbus, Ohio, which also made the 1860 Staff and Field Officer's sword in this collection, and it came with limited and confusing provenance. It was a gift from a friend, as a contribution to this collection, taken from their attic where it had been stored. At the time I was under the impression that it was a family heirloom and I thought I was told that the sword had been a grandfather's who had been an officer in World War I. I recently contacted my friend and got a rather different story. She said that it came from a friend outside the family whose father was an officer in the Navy in World War II. That story does not fit the sword since this is a 1902 Army sword and not a twentieth century Navy pattern. They must have gotten the story wrong somehow and perhaps they skipped a generation and it was a grandfather's sword from a time closer to World War I than World War II. I can at least imagine it being carried in a parade celebrating the November 1918 armistice, but really I can't say much more than if it had come to me with no provenance at all.
