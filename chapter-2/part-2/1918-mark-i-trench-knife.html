Mark I Trench Knife

1918

This knife was designed to replace the U.S. Model 1917 and Model 1918 trench knives. Unlike the former models that had bayonet-like triangular blades, the Mark I had a standard double-edged blade that was more useful for cutting purposes. The cast hilt, with separate rings for each finger, was designed to be easier to grip and hold onto in a fight. The heavy brass knuckles also made it a formidable fist weapon.

A small number of Mark I trench knives were produced by a French manufacturer, Au Lion (Au Lion/ Société Générale, France) in the summer of 1918. It was thought that by having them produced in France it would be possible to get some in the field sooner. In the fall of 1918, orders for a total of 1,232,780 Mark I knives were placed with several U.S. contractors, including Landers, Frary & Clark (L.F.&C.) of New Britain, Connecticut; Henry Disston & Sons (HD&S) of Philadelphia; and Oneida Community Limited (O.C.L.). However, with the signing of the Armistice in November 1918, these large orders were canceled and a single order reduced order for 119,424 knives was placed with Landers, Frary & Clark Co.

Upon delivery, most of the Mark I knives were put into storage until the U.S. entered World War II. In 1942 some Mark I knives were issued to airborne troops, Army Rangers, and Marine Raiders. Opinions of the knives were mixed, and by 1943 they were being replaced by more standard-appearing M3 fighting knives.

After World War II, remaining Mark I knives were sold off as government surplus and have since become relatively valuable collectors items. The classic brass knuckle appearance has also made this model popular for copies and reproductions that vary greatly in quality.
