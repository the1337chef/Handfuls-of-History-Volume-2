Circa 1868

Klingenthal, located in the Alsace, was one of the two main French armories producing swords at the beginning of the nineteenth century, the other being Châtellerault. From 1836 onward Klingenthal was privately owned and managed by members of the Coulaux family until it closed in 1962. Prior to 1870, Klingenthal produced many swords for French government contracts, as well as swords like the French 1822 Model saber, for private sales to officers. After the Franco-Prussian war of 1870, Klingenthal, along with the rest of the Alsace, was in German hands. They filled German contracts for bayonets, and the company continued to produce various items for sale including cutlery and even farm implements such as sickles.

This sword was found in a Florida antique market for the very reasonable price of $115. I have only once seen a similar piece and that was in the catalog of vendor in Paris. I believe the price on the Parisian sword was something like $1000. To the best of my knowledge, it did not match any military pattern that I was aware of, although it would be functionally equivalent to foot artillery swords of the mid-19th century used by France and the U.S. Typical artillery swords were based on the ancient Roman gladius and, compared to this sword, artillery swords have a somewhat thicker blade and a solid brass hilt. This sword has something of the dimension of a hunting sword or "hanger" and so I originally speculated that it might have been sold as a hunting sword. The edges have been sharpened, so it certainly was intended to be functional rather than strictly ceremonial, and the otherwise plain appearance is consistent with that. However, I subsequently learned that it is a Military Administration short sword (Glaive d'Administration), most likely of the "Second Empire" of Napoleon III. Swords were often intended to be signs of authority, their swords associated with postal or railroad officials. Frequently though, such swords are very ornamental and not so plain and functional as this piece. That fact turns the image around from awarding a bureaucrat a decorative bit of foppery to giving a military functionary something they might possibly use to defend themselves, or fall upon, should a German army show up.
