1914

Sublime and at the same time ridiculous, the U.S. Model 1913 saber was adopted at least 50 or more years too late to have been a useful weapon. The design, promoted by Second Lieutenant (later General) George Patton, was inspired by the French philosophy that the point was more important to the cavalry weapon than the edge, as exemplified by the French Model 1896 Cavalry saber. This idea was also accepted by the English and clearly influenced the highly refined British Pattern 1908 Cavalry saber. The Patton and English swords had similar hilts and balance but the Patton sword differed from the English by being double edged and therefore less of a 100% thrusting sword.

The concept for this sort of sword is that it would convert the limb of the charging horseman into a sort-of lance, a weapon for converting all of that kinetic energy into a thrust with monumental impact.

Could this sort of attack ever been successful? It certainly was not a good idea in World War I, when, if the terrain ever permitted such an attack, the riders would have been open to accurate fire from repeating rifles (or worse, machine guns) for hundreds of yards. The formation of infantry squares as a defense against charging cavalry is a tactic dating back to at least the Romans and was very effectively used during the Napoleonic war, with the addition of bayonets and volley fire. What were the proponents of cavalry tactics in the early 20th century thinking?

The U.S. came late to World War I, with the American expeditionary force shipping to Europe in 1917. Patton swords made prior to that date might have made it to Europe but there is no report that they were ever used in battle.

The Springfield Armory produced a total of about 35,000 of these swords between 1913 and 1918. Once the U.S. declared war, Landers, Frary, and Clark were also contracted to produce M1913 sabers. The total production of LF&C was about 93,000, but probably not a single one of these swords made it to Europe. By some reports, many of the WWI surplus blades were cut up to make trench knives for soldiers entering WWII. In any case, both the Springfield and the LF&C swords are relatively scarce today and rather valuable. I wonder, how much of that value comes from their association with George C. Scott, or rather, George S. Patton?

This particular example of the U.S. M-1913 Patton Saber was made at the Springfield Armory in 1914. It probably made it to France but never to the front lines.
