Circa 1849

In 1840, the U.S. Army went shopping for a new cavalry saber. They settled on a pattern based on the French Model 1822 saber. It was a relatively heavy sword, with a long (35 inch) curved blade and a swept-hilt hand-guard. Model 1840 sabers were first put in the field in 1843, at a time when the U.S. was switching from flintlocks to percussion arms. Older pistols that were still in service were converted to percussion and a new model percussion pistol was introduced in 1842. These pistols were of course all still single-shot, muzzle-loading guns. A well-equipped cavalry trooper would have a brace of such pistols in saddle holsters. Dragoons (mounted infantry) would have also had musketoons, short-barreled, muzzle-loading, smooth-bore shoulder arms. Equipped with firearms that were impractical to reload on horseback in a melee, cavalry and dragoons alike would soon find themselves relying on their sabers.

The regular army purchased sabers produced by the Ames Company, while state and local militia units purchased arms from other sources. The C.R. Kirschbaum Company of Solingen in Prussia was a major supplier of these supplemental swords. This is a Kirschbaum sword, marked with "K & C." It is quite solid and retains its original grip. The model was nicknamed "Old Wristbreaker," although that name might have only become common after the Model 1860 light cavalry sword was introduced at the beginning of the Civil War.

Many Model 1840 sabers remained in service throughout the Civil War, although by 1860, the use of Colt-style revolvers and breechloading carbines began to have a major impact on the tactical use of cavalry.
