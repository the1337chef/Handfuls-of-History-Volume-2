U.S. Navy Officer's Sword

Circa 1960

The pattern of the U.S. Navy officer's sword was set in 1852 and was basically similar to the Army's 1850 pattern for Staff and Field Officers, but with a smaller blade, a flatter guard, and appropriate nautically themed decorative motifs. Although the Navy regulations ceased to require swords as part of the dress uniform in 1942, brand-new swords of the 1852 pattern are available today from several manufactures for ceremonial purposes and high-ranking officers are still more-or-less expected to own a sword.

As with the Army version, the 1852 Navy sword has an etched blade that is straight and sharpened only at the point. They have a saber-style hilt with wire wrapped ray skin grips, although on newer examples the ray skin is likely to be simulated (i.e. plastic).

With such a long history of use it can be hard to date a sword like this. One indication that this sword is post 1941 is that the eagle on the pommel faces right (from the observer's point of view), which is a nearly universal feature post 1941. The proof mark is in the style of those on Wilkinson swords made for British officers, but that style was adopted by many manufactures, as on the British artillery officer's sword. The "Challenger" trade name was one of many used by the importer/ distributor; N.S. Meyer Inc, New York. According to a senior member of an internet sword forum, the sword was made by Eickhorn, Solingen, Germany in the 1950s. While this sword is one of the most recently made pieces in this collection, it is also the one I have owned the longest. I purchased it from a pawnshop in the neighborhood of the San Diego Navy base circa 1990.
