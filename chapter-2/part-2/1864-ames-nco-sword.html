Union Noncommissioned Officer's Sword

1864

This sword was made in 1864 by the Ames Manufacturing Co. of Chicopee, Massachusetts, one of the largest producers of swords for the Union forces during the Civil War. It most likely was issued to an infantry sergeant who would have carried it with him on campaigns. It would have also been used for drills and ceremonial duties. Similar swords with shorter blades (27 inches rather than 31) were issued to musicians.

Its utility as a weapon would have been for thrusting, since the point is sharp but not the edges. I suppose, as lines converged, presumably with bayonets fixed on both sides, a sergeant fortunate enough to evade enemy bayonets might have taken a stab of his own with such a sword. It would, however, been a rather desperate gesture. The cup guard was well designed for a thrusting blade, as it would allow for a safe pushing forward with force.

The Model 1840 Noncommissioned Officer's Sword was based on earlier European patterns and would have been worn with a baldric across the shoulder held in place by the belt worn around the waist. The manufacturer's marks and the date can be read on the blade but there are no clear inspector's marks on the hilt. There is however the expected A.D.K. inspector's mark on the scabbard drag. With a manufacture date of 1864, this sword might have seen as much as one year of service in the Civil War.
