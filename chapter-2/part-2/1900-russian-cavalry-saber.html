Circa 1900

Beginning with the reign of Peter the Great, Russia asserted itself as one of the great European powers, although the extent of its territory drew Russia into conflicts in Asia as well as Europe. Like most multinational Empires, Russia had exotic auxiliaries in its army, such as the Cossack cavalry recruited from the Ukraine. The Cossacks were known to carry a unique saber know as shashka, which was later adopted by the Soviets. The shashka had a mildly curved blade and lacked a cross guard or hand protection. Instead it had a large curved pommel, often in the form of an eagle's head. Like the pommel on a yatagan or shamshir, this prevented the sword from moving forward out of the hand, rather than preventing the hand from moving forward on the blade, as a normal cross guard would.

Sometimes the term shashka is incorrectly applied to the sabers used by units of the Imperial Russian cavalry who carried swords that were more on the pattern of other European swords, such as the French Model 1822 saber. This is a Model 1881 Russian Imperial Cavalry Sword. The pommel and knuckle guard are plain, indicating that it is a Trooper's model. On an officer's model they would have been more ornate as on the French sword, and the blade on an officer's sword would also have likely been etched.

The Russian Imperial eagle confirms the association of this sword with Russia, but there is relatively little else to go on. The letters struck around the eagle are difficult to make out. The significance of the stamped "0" on the blade is unknown. The sword might have been in service during the Russo-Japanese war or World War I. I discovered the sword in a roadside antique mall while on a family vacation.
