North African S'boula "Zanzibar" Sword

Circa 1880

This sword has a European-style blade and most likely came from one of the Berber tribes of North Africa. Similar swords are found with blades that are clearly of European origin, though often cut down from larger sabers. The fact that the fullers run straight into the hilt of this sword might also suggest that this blade was once longer. Arab influence is evident in the "I" or "H" shaped hilt that resembles that of jambiyas, but also types of Ethiopian swords including the shotel, guarde, and saif.

There is a certain amount of controversy about this style of weapon, which arises from a possible mistake in the literature. In his The Book of the Sword, the 19th-century English explorer Sir Richard Burton identified a sword of this type as coming from Zanzibar, based on an illustration and description from an earlier work published by Auguste Demmin. However, a later scholar, Charles Buttin, noted this as an error and identified the type as an s'boula from Morocco. While most people accept that Burton may have perpetuated an error, the attribution to Zanzibar is still common and in fact was how my friend Artzi advertized the sword.

Anthony Tirri* refers to this sword type as a "jiboula" and says it was used throughout North Africa. However, he shows details of "Moroccan decorative motifs" in the brass scabbard of his example that rather closely resembles this piece. He further states that the origins of this sword type were with a sect of (black) African Jews in Ethiopia, known as the Falashas (also Falas Mora or Beta Israel).

The handle and scabbard are both wood covered with thins sheets of chased brass. As is common with many types of African or Arab weapons, one side is for show and the back is rather more crudely finished. The blade has "eyelash" marks on each side that could have talismanic significance or were meant to imitate European proof marks. Crescent moons were often included in the marks of European makers. However, most often on European swords these marks appear in pairs or with other elaborations.
