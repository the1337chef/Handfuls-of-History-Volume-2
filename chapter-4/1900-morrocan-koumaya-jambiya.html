Circa 1900

The Moroccan Koumaya is one of the most distinctive of the Arab Jambiyas, with a large hilt and slender blade. On some examples, the pommel is very large, giving the weapon and very ungainly appearance to western eyes. Unlike other jambiyas, which were carried with their sheaths stuck into (as with the Kurdish example) or sewn onto (as with the examples from Yemen) a belt, the Moroccan Jambiyas have bosses (rings) onto which a baldric, in the form of a cord, attaches. The pommels and sheaths are usually embossed or engraved in silver, and the grips are wood, horn, or as on this example, ivory.

The relatively modest dimensions of this dagger make it appear as if it could have some practical utility as a blade, while others with enlarged pommels, sometimes compared to peacock tails, must have been almost purely ornamental. In any case, the decorative nature of these pieces was surely meant to communicate status and tribal identity.

The tribal people of Morocco are known as the Berbers who are spread out across all of North Africa west of the Nile River. Although brought into the Muslim world early, they broke away from the Arab Caliphate in 789 as a separate Muslim Empire that dominated the North African Barber states, collectively known as the Maghreb, as well as large sections of Spain for several hundred years. Morocco itself remained independent throughout the 19th century, although other territories in North Africa such as Algeria fell under European influence. However, in 1912 Morocco also fell under European control, divided into a French protectorate in the north and a Spanish one to the south. Spain also controlled the Canary Islands, which were just off the coast of the northern end of Spanish Morocco.

The European intervention in Morocco was supported by Great Britain as a way to prevent Germany from asserting their authority in the region. Morocco regained independent status as an Arab kingdom in 1956.
