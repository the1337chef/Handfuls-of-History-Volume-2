Circa 1930

Uzbekistan is a country largely unknown to people in the west. For much of its history it was absorbed by one empire or another and it is one of the several new nations that emerged from the break-up of the Soviet Union. Imperial Russia brought it into their sphere in the late stages of their great game with Great Britain, as they were looking for paths of conquest into India.

Uzbekistan is not only landlocked, it is bordered by five other landlocked countries: Kazakhstan to the north; Tajikistan to the southeast; Kyrgyzstan to the northeast; Afghanistan to the south; and Turkmenistan to the southwest. Most of the country is dry and desert like. In recent decades the inland Aral Sea, once the fourth-largest inland sea in the world, has shrunk to less than 50 percent its size in the 1960s, largely due to the exploitation of the country's water for agriculture, primarily cotton production, under the Soviets.

The first flowering of Uzbekistan came because of its strategic location on the ancient Silk Road, which allowed the cities of Bukhoro (Bukhara) and Samarqand (Samarkand) to become important centers of commerce and culture. After Islam reached the area with the expansion of the first Caliphates, these cities continued to thrive and as the Islamic world evolved, Uzbekistan came to be at the frontier of the Persian Empire.

The later Mongol conquests were particularly bloody and brutal in this part of the world, with entire cities destroyed and large populations massacred. The region was subsequently ruled by various remnants of the Mongol Empire, such as the "Golden Hoard" and the Timurids, until it was reconquered by Persia in 1510. However, the Persia Empire that returned to Uzbekistan had itself been shaped by its prior conquest by Timur (Tamerlane). Timur, the great-great-great-grandfather of Babur, founder of the Mughal Empire was born in Uzbekistan, and he is still venerated there as a national hero. There is a monument to him in Tashkent.

In the beginning of the 19th century, Uzbekistan consisted of the Emirate of Bukhara and Khanates of Khiva and Kokand. Bukhara was an Emirate because the rulers claimed to trace their lineage to the Prophet Mohamed, while the ancestry of the Khans traced back to Timur. Bukhara fell to Russian troops in 1886, although the Emirs continued to rule in name until 1920 when Bukhara along with the rest of Uzbekistan was absorbed into the Soviet Union. The two khanates suffered similar fates. While certain aspects of traditional Uzbek culture survived, much was lost during the Soviet occupation. The majority religion remains Islam, but the culture is more secular and the practice of Islam is less rigorous than in the Arab world.

The traditional knife of the Uzbeks is the pichaq. It is likely that that this example came from the region of Bukhara. The shape resembles that of the pichangeti, a traditional knife of the Coorg people of Southwest India, but there is almost certainly no connection. Traditionally, three things marked an Uzbek man, a colorful skullcap, an embroidered woven belt, and their pichaq. As with many cultures, these traditional knives were believed to be objects of spiritual power as much as they were weapons. The cloth pouch that came with this knife is also an important piece of Uzbek culture. It was not original to this knife and in fact the two are not the greatest fit together. I showed the hanger to Ginger Clarke who is a textile expert. She said that the middle of the main section of the hanger is needlepoint embroidery, you can see where the needle went through the canvas. The border is chain stitched. The backing is probably linen. The inside is definitely hand spun cotton or more likely linen. The houndstooth check of the larger sheath is knitted, possibly from mohair or another long-hair sheep or goat breed. Some of the thinner parts are mill spun and possibly tablet woven, or a narrow straight-hand weave. The larger sheath is almost certainly newer that the rest of the kit. The original was probably worn or cut through over time with the sheathing of the original knife.

Pichaq is the Uyghur word for knife. The Uyghur are Turkic ethnic group living in Eastern and Central Asia, and are a minority group in Uzbekistan. It is not likely they introduced this style of knife, as the style goes back at least as far as the 1500s. Most likely it is just a "borrowed word." During the period of Soviet occupation, the display and ownership of these knives was outlawed and many older examples like this one were confiscated and destroyed. Since the time of the Soviet break up, there has been a reemergence of the Uzbek knife makers, most notably in the modern town of Chust in the Eastern part of the country. The Soviets built six cotton mills in Chust, which only received City status in 1937.

This Bukharan pichaq came from Oriental Arms, along with a traditional scabbard of colorful embroidered fabric serving as a belt attachment. The scabbard was not original to the knife, but they might easily have been used together. The scabbard had a second compartment for a smaller knife that was also missing. This prompted me to contact my blacksmith friend Steve Bloom to see if he would make me a suitable companion piece. The size and shape of the sleeve meant it could not be a pichaq, which always have blades wider than their handles, so I suggest he make a kard, another common style of knife in Uzbekistan. In my correspondence about this with Artzi from Oriental Arms, he offered to send a couple exemplars of modern Uzbek knives, of the type presently being made in Chust. These are shown along with the Bukharan pichaq. One had scales of white plastic, one of which was broken, so I also asked Steve to repair it. The scales of the other appear to be made of horn. Steve repaired the white handled knife with white linen micarta, basically many sheets of linen impregnated with plastic resin

The handle of the knife Steve made is of Gabon ebony. Steve copied the construction of the pichaq bolster. The piece of ebony he had was not quite large enough to make the same style of bird's head so he carved an owl.
