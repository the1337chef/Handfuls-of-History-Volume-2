Circa 1910

The Islamic world's confrontation with the secular modernism and the neocolonial imperialism of the Europe and other western nations was the catalyst for several different Muslim reform movements. Although some of these reform movements, like that founded by Sir Syed Ahmed Khan in India, were progressive and tolerant of western secularism, others like the Wahabi movement were militant and reactionary. Wahhabism, based on the teachings of Muhammad ibn-Abd-al-Wahab (1703-1792) originated in what is now the nation of Saudi Arabia and since its inception has been supported by the Saudi royal family. This style of Jambiya is most common in Western regions of Arabia, Hijaz, Asir, and Najd, which was the birthplace of Wahhabism.

Wahhabism separated from mainstream Sunni Islam as a fundamentalist reaction to what its leaders felt were departures from true Islam. Its followers still reject other forms of Islam almost as strongly as they reject other faiths. The movement is at the root of the modern Islamic terrorist movements Al-Qaeda and ISIS (Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant). Even before recent emergence of Wahhabism as these extremist movements, the Wahhabis were a powerful minority in Saudi Arabia.

With a 14-inche-long, high-carbon steel blade, this jambiya seems much more like a serious weapon than the jambiyas of Yemen or Oman (as shown in one of the photos). However, with a handle and scabbard of finely chased silver, it also exuds wealth and power. The dagger with its original colored leather belt that was clearly made to go with it, would be difficult to separate from the scabbard. The belt has aged rather poorly, especially around the buckle, which hangs on by just a few threads. When I received the item there was also a problem with the blade, which had developed a fresh bloom of rust shown in some of the pictures. Artzi Yarom, the trusted proprietor of Oriental Arms from whom I have acquired many of the more exotic pieces in this collection, arranged for the problem to be attended to by a professional restorer.

As with most non-military edged weapons, it is difficult to assign a date with any real confidence. The condition of the belt however, suggests this piece is rather old, possibly predating World War I. If that is the case, then this dagger might have been in use during the time of T.E. Lawrence and the "Arab Revolt."
