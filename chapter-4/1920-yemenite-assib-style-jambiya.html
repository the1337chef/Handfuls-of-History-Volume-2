Yemen is one of the earliest settled parts of the Middle East. Ptolemy, described Yemen as Eudaimon Arabia (in Latin translation, Arabia Felix) meaning "fortunate Arabia." Yemen adopted Islam during the lifetime of the prophet, although interestingly, prior to Mohammed the official religion of many Yemeni kingdoms was Judaism.

Various Islamic dynasties contested this "happy" arable part of the Arabian Peninsula. In 1538 Ottoman Turkish armies invaded the country but were expelled from the interior around 1629, and from the coastal areas in 1635 when they were forced to leave. The Ottomans reasserted themselves after 1830 to finally establish Yemen as a territory in 1872 when the Empire was in decline elsewhere. The British exploited the weakness of the Ottoman hold and took the port of Aden as a coaling station in 1839

Nowhere else in the Middle East is the jambiya (janbiya) a more valued cultural icon than in Yemen. There are many varieties of Jambiya still sold in the Souqs of Yemen, although for the run-of-the-mill pieces, quality has been compromised by the use of plastics for the handles, synthetic fabrics for the belts, and blades imported from China. Most tourists would not know the difference, but a native would care and daggers made in the old style are still available for a price.

There are two main types of jambiya associated with Yemen, the assib and the thouma. For the Yemenite man the choice of style is strictly dictated by their social status. An ordinary tribesman would wear an assib, never a thouma, which would be worn by an Islamic cleric or someone else of high social status.

In Yemen the jambiya is worn always attached to a belt, usually made with the scabbard. The name of the dagger presumably comes from the Arabic word "jamb," meaning side. However, the Assib-type jambiya is worn in the front, under the belly. As befits a common tribesman, the Assib jambiya and its associated sheath are usually relatively plain. The scabbard on this example is plain brown leather, they are often green or white with only leather trim as decoration. The angle of the scabbard identifies it as a Hashid-type Assib, as opposed to a Bakeel type, which has a less acute upturn angle. As noted above, in Yemen jambiyas are normally sold along with a belt made for the specific dagger and the belts may show more variety and personality than the daggers themselves. I asked some friends who are textile experts to comment on this belt:

"The belt has a canvas background with embroidery. The canvas background is probably hand woven with hand-spun cotton. The yarns are not as consistent as one would expect with a commercially prepared yarn. Also, the threads appear to be single ply, consistent with handspun cotton but not a commercial yarn from the period. It would have been woven on a backstrap loom in one long narrow strip. There are some holes in the fabric, probably from insects. Since the fabric is cotton, most likely the damage is caused by a beetle of some sort rather than by moths. The silver threads are in all likelihood a linen thread wrapped with a fine flattened silver strand. It is in excellent condition. Because of the blue thread's uniform twist, ply and and overall smoothness, it appears to be commercially prepared wool or linen. It would have been dyed in indigo as commercial blues were not much in use when this was made. Indigo and woad are readily available there and vats of it would be in just about every home where spinning or weaving was produced."

This particular style of Assib Jambiya is most likely from the central region of Yemen, around the cities of Sana'a. It is the type worn by members of the Manasib class. Assibs from the mountainous western coast Asir region are usually of silver construction. The man who owned this would have worn it every day, although it is not likely that it would have been drawn very often, to do so casually would defame it. It would be used as a weapon only under extreme duress. It might however been drawn on ceremonial occasions, such as to dance at a wedding.
