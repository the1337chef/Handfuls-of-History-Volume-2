1870

The homeland of the Georgian people is on the east coast of the Black Sea in Caucasia, the mountainous area separating Europe and the Asian Middle East. In antiquity, the land was known a Colchis. It was here that Jason had to go to find the Golden Fleece. At various times over the past 1200 years the Georgians have had their own nation state, while at other times, they have been ruled by others. They were among the earliest people to accept Christianity, but were hard pressed by the Muslim Ottoman Turk and Safavid Persian Empires, which bordered Georgia to the South. The country was split and divided between the two Empires for much of the period between 1460 and 1700. Relief of a sort from the pressures from the Islamic Empires came in the form of intervention by Russia, which led to conquest and annexation of Georgia by Russia in 1805.

The Russian Empire was in some ways a better fit for Georgia, at least in the sense that they shared similar forms of Christian worship. Georgians were otherwise more culturally compatible with people in the neighboring Russian territories than with the Ottomans. However, the Georgians never really assimilated into the immense Russian Empire of the 19th century, which was very culturally diverse, socially troubled, and not prepared to accept any ethnic minorities as full citizens. With fall of Mother Russia in 1917, Georgian nationalists declared a Democratic republic in 1918. This Georgian Republic lasted only three years before the Red Army invaded and Georgia became one of the "Soviet Republics" in the USSR.

For many hundreds of years one the most iconic weapons of Georgia and the neighboring regions of Russia and Caucasia, was the qama or kindjal. They have clear resemblance to the ancient Roman gladius, although they are generally shorter in length, and often with a more slender profile. Some, especially ones connected to the Russian army, are rather plain. Others, like this one, are very elegant.

This Kindjal was initially identified by the seller as Georgian, and probably from early 20th century. The inside of the fullers of the 13.5-inch blade are engraved to imitate twisted steel structure. The silver handle and scabbard are engraved and highlighted with a technique referred to as "nielloing," through which a black metallic substance, consisting of an amalgam of silver, copper, lead, and sulfur is used to create an ornamental effect and fill in an incised design. There is script within a cartouche on the back of the scabbard. I presumed that it must be Georgian but I could not transliterate it. It was subsequently identified for me as Arabic*. I was told that what is legible appears to read, "Kar Ahmad Ibn..." and some numerals. It means, "Work of Ahmad son of...." The numerals 287 can be read as a date, 1287, or 1870 CE.

The translation was provided by Oliver Pinchot, who suggested that the maker was probably Chechen or Circassian and that it probably came from armors in the city of Tbilisi, the capital and the largest city of Georgia. Tbilisi is now as it has been for several hundred years, very cosmopolitan with people of various ethnicities and both Christians and Muslims of different denominations and a significant Jewish population as well. The craftsman who made this would have used whatever script was native to him unless wanting to please a particular customer, which by the 1800s could have included Russians.

The Chechens and Circassians are other peoples of the North Caucasus. In the 19th century the predominately Muslim Chechens and Circassians were nearly exterminated due to their resistance to Russian conquest, and survivors migrated into the Ottoman Empire and elsewhere. In earlier centuries the Muslims took many slaves from the various people in the Caucasus region. The favored slaves were Circassian women for the harems and boys and young men to fill the ranks of the Egyptian Mamluk soldier slaves and later the Ottoman Janissary warrior elite.
