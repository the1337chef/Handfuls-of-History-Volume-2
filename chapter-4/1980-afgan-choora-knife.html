Afghan Choora Knife

Circa 1980

I don't think this knife is particularly old, just cheaply made. It is in the style of the antique Choora from Afghanistan, and is probably just as useful for its intended purposes as a utility knife. It is a recent "bring back" by a U.S. soldier from Afghanistan. It was probably purchased cheaply over there, and I bought it very cheaply at a gunshow in Florida. I bought it to illustrate the endurance of a style even after the departure of quality. The grip pieces are reputedly camel bone and are ill fit together. The pommel style is similar to the old knife and both have three peg attachments of the grips to the tang, but the rivets have been replaced with nails on the newer knife.

If there ever was a place in the world that has been a logjam between civilizations, that place is Afghanistan. The conquerors who have passed through it, from Alexander to the U.S. Army today, have been transients at best who held their places for a while and then let go, allowing the native peoples to retain control. Indo-Persian is a term used to describe the blending of styles between those two cultures. Afghanistan has been both the medium for that culture diffusion and the barrier that kept the two Empires at a distance.

I look at this knife and wonder about the making of it. Was it made cheaply to be sold as a trinket to an outsider, or made by one poor local in a hurry to be used by another to serve as a tool. I favor the second hypothesis. The blade is the best part of the knife. The crude finish elsewhere might have easily been improved to add value for the tourist trade.
