Kurdish Jambiya

Circa 1800

The jambiya is arguably the most iconic of Arab weapons. However this dagger is not strictly Arab, but rather comes from the Kurdish people who have somehow held onto a cultural identity in their homeland located between Iraq and Iran for hundreds of years. They are sometimes considered an Iranian people but they have their own language and ethnic customs. Part of their ethnic identity is that the tribal jambiya is distinct from that of other peoples living on the Arabian Peninsula. The Kurdish dagger is not so acutely curved as those made in Yemen or Oman. It probably is in fact a more functional weapon than the typical Arab jambiya.

The Kurds go back to at least the medieval period and they probably descend from the ancient people known as the Medes. There existed several Kurdish kingdoms prior to the consolidation Safavidic Persia, one of the so-called Gunpowder Empires that arose after the period of Mongol conquest. However, empires absorb nations, and although the Kurds remain, there has not been an independent Kurdish state for over two thousand years. While it seems unlikely that there will ever be a new Kurdish state, the conditions in the Middle East remain volatile and it is possible that the Western interventionist policies may some day rescue them from their oppressive neighbors. I know very little about "Kurdish ways" (excuse the pun), but I understand that they resist assimilation and still dress in a traditional costume that is more Persian-like than Arab.

The blade of this dagger is wootz, true Damascus steel. It was the first piece of true Damascus in the collection. Close-up shows the "watered steel." Unusually for a Kurdish jambiya, the scabbard contains a companion knife in a compartment on the rear side of the scabbard. Similar attachments for a companion utility knife can be found on various Islamic daggers and swords, as well as Japanese swords and Scottish dirks, but it is very uncommon on a Kurdish dagger. The presence of the knife is something of an admission that the jambiya itself is not a very practical tool to cut your meat or peel a fruit. The decorative rivet bosses are made from old Turkish coins.
