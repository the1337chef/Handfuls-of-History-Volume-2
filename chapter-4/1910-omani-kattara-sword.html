Omani Kattara Sword

Circa 1910

The traditional battle sword of an Omani warrior would have been a saif (sayf). During the 19th century, the kattara replaced the Arab saif as a traditional Omani sword. However, it is unlikely that the kattara was used extensively as a serious weapon. It is now almost entirely associated with traditional forms of ceremonial dancing at weddings and religious events.

The blade of this sword was most likely produced in Europe, although there are no marks to confirm this. The sword is 41 inches in length, with three short fullers on each side of the blade. The blade is double edged, with a rounded tip, and not particularly sharp. The leather-covered grip tapers continuously to the iron pommel and there is no cross guard. In this regard, it is similar to the Mandinka saber but this is probably just a coincidence. The sword fits very tightly into its leather-covered wooden scabbard that has molded rings, but it is otherwise quite plain. The fit is so tight that I am reluctant to push it all the way in, for fear I will not be able to remove it again.

The traditional form of Omani dance and music, known as funun, originated in the Dhofar mountains in southern Oman. The dances are intended to mimic battle, with the men brandishing their jambiyas or kattaras. In the dances with kattaras, men often hold small shields (bucklers) in their left hands so that parrying movements are incorporated in the dance.

The dancing of funun is very much a custom of the common people of Oman. One tourist recounts on the internet how when he went to observe a sword dance ceremony in Oman he was surprised at the large number of empty taxis parked outside the event. He later realized that many of the dancers were in fact the taxi drivers. It seems that it is also common to invite foreign dignitaries to participate in an Omani dance ceremony. Videos can found on the internet of Britain's Princes Charles and Harry, on separate occasions "dancing" with Omani kattaras among Omani men in traditional dress. In 2017, the U.S. President Donald Trump was also invited to join in an Omani dance, however he was among a group rich Sheiks and they did little more than raise and lower ornate saif-type swords. It seems that Mr. Trump is not someone who would dance with taxi drivers.
