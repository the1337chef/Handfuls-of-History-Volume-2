Circa 1940

To the Romans, Yemen was Arabia Felix, the part of the generally inhospitable Arabian peninsula with a more tolerable climate and enough rainfall to make agriculture possible. At the time of the birth of the prophet Mohammed some of the kingdoms in what is now Yemen were Jewish. However, Yemen was swiftly caught up into the Arab Islamic world. At times Yemen was a target for various Imperial ambitions including those of the Ottomans and the dynasties that ruled Egypt, but usually they were able to maintain their independence.

Part of the cultural identity of the men of Yemen is their wearing of jambiyas, a traditional Arab dagger. In Yemen two styles of jambiyas are most common and the choice between them is dictated by social status. Ordinary folk wear the Assib-style jambiya, while aristocrats and ranking clerics wear the Thouma-type jambiya. The actual daggers of these two styles are essentially the same. They have double-edged curved blades and handles usually made of some sort of horn worked into an "I" or hourglass shape. The handles are generally sparsely decorated with perhaps a couple of small silver colored disks or buttons and sometimes freckled with nail heads. What distinguishes the Assib- and the Thouma-type jambiya are the styles of belts and scabbards, which also determine how they will be worn. While the Assib-style jambiya is worn in the front, a Thouma-type jambiya is worn on the right-hand side. The Thouma-style scabbards are not upturned at the end and are decorated with an embossed or carved metal cover, called a tuza. On this example, the tuza is brass, but they are sometimes silver, or on rare pieces, gold. The shape of the scabbard is like that of the Kurdish jambiya but unlike the Yemen thouma, the Kurdish jambiya fills the scabbard. A thouma belt typically also has other metal decorations, coins or coin purses and decorative buckles. These ornaments have various names; the false buckle is an ibzeem, the false belt tip is a siwa, and the floral disks are zaharat.

As with most Arab jambiyas, the surviving custom of wearing a thouma is an important statement of ethnic identity and social status.
