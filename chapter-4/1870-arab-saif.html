Circa 1870

Oman benefited from early opportunities to trade with India and also explored the eastern coast of Africa. In 1698 the Sultan of Oman expelled the Portuguese from their bases on the strategic island of Zanzibar. Trade routes from the interior of Africa brought goods such as ivory and the dark business of slave trade to Zanzibar. The island itself was notable for cultivation of cloves. This Arabian saif, believed to have been made on the island of Zanzibar, was probably brought to Arabia via Oman. The design is similar to nimcha swords associated with Morocco.

Moroccan nimcha swords have similar horse-head shaped grips and four quillions forming the cross guard. On the Moroccan swords, three of the quillions project down, while the fourth bends up to make the knuckle guard. On this type of Saif, two of the downward quillions project around and join to form a D shaped guard, which is not present on the Moroccan swords. Another feature that distinguishes the Zanzibar swords from the Moroccan swords is the way in which the pommel is secured to the blade. On the Moroccan swords this usually with rounded stud. On the Zanzibari Nimcha there is a plate resembling a turtle at top of the pommels.

Swords like this one were commonly used by Arabs in the Southern Arabian Peninsula in countries including Yemen and Oman. This particular sword is believed to have been produced on the island of Zanzibar in the 19th century. The sword was purchased from Artzi Yarom of Oriental Arms, who has sold numerous similar swords over the years. One of the pictures is a composite (included with Artzi's permission) of this sword (upper left) and the 14 other similar swords sold by Oriental Arms. You will note the similarities, but also note that each one is different. Some are clearly rather crudely fashioned, others are quite elegant. This one is in the mid-range. Mostly these swords used imported 19th-century German-made blades which were mostly curved but a few were long straight. They all have this distinctive guard with the "ring." Some swords of this type have Passau and Solingen running wolf stamping. The mark on this blade may in fact be the Solingen running wolf or something intended to be taken for that mark.

It has been speculated that many of these D-guard swords come from a trove of swords found in an abandoned Yemeni armory in the mid 1980s. The association of these with Zanzibar is well established in collector's lore but with no hard proof, although stated as a fact by Anthony Tirri (Islamic Weapons, Maghrib to Moghul). It is known that that Zanzibar was a manufacturing center for edged weapons in the 19th century when the island still had a strong connection to Oman, which makes it reasonable that they might have been made in Zanzibar for the Arab market. If they were brought into Arabia in the late 19th century, they were likely in use during the Arab revolt during World War I as made famous by T.E. Lawrence. The curved blade is consistent with use by a warrior mounted on a horse, or perhaps a camel.
