The jambiya is the most iconic of all Islamic weapons, always with a curved double-edged blade and usually an hourglass or "I" shaped handle, often made of horn. This example is from the Mesopotamian region that is now known as Iraq. However, the nation state of Iraq is something of European fantasy turned into a bad dream. It is one of the many nations that came out of the break up of the 19th-century empires. After the end of World War I, the victorious allies felt it was their prerogative to redraw the maps of the Middle East over what had been the former domains of the Ottoman Empire. The allies had encouraged Arab tribes in their efforts to revolt against the Turks, with the promise supporting their independence after the war. However, the Europeans, Britain and France in particular, ultimately chose to put their own interests above those of the Arab people, especially when they realized how important Mideast oil reserves would become. All through the war years, when the Arabs thought they were fighting for their independence, they were under the shadow of the secret Sykes-Picot agreement that promised Syria to France and Iraq and Jordan to Britain.

The most powerful Arab clans fighting the revolt during the war were led by the Hashemite Sharifs, who had been the hereditary rulers of Mecca and the region known as of the Hejaz since the 11th century. The patriarch of the Hashemite family was Sultan Abdul Hamid II. Hamid's sons, Abdullah and Faisal were allowed by the British to claim the thrones of Jordan and Iraq, respectively in 1921. In 1924, Hamid's eldest son, Ali succeeded to the throne of Hejaz, but he was overthrown by the forces of Ibn Saud in 1925 and the holy cities of Mecca and Medina became part of Saudi Arabia. The Saudi were followers of the radical fundamentalist form of Islam known as Wahhabism.

Faisal ruled under the British mandate until 1932 when Iraq was declared independent. However, the monarchy was destabilized by a sequence of military coups beginning in 1936 and the Hashemite monarchy in Iraq officially ended in 1958. The dynasty founded by Abdullah still rules Jordan.

This Jambiya comes from the Basra area of southern Iraq, probably dating to the Hashemite period. The curved blade is, of course, double-edged with a shallow central rib. The hilt is attractively carved from a solid piece of horn with elegant down-curving corners on the pommel.
