1834

Bichaq, basically the Turkish word for knife, is applied to small daggers that are similar to Persian kards. They are basically nicely sized functional knives that might have been carried for self defense or to slice your meat at dinner. They were common throughout the Ottoman Empire with small variations in design based on the region of origin. Bosnian examples often have bone or horn grips. This one, most likely from Turkey proper, is sheathed in silver, although there may be wood in the core of the grip. One edge of the bolster is damaged and there may or may not be a silver panel missing on the top, from the between the two side plates. However, the side plates themselves and the front of the grip are nicely edged so it not clear that anything is really missing. It may just be that the age is showing. The wood scabbard, covered with faded red fabric and white metal mounts appears to have been made to go with the knife. As is often the case with these types of knives, the pommel has the appearance of a miniature yatagan.

The blade on this is bichaq is nicely marked. On one side there is a maker's mark, which is symbolic, almost Tolkienesque, with a tree and stars, rather than translatable. On the other side there is a date, 1250 AH (1834 CE). So, while not made at the height of the Ottoman Empire it was made before its most profound decrepitude.
