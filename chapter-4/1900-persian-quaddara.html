Circa 1900

This sword is a Persian quaddara. Similar swords are found in other central Asian countries, especially those in the Caucasus Mountains. In some ways the quaddara resembles the qama/ kindjals common in the same region, but kindjals usually have relatively symmetrical double-edged blades, while this sword has a massive asymmetric 25-inch single edge blade with a 7-inch false edge on the back. Quaddara also bear a resemblance to the Russian bebut swords, although the mass-produced Russian military-issue items are significantly smaller with double-edged blades.

The grips of this sword are black buffalo horn mounted with rivets over old silver coins. The scabbard has restored leather with the original chased silver collar. The leather of the scabbard is built up into a ridge under the collar that would prevent the scabbard from slipping out of a frog or hanger. The blade is fullered with fine deep maker marks. The same marks appear on a sword in plate 206 of Kirill Rivkin's book, Arms and Armor of Caucasus (page 295). Although Mr. Rivkin does not offer any further information in his book, I was able to contact him by e-mail. He suggests that the mark says "Mohammed," though in a very abbreviated manner, and is not to his knowledge traceable to any specific person. He further remarked that these stamps, apparently, were very seldom changed. One finds the same names and same exact marks over many years, so probably a stamp could have been used until it wore out. Some are also purely decorative, without even a discernible name, but that was not common. Stamps in this form appear to have been invented in Georgia around 1800, but were also used in Iran. He also confirmed that the mounts are definitely Persian. Since there are no records of the actual blade smiths, guesses regarding the origin of the sword are always based on where the mounts come from, so it can be assumed that the blade should be Iranian as well.

This sword is from the period of the Persian Qajar Empire, which lasted from 1796 to 1925. The sword bearing lion and rising sun motif on the collar of the scabbard confirm the association with Imperial Persia (Iran). The script below the lion includes the name of Allah. The Qajars, were descendents of Oghuz Turks who supported the earlier Safavid rulers of Persia. After the fall of the Safavid Empire to the Ottomans and Russians in 1724, the Treaty of Constantinople ceded Georgia and many other Persian territories in the Caucuses to Russia. In the years that followed the fall of the Safavids, the heart of Persia was in chaos, frequently raided by Turcoman and other tribes from the region now known as Afghanistan. In 1779, Mohammad Khan Qajar raised an army that consisted mainly of Turcoman and Georgian slaves, and reunified Iran under the banner of a new empire. It was a brutal campaign of conquest that left in its wake destroyed cities and massacred populations. Mohammad Khan was able to recover Georgia and some of the other territories that had lost to Russia, and was subsequently installed as Shah.

By 1804 however, Russia under Czar Paul I had revived its interests in Georgia and the other Khanates of the Caucuses, leading to the first of two 19th-century Russo Persian wars (1804-1813) and the loss of most of the territories recovered by Mohammad Khan back to Russia. In 1826, thirteen years after the treaty of Treaty of Gulistan ended the first Russo Persian war, the reigning Iranian Shah, Fath 'Ali Shah, led on by empty promises of British support, attempted to regain the lost territories, instigating another war with Russia (1826-1828). The defeat of Persia in this second war ultimately resulted in further loss of Persian territories in present-day Armenia, Azerbaijan, and Igdir Provinces.

By the end of the second nineteenth century Russo Persian war, the Great Game between Russia and Great Britain had begun in earnest, as Russia set their designs on territories between the Caspian Sea and Afghanistan. Britain saw these territories as essential for the defense of their colony of India. A Persia attempt to assert control over the city of Herat in this region led to a short Anglo Persian war (November 1, 1856 to April 4, 1857) and a further loss of prestige for Qajar Iran.

Many of the artifacts of the Qajar era make a show of Persian heritage and Imperial status, but the Qajar rulers of Iran also realized the importance of modernization for their military and industry. Unfortunately, Iran had been almost bankrupt by its wars against the European powers. While the Qajar emperors tried to limit foreign control of domestic affairs they had a strong need for foreign trade and investments, and by the end of the nineteenth century, the Qajar Emperors had all but lost control of their country. Internally, constitutionalists and the growing power of the Shia religious establishment challenged the power of the Shahs, while at the same time the Empire was in crushing debt to foreign bankers and governments, Russia in particular.

Although the title of Shah was carried by members of the Qajar dynasty up until 1921, when Reza Shah Pahlavi, an erstwhile general, orchestrated a coup, the Qajar Empire effectively ended in 1906 when Mozaffar ad-Din Shah Qajar was forced to accept a constitution. The next year, Iranian hopes of independence from European influences ended when the Anglo-Russian Agreement of 1907 divided Persia into separate Russian and English spheres of influence.

This sword is a type used by Persian infantry. The coins that decorate the rivets that hold the scales of the handle in place allow it to be roughly dated to the period of Qajar decline. The coins on the front side of the grip are Russian, of two different denominations, 20 and 15 Kopeks. Some letters of the word kopek can be made out on the facing side of the smaller Russian coin. Imperial Russian two-headed eagles are hidden on the other sides.

The coins on the back are Persian 500 dinar coins*. The facing sides have the name of the ruling Shah in Farsi script. Close comparisons of a coin obtained from eBay and those placed on the sword suggest that these are from the reign of Naser al-Din Shah Qajar, (September 1848 to May 1896). The hidden sides of these Persian coins would have the sigil of Imperial Persia, a lion wielding a sword before a rising sun, as on the coin from eBay. Unfortunately, the eBay coin, although sold as showing only "normal wear", was in fact converted into a button or bangle with the soldering on of a loop on the backside. The remnants of the loop obscure the Imperial lion on the 500 dinar coin, so for comparison, I show the backside of a somewhat later 2000 dinar coin.

The successor of Naser al-Din was Mozaffar ad-Din Shah Qajar who ruled until the time of the constitutional monarchy. Mozaffar ad-Din died of a heart attack just 40 days after signing the constitution and seven months prior to the declaration of the Anglo-Russia accord that carved up Iran into European spheres of influence.

The 2000 dinar coin is from the time of Ahmad Shah Qajar, the last Shah of the Qajar dynasty (1909-1921), and a picture compares the Persian script of the 2000 dinar coin with that on the 500 dinar coin (enlarged). The name of Ahmad in Farsi script is subtly different that of his grandfather.

We can place the Persian coins as being minted before 1896, and the two Russia coins are of a type that was made from about 1860 to 1910. Since the mounting of the Russian coins makes it impossible to read their actual dates, we cannot use them to help post-date this sword with much accuracy. In all though, the coins are consistent with a date around 1900. This was a time when Gertrude Bell, the remarkable woman who was instrumental in drawing the map of the Middle East after World War I, had her first adventures in the east.
