North African Flyssa

Circa 1840

The flyssa is a highly distinctive sword, often attributed to Morocco but more correctly attributed to the Berber Kabyle tribe known as the Iflissen Lebhar, from whom the name is derived. The Kabyle were native to North Western Algeria including an area bordering with Morocco. Many of the Kabyle dispersed to other areas of North Africa after the French conquest and colonizationof Algeria in 1830.

There are some questions about the origins of this style of sword. The oldest surviving examples pre-date the French invasion of Algeria and production of weapons in the native towns and villages declined sharply thereafter. Some attribute inspiration for the style to the Ottoman Yataghan, but I can't really see the justification for that. It is also hard to say what style of fighting for which it would be best suited.

Swords of this type come with blades varying in length from 12 up to 40 inches. They always have a sharp point, which has been suggested to be for piercing mail armor, but the tip is not reinforced, as on Indian weapons like the khanjar and katar made for the same purpose. It seems likely to me that such a slender tip would be liable to snap off if at all deflected when thrust into armor. Clearly it is a thrusting weapon nonetheless, but with no cross guard, it might be ill advised to deliver a forward blow with very much force. Flyssa blades do widen in the belly, so they might also be used as slashing weapons, but at least with the longer weapons the balance is terrible.

As is typical for a flyssa, the blade of this sword is inlaid with brass decoration. The hilt is a combination of steel and wood covered with engraved brass in a stylized animal head, usually assumed to represent a camel.

The Berber Kabyle tribes put up great resistance to their would-be French conquerors, persisting in open rebellion until 1871. Fifty years later, Kabyle peoples were active in the National Liberation Front, which ultimately led to Algerian Independence in 1962. Even today the Kabyle are not fully integrated in to modern Arab-dominated state of Algeria.

The carved wooden scabbard has two channels cut through, where a belt or baldric could pass, to help carry the sword. In the few historical portraits of Kabyle men I have been able to find, the men are shown armed, usually with both a musket and a flyssa. However, in these pictures, the flyssa was shown just stuck through the belt, riding high and at a shallow angle necessary for carrying such a long sword. The pictures did not make it appear as if cords or straps were mounted through the slots.

With such a long blade, this sword is awkward to draw from its scabbard and even more so to re-sheath, requiring the full stretch of my arms. I cannot imagine trying to draw it gracefully if were attached mid-hilt in a belt around my waist. I can almost imagine it suspended by cords from a camel saddle and drawing it from there, but I have no evidence for it being used this way and it is so frail that it probably would not be practical either.

This sword is really in excellent condition and in fact shows no real signs of hard use as a weapon, confirming perhaps its fundamental impracticality as such.
