Hadramaut Gusbi Jambiya

Circa 1945

This jambiya is from Hadramaut, the arid coastal area in the south of Yemen along the Arabian Sea. The style is characterized by a sharply up-curving scabbard tip, with the chape (scabbard tip) sometimes higher than the pommel. Usually the wood scabbard is covered with leather and mounted with a white metal locket (collar). The locket and chape are often set with red colored glass or, as on this example, with carnelian stones. The chape is also decorated with beads of red coral, although one is missing. The hilt appears to be rhino horn with silver fittings.

Most collectors would identify this as a Gusbi jambiya, however in the book "Jambiya" Stephen Gracie states that the term "Gusbi" refers to a "blade with a thickened mid-section and central ridge, typical of jambiyas from Hadramaut." There is a central ridge to the blade of this dagger, as there is on other jambiyas made in Oman and Yemen, such as the Thouma, Assib, and Asir styles. However, none of these jambiyas made in the 20th century stand out as having high quality blades, especially compared to the older Kurdish jambiya with its wootz blade. Rather, these jambiya styles are distinguished by the scabbards, hilts, and, in some cases, belts. Therefore, it is not surprising that the term Gusbi has come to refer in most cases to the complete jambiya and not just the blade.
