Assib Jambiya from the Asir Region of Yemen

Circa 1940

This assib jambiya is from the Asir region of North West Yemen. Asir means "difficult" in Arabic, referring to the rugged coastal mountains that make the region almost inaccessible from the gulf side. On the inland side, the mountain slopes are gentler and the land is fertile. The fertile soil and a relatively moderate climate allow the Arabs of this region to be agriculturalists rather than nomadic raiders, as they are in other parts of the Yemen.

While the assib jambiyas of central Yemen are rather plain, typically with leather scabbards, even the common man's dagger from the Asir region is likely to have a silver covered scabbard and handle, usually with fine filigree work. The blades however are simple polished steel and almost indistinguishable from those made in central Yemen, and, in fact, are likely to be products of the same blade smiths. The large and impressive scabbards are far larger that required to sheath the modest blades. In some ways the jambiyas from the Asir region resemble the so-called Mecca-style jambiyas, however those associated with Mecca are usually less decorated with filigree and would not have the silver points on the pommel.

This dagger came with one (out of four original) decorative dangles on the scabbard tip. My friend, Bernie Reller, used the one original to cast additional replicas. His replacement pieces, identical in form and nicely antiqued, are indistinguishable from the original, except that the split loops attaching them are clearly newer.
