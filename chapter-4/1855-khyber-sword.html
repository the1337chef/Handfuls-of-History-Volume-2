Circa 1855

For centuries the Afridi and other Pashtun clans, including the Afghan Shinwaris people have lived in the Khyber region that was the ancient border between the Safavid and Mughal empires. They controlled the pass through the mountains and extracted tolls from all who wished to pass. The Mughal made numerous unsuccessful attempts to overwhelm them. For a time the Sikhs under Ranjit Singh captured the Khyber Pass and the British also made several attempts with varying success through the 19th and early 20th centuries. Although the British sometimes prevailed against the natives their successes were always temporary. The pass, which is on the modern border between Afghanistan and Pakistan, remains of great strategic importance to the U.S. and NATO.

There is some uncertainty about the ethnic origins of the Pashtun clans of the Khyber region, although their culture has evidence of both Persian and Indian influences. They speak Pashto, which is classified as an Eastern Iranian language, and they are primarily Shi Muslim.

This is an example of a classic khyber sword of mid nineteenth century. The design is similar to that of a Persian pesh-kabz, a knife designed to penetrate chain mail and other types of armor. The khyber weapons, however, are much longer than the Persian daggers, making them effective as short swords. This sword has the typical original leather covered scabbard and the sheathed weapon would have been carried just inserted in a belt or sash without any accessories. The blade is single edged with a reinforcing T-spine and the grips are ivory. The blade is finely forged from Indian Wootz (true Damascus) steel.
