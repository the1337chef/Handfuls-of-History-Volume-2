Black Sea Yataghan or Laz Bichaq

1877

There is considerable debate regarding the origin of this type of sword. One noteworthy expert, Anthony Tirri, attributes its origin to Egypt, but there is widely held opinion that they come from the Black Sea edges of the Ottoman Empire and, in particular, from a people known as the Laz. Lazistan, which is located on Southeastern edge of the Black Sea, was the site of the ancient Kingdom of Colchis where Jason went to seek the Golden Fleece. The Laz have a distinct spoken language but virtually no written history. They converted to Christianity during the Byzantine period and then to Sunni Islam under the Ottomans.

This type of sword is known both as Black Sea yataghan and as a Laz bichaq, neither of which names are entirely suitable because in notable ways it resembles neither a bichaq, nor a typical Turkish yataghan. The term "bichaq" is usually applied to a style of Turkish knife (not a sword) with a double lobed pommel, an ornate grip, and scabbard, but a relatively short and simple blade. "Yataghan" usually refers to a style of Turkish sword, also commonly with a double lobed pommel and no cross guard, but with a very different style of blade. A yataghan blade typically has a gentle uniform curve, but unlike most sabers and oriental shamshirs, the cutting edge is the inside (concave) rather than the outside edge. It is the outside edge of this strongly recurved blade that is the cutting surface. While the pommel of the horn handle is bifurcated, as shown in the photo comparisons to the Turkish yataghan, the style is quite distinct. Also, while the Turkish Yataghan has cast silver bolsters and gold inlays, the decorations on this sword are limited to patterns of punched dots that took no particular skill to apply. An Islamic date of 1294 has also been applied with punched dots, which corresponds to 1877 in the Gregorian calendar.

Swords of this type are rare but yet are all of a distinctive style in regard to the recurved blade and unusual pommel. The shape of the pommel and lack of a cross guard indicate that they are "cut" rather than "thrust" weapons, such that the hand of the wielder would be drawn back, rather than pushed forward when they were used. These swords sometimes show up with a characteristically odd scabbard, resembling a sort of leather peapod to accommodate the recurved blade.

If regular Ottoman conscripts used these swords, it is likely they would be better known, appear in period photographs, and be more easily identifiable. Since they are not common in photographs of the period, the idea that they came from the edges of the Empire, or elsewhere even more remote, seems reasonable. The date confirms an origin with an Islamic people, but the lack of proper Arabic or Persian inscriptions supports the idea that it came from a people living on the fringe. Other areas of the Caucasus have been proposed as a site of origin for these swords, as well as even more distant locations such as Kurdistan and Malaya. However, each of these people and places has their own associations with distinct weapons, so that may be reasonable to ascribe this to the largely unknown people of Laz. On the other hand, there are features of this sword, such as its simple ornamentation, that also make the North African origin suggested by Tirri seem at least equally plausible. The recurved blade is somewhat reminiscent of the Abyssinian shotel, but the overall quality is significantly better than is typically seen in weapons from the interior of Africa, and closer to that of North African weapons like the Algerian flyssa. We are left with more speculations than conclusions about this interesting weapon.
