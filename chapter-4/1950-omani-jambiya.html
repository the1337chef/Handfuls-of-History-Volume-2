The early Arab-dominated Islamic world was not a strong unified Empire, although it did initially recognize a single Caliph ruling from Mecca, there were in reality many small dominions ruled by sultans that were often at war with one another. The divisions were political but also based on the sectarianism of early Islam. Oman on the very northeast corner of the Arabian Peninsula adopted Islam in the 7th century, during the lifetime of the prophet Muhammad. However, they ended up adopting an unusual variant known as Ibadism. The Ibadi movement was founded 20 years after the death of the Muslim prophet Muhammad, predating the schism between the Sunni and Shia denominations, which occurred after the death of the third caliph. The Ibadis were among the more moderate groups and opposed both sides of the fundamental rift. Oman remains the only country with a majority Ibadi population.

Oman, while part of the Arab world, was able to maintain a certain degree of autonomy due to both its geographic and sectarian separation. However, while Oman was off the direct path of invaders such as the Mongols, and removed from the conflict with the European crusaders, the position of Oman across the gulf from Persia, accessible by sea on the east, presented Oman with its own opportunities and challenges especially once Europeans reached the Indian Ocean in the 1500s.

As with other Arab cultures, a jambiya is part of the native dress in Oman. In Oman the knife also frequently referred to as a khanjar, a more generic term for a dagger. Although in many ways similar to the jambiyas of other countries such as Yemen, the Omani style dagger is unique and identifiable. The scabbard of an Omani Jambiya is "L" shaped, bent at 90 degrees, in-between the angle of the two types of jambiyas from Yemen, bent at more of an angle than the thouma and less of an angle than the assib. The other most distinctive feature of the Omani style jambiya is the belt attachment, made of complicate arrangements of silver rings and twisted silver wire. However, as with other styles of jambiyas, although we can identify Oman as the likely origin of this jambiya, similar daggers will be found all over the Arabian Peninsula. jambiyas of the Saudi royal family for example, are typically of the Omani style, but fitted with gold rather than silver.

This particular piece has a well-forged eight-inch blade with a pronounced central rib. The hilt has a central core of horn in a classical "I" shape with the front face and pommel sides decorated with silver and a solid silver backside. The scabbard and hilt fittings are chased silver. This was also made with a belt of silver foil, backed with black velvet and mounted with 1943- 1945 silver coins from India. Oman had long been in a position to trade with India and rest of the east.

It is an impressive piece, representing both a traditional style and the social status and wealth of its original owner. The blade is sharp, but it is unlikely that this was ever used as a weapon.
