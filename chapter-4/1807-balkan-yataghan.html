Balkan Yataghan

1807

The yataghan was a common sword in the northern territories of Ottoman Empire. It has a forward curving single edge, like the Indian soussan pata (shown in some of the pictures), but with a distinctive pommel and no cross guard. Since there is no cross guard, the rather large-eared pommel serves to keep the users hand from sliding back off the grip. This design feature reflects the intended use of the blade as a slashing, rather than thrusting weapon, the blade is intended to be pulled back rather than pushed forward. The particularly wide ears on this sword are consistent with a Balkan rather than strictly Turkish origin. Prior to the 20th century, the army of the Ottoman Empire was drawn from its various provinces and so its varied soldiers would carry the weapons of their various homelands.

The town of Yatağan in southwest Turkey was famous for its yataghan smithing, and in folklore it is considered to be the birthplace of yataghans. The Arabic inscriptions of inlaid silver indicate it was made by Muhammad Sahib Ali Basha in the year 1222 (1807 in the Gregorian calendar).
