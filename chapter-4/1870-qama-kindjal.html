Circa 1870

This qama (kindjal) dagger is most probably Georgian, from the late 19th Century. Although the qama is the national sword of Georgia, numerous people of the Caucuses, northern Turkey, Persia, and most notably, Russia, use similar or identical swords. Qama is the name used for this weapon in Georgia, Iran, and Turkey, while kindjal is the name used in Russia, Armenia, and Daghestan. The use of this type of sword by Cossacks was once so wide spread that they came to be known as Cossack swords, an attribution which did not please the Georgians. They have the basic form of the ancient Roman gladius, being relatively short double-edged stabbing swords. However, they vary greatly the details of their fit and finish. Some kindjals are fitted out with profusely engraved silver, or even gold, grips and scabbards. Others, like this example, are relatively plain with horn handles and leather scabbards. Examples can be found that are anywhere between these extremes. On this example, the 15.5-inch blade has typical off-center fullers. The otherwise plain black horn handle has nielloed silver rivet heads that match the throat band and finial on the scabbard, the leather of which has been renewed.

The lack of any maker's mark or other inscription makes it essentially impossible to date this sword with any accuracy or to place it geographically more specifically than to Georgia, and as noted above, even that is speculative. Although having an ancient history as an independent nation, since the 16th century Georgia has been an anvil between the imperial hammers of Turkey and Russia, and not far from the reach of Persia. The spirit of nationalism has nonetheless remained strong in Georgia and when the opportunity presented, they have reasserted their independence as they did after World War I and the dissolution of the Soviet Union.
