Circa 1820

There has been a Shah, Khan, Emir, Emperor, King, President, Dictator, or Ayatollah in Kabul at most times over the last 2,000 years. Sometimes, the ruler of Kabul could claim to have power over a significant empire, and at other times they were more akin to a local warlord. Prior to 1747, the person on the throne in Kabul could not even lay claim to being an Afghan, since the place we know now as the land of the Afghans (Afghanistan) has historically been on the path of numerous conquering armies. Rulers in Kabul have been Persian, Mongol, Turkoman, any of a number of local peoples, or, for a brief period of history, Macedonian Greek. Descendents of the Mongol conquers of Afghanistan invaded India, initially for plunder, and later to establish first the Deli Sultanate and then the Mughal Empire.

In 1747, Ahmad Shah Durrani, commanding an army of 4,000 Abdali Afghans, took control of Kabul. This marked the beginning of Pashtun rule and an Afghan Empire, with at first dual capitals in Kandahar and Kabul. In 1776, Kabul became the sole capital of the Afghan state. However, ever since its founding, the Afghan nation has been beset by local rivals and would-be foreign dominators. The nation found itself squarely in the middle ground of the "great game" played out between Russia and Great Britain in the 19th century, which still resonated through the 20th century with first a Soviet Russian invasion and later U.S. military intervention against rising militant Islam.

The tribes that controlled the mountain passes of Afghanistan fought with Khyber knives and lohars. However, the iconic sword of the Afghan nobility was the pulwar (paluoar), a style of sword derived from Indian tulwars, particularly those used in northern India. A pulwar can be distinguished from a typical Indian tulwar by the downward-curving quillions ending with tassels shaped finials, and pommels which have either a cup below the tulwar-style disk or are semispherical turban shaped (as on the great game pulwar).

This is a well-constructed and substantial sword. It is likely that the blade was made in Persia for export. The wrap-around channeling with partitioned fullers is frequently seen on Afghan swords. In some extraordinary examples the channels are deeper and made so that they can hold a free-rolling bead. These beads are referred to as "tears of the wounded." The finials in the form of tassels at the end of the quillions are a very consistent feature on Afghan pulwars, although I do not know the origin or significance of this feature. It has been suggested by Davis Alexander (Islamic Arms and Armor in The Metropolitan Museum of Art) that they may be intended to represent pierced and lobed palmette tips. Overall, the incised embellishments on this sword are nice, clearly done by hand, but purely decorative without figurative art or dedication to Allah.

This is a serious sword from a very serious time in the history of the world. Afghanistan was a reasonably strong state in the early 1800s, defensible largely by virtue of its isolated location surrounded by mountains and desserts. However, it was also surrounded by hostile neighbors and, as noted above, was the target of designs by distant powers. While Russia never advanced as far as Afghanistan in the nineteenth century, Great Britain, fearing for the safety of their Empire in India, fought three Anglo-Afghan wars between 1839 and 1919. In the first of these wars, most likely contemporaneous to this sword, Britain was soundly defeated and embarrassed by the son of Dost Mohammed the Emir of Afghanistan who the English deposed in favor of their own unworthy candidate. The English suffered over 16,000 casualties, to the Afghan's mere 500 and Dost Mohammed was restored to the throne. The game played on and war erupted again in 1878, this time with a more favorable outcome for the English, and arguably not a bad outcome for the Afghans as well.
