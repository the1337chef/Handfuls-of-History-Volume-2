Circa 1960

While the Jambiya is the most iconic Arab dagger, its use is not universal. The Bedouin men of the tribes residing in Israel, Palestine and Jordan carry a smaller, rather less ostentatious, dagger known as a shibriya. The blade of a shibriya is not curved like that of a jambiya, but has a sort of bent appearance. They often look flashy but are rather cheaply made. Reportedly, the name shibriya is in reference to the relatively small size of these daggers. "Shibr" is a unit of length referring to span from the tips of the index finger and the thumb. The smaller of these two examples, with a four-inch blade, is rather smaller than the norm. The larger example, with a six-inch blade, is more typical. Perhaps the smaller one was intended for a boy.

The Bedouins of Palestine and Jordan who maintain the old nomadic lifestyle have few possessions. A simple useful knife probably suits their needs. The blades on these knives are double edged with an acute angle toward the tip. The smaller one is signed, "Made by Sayed Farhood." I am told that the inscription on the larger on reads "God is the greatest. Made by Alaa Mohaisen and his Sons, 1961, Amman."

The grips and the scabbards are covered by some sort of decorated white metal on the front side and set with colored stones that are probably just glass. These knives look good from the front, but the backsides of the knives and the scabbards are simple iron or copper sheet, making it clear that they are a poor men's knives.
