Circa 380 BCE

While not strictly "handfuls," especially absent the bow and the rest of the arrow, arrowheads represent one of the most important and enduring artifacts of human weapon history. They can be traced back further and to more diverse cultures than nearly any other artifact, whether they were made of stone, flint, bone, copper, iron, or, as in this example, bronze. Bronze, which is an alloy of copper and tin, was first discovered and subsequently widely used in ancient Europe and Asia sometime before 3000 BCE. It is malleable, but hard enough to be sharpened and to hold an edge-at least for a critical period of time. It was a very suitable material to use for arrowheads that were not going to come up against armor made of iron. This arrowhead was excavated in Israel and is of Scythian origin. Later arrowheads, such as those from the crusader era (1100-1300), were made of iron and did not survive well. Bronze will corrode on the surface but such corrosion can effectively seal the metal from further, more invasive, corrosion. The same is not usually true for iron.

The Scythians were a nomadic equestrian people who spread from the steppes of the Caucasus Mountains into the Balkans and the Near East beginning in about the 7th century BCE. The Scythian kingdom was among those that fell in the formation of Alexander the Great's short-lived empire.
