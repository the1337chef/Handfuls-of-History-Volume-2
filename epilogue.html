Epilogue

September 2017: Work on this book began in earnest after I finished the fourth edition of the first volume of Handfuls of History in March of 2015. The collecting began much earlier and probably can be credited to my enthusiasm for Medieval and Renaissance fairs. I have several nice swords that are modern reproductions of Medieval or Renaissance pieces and do not appear in this book. Of the swords that does appear in this book, I think the one I have had the longest, is the U.S. Naval Officer’s Sword that I actually bought to pass off with a Renaissance costume. After I had been drawn into collecting antique firearms for their historical significance, while I was working on Volume 1, it was pretty natural to also be attracted historical edged weapons. Of course, I had assembled a nice collection of bayonets in the process of collecting the rifles in Volume 1.

I was fascinated by the story behind “true Damascus” steel (wootz), which I learned from my friend Steve Bloom, and I wanted some examples. I already had a nice collection of knives made from pattern-welded steel, several of which were made by Steve and a couple of which appear in the early part of this book. It was in my search for wootz that I first came upon the Oriental Arms website of Artzi Yarom, who I am now pleased to call my friend. The first two pieces of antique wootz I bought from Artzi were the Kurdish Jambiya and the Soussan Pata.

By March of 2015, I had already accumulated over 130 of the pieces that appear in this book that I knew I would be writing. I had actually begun photographing them two years earlier.

One thing that drew me into the writing of the first volume was a fascination with the interplay between political events (wars), and the development of firearms technology. Most importantly though, was how these things affected the individuals who were the first persons to have held those pieces of history in their hands. Of course guns have serial numbers, and although there were few cases where I could trace histories to particular people, it was, in general, easy to get fairly accurate dates and reasonable assessments of manufacturer, country of origin and “political events.” Nearly all the dates in this book, outside of Chapter 2 on European and American items, have to be considered as very approximate.

The writing of this book has been a valuable experience, as it has taken me to places in the world I knew relatively little about and challenged me to educate myself on the cultures of many different peoples. I would be brought into literature and histories that covered Arab Empires, the complex histories of central Asia and the tribal cultures of Africa and the Asian Islands, and the amazing and complex history of India. I am particularly indebted to the Dayak of Borneo who carved the mandau in the collection; since I found that image so fascinating that I wanted to explore the psychoactivity of betel nut, a topic on which my lab has now made several discoveries.

As I close this book, I wonder what will be next for me. I certainly have reading to catch up on, and I suppose there is a chance for a second edition. You might like to imagine that all of the items in this book are nicely on display, as if in a museum. I suppose that would be nice, sometimes I think about it, maybe if and when I retire…. In the meantime, they are mostly scattered around the house, mainly in the garage where they share space with the guns from Volume 1, seven motorcycles, glasses and goblets, liquor bottles, and the usual garage stuff. If there is to be a second edition of this book, I may need a bigger garage. However, my plan for volume 3 of Handfuls of History is to write about Coins of Ancient Realms.
