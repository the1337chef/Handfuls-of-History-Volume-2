Circa 1820

This type of short sword or dagger is known as a sekin. Most likely it came from the Minangkabau people who live in the high country inland of Padang in the Barat province of southwest Sumatra. They are Islamic but, like most Indonesians, their culture is a blend of Islam with Hindu and Buddhist traditions. The sekin was the local form of the kris and has some elements in common with the kris. There is a "greneng" at the base of the blade, called a'jour for these knives. It usually takes the form of a janggut with curved and hook-shaped projections. However, on this example the janggut corresponds to the three holes, which are sealed rather than open. The ring of the topmost hole appears to have been bent inward. Although connected to the kris, in general, a sekin also resembles other small Indonesian daggers such as badeks and sewars.

This sekin was in fact originally misidentified as a badek, but it can be distinguished from a bakek by the curved or bellied blade, the stylized hilt, and the decoration on the flared mouth of the scabbard, as well as the kris-like janggut. With most sekins the hilts are characterized as stylized bird heads, but on this example, it appears more like a stylized elephant. It should be noted though, that the tern sekin is also applied to short swords of the Batak people, but those are larger and more machete-like, while this is a thrusting blade.

The Minangkabau are also known as the Minang and today about 4.5 million Minang still live in the Barat province of Sumatra. Their culture is strongly matrilineal with most property and other family assets passing though female lines, something that is rather unique for an Islamic people. The name, Minangkabau reportedly derives from the conjunction of the words for victorious and buffalo. Legendarily, this relates to the founding of the Minangkabau kingdom when they settled a dispute with a neighboring kingdom by allowing the possession of the territory to be decided by a contest to the death between two buffalo. While the opposing king choose an aggressive male buffalo, the Minang choose a baby buffalo with small horns that were ground to be as sharp as knives. The bull ignored the calf, which then punctured the belly of the bull when it searched for an udder in an attempt to nurse.

In the early 1600s the Minang territory was occupied by the Acehnese sultanate and the first contact with the Dutch was to "liberate them" from Aceh in return for a trading monopoly.
