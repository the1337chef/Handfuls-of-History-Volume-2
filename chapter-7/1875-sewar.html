Circa 1875

Although the likely origin of the sewar-type dagger is India, they were most used and developed their unique features in Sumatra. In the Aceh Sultanate of northern Sumatra this style of dagger was known as a siwaih. They were called siva or sewah by the Alas people, sewah by the Gayo, and seiva by the Minangkabau. They are generally not considered to be so magical or sacred as keris or rentong daggers.

Sewars are typically of rather modest size and have a single-edged, usually curved blade that is sharp on the concave side. There is no guard but the rather bulbous handles mounted at a slight angle allow them to be held firmly. The most bulbous hilts are said to resemble pomegranates (hulu boh glima). The scabbards, which can be rather plain or highly decorated, as on this example, have an extension on the side under the curve of the handle known as cangge. On this example the cangge is beautifully carved to match the hilt.

A sewar is carried at the side, stuck under a belt or sash, and the cangge likely served to hold the scabbard in place. They would be carried on the left to be drawn with the right hand. As is sometimes the case with other weapons, the decoration of the scabbard of this example is just on the side that would face out (see the Thouma and Omani Jambiya and the Shibriya daggers). The blade is pattern-welded steel, similar to the pamor of a keris.
