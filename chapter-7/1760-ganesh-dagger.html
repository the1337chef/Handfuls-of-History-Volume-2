Circa 1760

This is really a one-of-a-kind item and so difficult to place. The image of Ganesh, the Hindu elephant god confirms Indian influence, but the style of the blade and somewhat atypical depiction of the god makes it more likely to be from one of the islands of the Malay Archipelago or possibly from Sri Lanka.

Approximately 2 percent if the population of Indonesia today identify themselves as Hindu, although on Bali over 80 percent of the population is Hindu. Prior to the wide acceptance of Islam in the 14-15th centuries, there were many Hindu kingdoms throughout the archipelago. One of the largest, and last to fall, was the Majapahit Empire, which dominated Java until 1527 when it was conquered by the Demak sultanate, one of the early Islamic states established on Java.

There is no such thing as Hindu orthodoxy. There are many Hindu deities worshiped in many different ways. Most Hindus will associate themselves with a specific patron deity and Ganesh (Ganesha) is one of the most popular deities in the Hindu pantheon, even venerated by Buddhists and Jains. He is sometimes considered an avatar of Vishnu, but more usually thought of as a distinct deity who is worshiped as a remover of obstacles. It is likely that he originated as a pre-Hindu tribal god. There is wide variety in how Ganesh is pictured, sometimes rotund and jovial, and sometimes more militant and severe. He is always represented with the head of an elephant, and there are various stories about how he came to acquire that attribute. He is usually considered to be a child or creation of Shiva and/or Parvati. In some stories, he was considered too beautiful or powerful in his original manifestation and was beheaded and given the elephant head as a replacement. Sometimes he is shown with multiple elephant heads.

The Ganesh of hilt of this dagger is a relatively simple and meditative figure, less ornate than typical Hindu representations. The carved wood was originally gilded but little of the gold survives. The single edged blade is 7.5 inches long, of a very simple and practical shape. For the people of the Pacific islands many sorts of weapons have spiritual or religious significance. The keris for example has great significance for the peoples of Java, Sumatra, and the Malay Peninsula, that predated by many centuries the coming of the Islamic faith and is preserved to the present. This dagger may have had a similar significance for a family preserving an ancestral connection to the Hindu god who was the remover of obstacles.
