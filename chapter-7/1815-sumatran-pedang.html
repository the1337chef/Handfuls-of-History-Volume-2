Circa 1815

This Indonesian sword is a tribute to the owner's faith in Allah. Islam is the majority religion throughout Indonesia, but more fervently practiced in some regions than in others. As an example of lax practice, every year on Java, the current Sultan of Yogyakarta puts Allah aside for a night to celebrate a spiritual union with Nyai Roro Kidul, the mythical Queen of the Southern Ocean, a rite that has been celebrated by the Yogyakarta Sultans since 1576.

The people of Sumatra take the formalities of the Islamic faith more seriously than peoples elsewhere in Indonesia. This is especially the case in the northern region of Aceh where even today there is popular support for the imposition of Sharia-based law. Another important, and sometimes powerful, Sultanate on Sumatra was in the south of the island at Palembang. The Sultanate of Palembang was persistently a thorn in the side of the Dutch colonizers, as well as the British, during the brief period when Thomas Raffles was governor of Indonesia and Napoleon controlled Holland. The Dutch expelled the last Sultan, Mahmud Badaruddin II, in 1823 and destroyed the Palembang kraton (palace). I feel it is a reasonable speculation to attribute this sword to the Palembang region of Sumatra.

This splendid sword has a blade that is over 25 inches in length, made of unusually treated pamor pattern-welded steel, blackened to highlight the Arabic inscriptions fully inlaid on both sides and all along the spine. The scabbard is wood, covered with silver mounts. The handle is carved horn with a deeply chased silver collar. It should be noted that the blackened pamor and aspects of the silver work are sometimes seen in work on kris daggers from Lombok. There are groups of Islamic people on Lombok, but they have never been politically important or powerful. Though much of its history, Lombok was a vassal state to the Kings of Bali.

As illustrated throughout this chapter, there are many beautiful and unique sword styles that come out of the archipelago, but this sword does not readily fall into any common category. It came to me from Oriental Arms and Artzi referred to it as a parang. Others might insist that it is a pedang, but both parang and pedang are simply generic terms for sword. Nonetheless, parangs in general are more machete-like than pedangs, so that pedang is probably the more appropriate term for this sword. Specialized weapons that are classified as either a parang or pedang usually come with additional descriptors such as pedang lurus, piso pedang, parang nabur, or parang latok. Zonneveld lists 40 different subtypes of pedang and 44 different subtypes of parang, including the redundant and wonderfully alliterative, parang pedang. While the hilt of this sword is similar to what Zonneveld classifies as a Type III pedang the blade does not match that type, having more of a point, so it is probably most reasonable to just refer to this item as a sword.

A couple of the pictures show this sword compared to a pedang lurus and the Javanese golok with carved wood handle. Clearly this is a more substantial weapon than either of the other two. With the fullered blade it has a resemblance to a western saber, such as the Mounted Artillery officer's sword. The style of the grip resembles that of the golok, but in keeping with the Islamic dedication, the design is abstract rather than figurative.

The inscription long the spine is from the Quran, Taubah 51: "Never will we be struck except by what Allah has decreed for us; He is our protector, and upon Allah let the believers rely."

The inscription on the right side is also from the Quran, Hud 56: "Indeed, I have relied upon Allah , my Lord and your Lord. There is no creature but that He holds its forelock. Indeed, my Lord is on a path [that is] straight."

On each side at the base before the inscription is the Shahada, the standard invocation to Allah: "There is no god but Allah and Muhammad is the messenger of God". At the very base of the blade on each side are the names of the first two caliphs:

Abu bakr and Umar ibn al Khattab. Between the names of the caliphs and the Shahada there is an inlay that looks like it might be a snake or dragon but it is hard to for sure what it is, or why it would be there on both sides.

My Iraqi friends who identified the Arabic inscriptions given above were puzzled by the inlays on the left side. They confirmed that they look like Arabic script, but they did not think the words were Arabic. That led us to speculate that perhaps this inscription is something in another language (Indonesian or Malaysian?) written in the Arabic alphabet.

The Muslim Quran and the Jewish Torah are examples of ancient holy books still preserved in their original language and script, but both of these sacred books have been translated into many other languages. Muslims who are not native speakers of Arabic use translations of the Quran in their native languages, just as nearly all Christians know the New Testament best in their own vernacular. The Indonesian artist who crafted these inscriptions was certainly not a trained Arab calligrapher, so that even the identified verses were more "recognizable" than easily readable. For this reason, even just transliterating the unknown inscription would be difficult. To take it further and translate that from whatever unknown language it might be in would near impossible. Therefore, I am left with the speculation that the inscription on the left may even be the same verse as on the right side of the sword, but translated to a native language and transliterated.
