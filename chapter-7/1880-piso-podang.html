Circa 1880

This type of sword was used by several Indonesian peoples in the 19th and early 20th century, primarily of the Batak group in northern Sumatra. It shows a couple of different foreign influences and comes from a time when the Batak had come under the influence of the Dutch. The hilt bears a resemblance to the Tulwars of India. The blades, like those of European swords, are usually forged of solid steel, rather than local pamor pattern-welded steel. The curvature and fullers on this example are similar to those of the Russian saber. Piso Podang blades are in fact quite often European made, and it has been suggested that the name Piso Podang comes from the Portuguese word for sword, "espada." Similar swords with straight blades are known as chenangkas.

Based on their language, several different tribes are considered to be part of the Batak group, including Alas, Kluet, Singkil, Karo, Pakpak, Simalungun, Toba, Angkola, and Mandailing. Only the Toba specifically associate themselves with the name Batak. Their root language is of the Austronesian language family, rather than the Malay languages, and the various Batak peoples remained for a long time outside of the main stream of Hindu/Islamic cultures on Sumatra. The Batak were animist headhunters and cannibals at the time when they first encountered Europeans, and they remained so even for some time after conquest by the Dutch. The very first accounts of these tribes in Western literature by Marco Polo passed on stories of ritual cannibalism, although Polo himself was reluctant to venture inland to verify the tales. The continuing practice of cannibalism by the Batak was confirmed by Thomas Stamford Raffles in the 1820s. Raffles reported: "It is usual for the people to eat their parents when too old to work," and that for certain crimes a criminal would be eaten alive: "The flesh is eaten raw or grilled, with lime, salt, and a little rice." Franz Wilhelm Junghuhn who visited the Batak in the 1840s reported slightly more refined behavior, "People do the honest Battaer an injustice when it is said that they sell human flesh in the markets, and that they slaughter their old people as soon as they are unfit for work... They eat human flesh only in wartime, when they are enraged, and in a few legal instances." Presumably, when a criminal was sentenced to be eaten by the tribe, relatives of the condemned were obliged to provide salt, red pepper, and lemons to show that they accepted the verdict.

As with other tribal holdouts against Islam, like the Dayaks of Borneo, in the 19th century the Batak were largely converted to Christianity by protestant missionaries, however not until some of the first missionaries were lost to the cooking pots.
