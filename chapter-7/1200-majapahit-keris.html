Circa 1200

The small daggers and knives known as Majapahit keris are the oldest known forms of edged weapons indigenous to the Malay archipelago. The very oldest are as much 1600 years old or perhaps even older. The association with "Majapahit" comes from the name of the last large Hindu/Buddhist empire centered on Java prior to the widespread conversion of the islands to Islam. While the oldest of these talismanic daggers certainly predate the era of the Majapahit (1300-1500) the tangible and intangible remains of that culture are what the local peoples of Java probably most associate with their ancient ancestors, hence the attribution. The real origins of these objects are too far back in their history to have anything but a mystic and spiritual association.

While it is generally agreed that the commonly used term, Majapahit, is not really appropriate, there is little consensus on what would be a better name. They are sometimes called sajan (offering), membela (protection), or pak mudi, pusaka (heirloom or legacy) keris. They are always considered to have great spiritual power that comes from their veneration. They are often kept as objects within a domestic shrine and are always passed from one generation to the next. Some legends state that their steel is imbued with power for the rightful possessor and that they are poisonous to the enemies of the owner.

Two things identify a Majapahit keris. Firstly, the blade and hilt are forged together from a single piece of pamor, the Malay pattern-welded metal that uses meteoritic steel. Secondly, the handle is always a stylized human form, representing a symbolic unity between the weapon and the owner (or the ancestral spirits). The Majapahit keris is thought to be a precursor to the later traditional keris. However, the oldest examples, like this one, have a relatively symmetrical shape to the blade, unlike the typical keris. Examples are seen which have blades that more resemble those of post-Majapahit keris, and sometimes even have other keris features like the spines (janggut) and an "elephant trunk" on the top of the blade, or even a false ganja, making it look as though the blade was forged of two pieces. These pieces are clearly of relatively more recent production. It seems unlikely that examples made in the style of modern keris would have been held as family heirlooms and that more likely they were merely made for sale to tourists (perhaps as long ago as the early age Dutch colonialism).

More talismans than weapons, Majapahit keris are never particularly large. This example, which is only 9 inches in length, is perhaps a bit on the small side, but ones of half this length are also known. Examples of much greater length are more like to be modern tourist items. The human figures on the handles will also vary in the amount of detail, again with those of greater detail being more likely of recent make. The figure on this piece is very stylized, reminding me of the figures from the Cycladic period of Aegean art, a style emulated by the modern artist Brancusi.

I acquired this item through eBay and it was shipped from Bali. When I opened the package there was an amazing aroma of incense that still lingers on the nearly paper-thin blade. The scabbard is, of course, modern construction, made to protect this ancient talisman.
