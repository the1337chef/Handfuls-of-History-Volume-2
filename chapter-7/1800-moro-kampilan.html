Circa 1800

This kampilan was purchased in Manila in the late 70s by my friend Artzi Yarom of Oriental Arms and was in his private collection for many years. It was most likely a weapon of the Moros of the Southern Philippines. Although the Moro were converted to Islam prior to the 15th century, there is a lot of tribal symbolism in this sword linking it to animistic tribes such as the Dayaks of Borneo.

The kampilan is typically a rather large sword. One picture shows it in comparison to a Dayak Mandau. It can be used with a single hand, but probably was more often used as a two-handed weapon. The most famous use of a kampilan was at the Battle of Mactan on April 27, 1521, when the king (Sultan) of the Mactan tribe, Datu Lapu-Lapu and his warriors fought the troops of Ferdinand Magellan, resulting in the death of the Spanish explorer. This sword from the 1800s is a direct connection to those peoples who have continued to fight the Spanish since that first encounter more than 490 years ago.

Although the natives scored an initial victory against the Spanish, the Spanish came back to conquer most of the tribes of the islands which would be their future colony. Conquest by the sword was followed by conquest by conversion. The tribes who resisted both forms of conquest were called by the Spaniards "Moro" (Moors), from the Spanish term for Muslims. In the present day, about six percent of the people of the Philippines are identified as Moro, representing at least 13 different ethnic groups. More than other peoples of the Philippines, the Moro are connected to their tribal origins, which are essentially similar to other Malaysian peoples. The Moro were never assimilated into either the colonial or modern Philippine nations. The Moro also successfully resisted Japanese conquest and occupation during World War II. The Moro who continued to resist incorporation into the modern nation of the Philippines were eventually allowed to establish the Autonomous Region of Muslim Mindanao. Other swords associated with the Moro are the large kalis form of the kris (also known as a sundang), and the smaller machete-like barong.

The shape of a kampilan is quite distinct. As on this piece, horse hair was a common adornment for the hilt. The single-edged blade is broader and thinner at the tip than at the base, with a characteristic spike on the upper edge. The steel is pattern welded, but not the same as the pamor of other Indonesian swords. The hilt is meant to symbolize the eye and open mouth of some mythical beast.

Not surprisingly, this sword came without a scabbard. When it was originally carried as a weapon, it would have had a scabbard that was just a pair of wood slats bound with rattan. When the sword was used it would often just cut through the rattan so that the scabbard would be destroyed.
