Moro Barong

1885

The Islamic tribes of the Philippines who held out against the colonizing and Catholization efforts of the Spanish came to be know as Moro (Moor). Their most renown war swords in their struggle against the Spanish and other tribes were the kampilan and later the kris-like kalis (sundang). These were not really the sort of swords that were fit both for warfare and everyday use in the field or forests. The barong of the southern Moro tribes such as the Tausug, Sinama and Yakan however, is well suited to serve either as weapon or as a machete. The barong is characterized by having a heavy single-edged, leaf-shaped blade, usually of laminated (pattern-welded) steel, but not the pamor steel typical of kris blades. It is the sort of weapon that can be used in relatively tight quarters, such as a jungle path. There is usually a metal collar or ferrule above the blade that joins with a sleeve and the curved wooden (or sometime ivory or bone) pommel to make up the handle. The pommels are often elaborately carved in shapes suggesting a cockatoo, naga, or other fanciful creature. However, the Moro being Islamic, the creature represented in the pommel is usually more stylized than literal. The shape of the pommel protects the hand and makes for a sure grip. However, lacking a cross guard, the barong is strictly a sword for cut rather than thrust use.

The size of the blade and style of the scabbard suggest this barong was made in the late nineteenth century when the Moros were still at war with the Spanish, before they transferred their struggles to first the Americans and then to the modern Philippine government. In 2012 the Autonomous Region of Muslim Mindanao was recognized giving the Moros some measure of independence. The sword on the Moro flag however is a kalis, not a barong.
