Rentong (Rencong)

Circa 1875

The Sultanate of Aceh, located on the northern end of the island of Sumatra, was one of the earliest and most powerful Islamic states in the Malay Archipelago. Although the keris was used on Sumatra, the Acehnese also had great veneration for their own native dagger, the rentong. Moreover, since the Acehnese have endeavored to maintain their culture identity, and on numerous occasions have fought for political independence, the rentong has a significance for them that is probably greater than that of the keris. One story attributes the origin of the rentong to one of the early sultans, Alaiddin Riayatyah (1528-1568) at a time when the country was fighting early battles against the Portuguese at sea. Sewar-type daggers, which have a relative small bulbous handle, were thought to be difficult to hold in a bloody fight. At the sultan's request the redesigned dagger was given a larger curved handle that was easier grip in a fight. Since that time the rentong has been a national symbol for the Acehnese.

The location of the Aceh Sultanate on the straights of Malacca put them in a strategic position for trade and, as recently as the nineteenth century, the control of commerce through piracy. Aceh was in open revolt against the Dutch through most of the late colonial period and so their traditional weapons such as the Rentong and the larger Sikin Panjang swords were emblematic of their national identity. The Acehnese have also been largely resistant to incorporation into the modern Indonesian state. The pre-Islamic threads evident in the cultures of Java and the other islands are much less apparent in northern Sumatra where Islamic practices are followed far more strictly than elsewhere in Indonesia and include the enforcement of sharia law.

There are several variants of the rentong, and this is an example of the most distinctive style with a hilt referred to as "hulu meuccange" which has a bulge in the middle called a "Boh mano" (chicken egg). The relatively thin single-edged blade is probably most well suited for thrusting attacks, as is the grip required with the uniquely shaped handle. As with the keris in other parts of Indonesia, the Rencong are considered an essential part of traditional Acehnese apparel and are still worn for ceremonies such as weddings. They are carried on the left side or in the back under a sash.

This is a comfortably sized weapon. The steel is pattern welded but is not typical Indonesian pamor. There is a fuller (blood grove) as on most examples. The scabbard, which shows evidence of damage and repair, has an elegant form with a combination of deep carving and light etchings perhaps burned into the wood.
