Circa 1850

Java is the fifth largest island in the Malay archipelago but is the home to over 50 percent of the Indonesian population, making it the most populous island on the planet. It was the center for powerful Hindu Buddhist empires that left behind some of the largest temple complexes ever built at Borobudur and Prambanan. By the 16th century, Islamic sultanates began to dominate the island with the Mataram Sultanate being the largest and most enduring. It is on Java that the Dutch also centered their colonial enterprise in the city they called Batavia, which we know as Jakarta. The name comes from the Batavian people who inhabited Holland during the time of the Roman Empire.

Assigning an origin to a particular keris is always fraught with some uncertainty. Artzi of Oriental Arms attributed this piece to Java and that is consistent with other pieces in his extensive archive. It is also consistent with the pieces identified as from Java in Zonneveld's book. In my opinion, this is a piece of outstanding condition and quality. The carving of the bone handle is especially impressive. You might let your imagination persuade you that it is a fanciful representation of demon or other supernatural creature.

Ostensibly, since the 1500s, the people of Java have been Islamic, however they remain steeped in many rich and more ancient traditions. At the founding of the Mataram Sultanate by Sutawijaya in 1584, it is legend that the Sultan was taken as a spiritual consort by the goddess Nyai Loro Kidul, the Queen of the Southern Oceans. This tradition of a spiritual relationship between the Sultans and the goddess continues to the present day. The goddess is worshiped in annual festivals at Samudra beach in Western Java every year on April 6. Likewise, the characters of the Wayang klitik puppet theater transport ancient Hindu legends into a uniquely native culture that tells stories of Rama not Mohammad. Clearly, in their hearts the people of Java do nurture gods other than Allah and objects like this Keris are testimony to their rich culture.
