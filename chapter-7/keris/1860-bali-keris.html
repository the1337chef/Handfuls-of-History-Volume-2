Circa 1860

Bali is arguably amongst the most exotic locations in Indonesia, distinguished for being the home for most of the nation's Hindu minority population. Their culture is distinct, and therefore the Bali keris is one of the more recognizable types. Lacking the Islamic prohibition against figurative art on sacred objects, keris from Bali often have handles carved to represent supernatural beings, like the demon portrayed on this example. It is likely that this creature is Ravana, enemy of Rama, abductor of Rama's wife Sita. This story in told in the Ramayana, as depicted in a Lontar scroll associated with the scribe's knife in Chapter 5. Bali keris also sometimes have gods or magical beasts such as naga serpents carved on their blades.

The carved handle and stone mounted circular selut, in place of a cup shaped mendak, identify this as originating from Bali. The scabbard is nicely done with decorative tortoise shell panels. However, there are some things that are not quite right about the blade. Firstly, it has been over polished and polished poorly. Scratch marks are clearly visible, and if the blade is made of pamor, the patterns have been largely obscured. What are visible are traces of old etchings that may have been intended substitute for the correct texturing of the steel. There is a ganja of a separate piece of metal but it too appears to be plain steel rather than pamor. There are no Janggut (spines) or other decorative elements to the edges of the upper blade, but the absence of these features is not so uncommon as the polished appearance of the metal. The pronounced central ridge in the upper portion of the blade is also unusual. These peculiarities lead me to suspect that the blade may be a replacement. Alternatively, adherence to these traditional features may not have been so important for a Bali Keris.
