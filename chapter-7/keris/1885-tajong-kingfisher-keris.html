Circa 1885

People in the West know this style of dagger as a kingfisher keris, mistakenly believing that the ornate hilt is carved to represent a long-beak bird. They are therefore sometimes called keris pekaka for the Malay word for the kingfisher bird. The image however is actually that of a divine spirit or demon probably out of the mythology and imagery of the Wayang theater. It has been argued that the image has become particularly distorted to make it more acceptable for Islamic people and in the oldest examples a human form is more clearly detectable. It might also be argued that the "beak" could also add to its functionality as a weapon, if used to deliver a backhanded blow.

The tajong type of keris actually originated not on the islands but on the mainland. They are associated with the peninsular Malaysian state of Kelantan and the Pattani region of in Southern Thailand. These areas formed the core of the ancient empire of Langkasuka. The people of Malaysia and Indonesia have similar cultures, and the two nations are made up of diverse peoples bound into different countries as a consequence of their separate colonial histories. While the Dutch exploited the Indonesians, it was the British who wielded the greater influence on Malaysia. I have read from multiple sources that it is a commonly held opinion in Indonesia that they would have been better off if they had been "colonized" by the British rather than by the Dutch. I have not read any opinions expressed regarding whether the Indonesians would have been better or worse off if had they been conquered by the Spanish.

There may be something to the idea that the British did a better job preparing their colonies for independence than the Dutch did in Indonesia. However, in the dying days of the regime of Indonesia's first president Sukarno, the failing president agitated his nation into confrontation with Malaysia with the goal on annexing Malaysian territories, particularly those on the largely Indonesian-controlled island of Borneo. These events are some of the stories told in the novel, The Year of Living Dangerously.

Counting the undulations, this is a seven-lok sword. It has good authentic pamor steel, a clearly separate ganja, and both the elephant's trunk and Janggut on the opposite side. As is correct for a Tajong keris, the sampir is boat shaped with high curves both front and back.
