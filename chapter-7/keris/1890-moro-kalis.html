Circa 1890

The term "Moro" comes from the word Moor, the generic term the Spanish used for Muslims. The Moro of the Philippines are people who retained the Muslim faith, which they accepted prior to the arrival of the Spaniards. To the Spanish, I suppose, the majority peoples of Indonesia would all be "Moro." In the Philippines 90 percent of the population are nominally Christian (Catholic) and the Moro are the disenfranchised minority who never submitted to the Spanish, and indeed still fight for independence. They fought the Spanish, the Americans, the Japanese, and the modern national government. In 2012 the Autonomous Region of Muslim Mindanao was recognized giving the Moros some measure of independence. A kalis is on the flag of the Moro state.

Their Islamic faith, of the flavor unique to the Asian island peoples, is something that binds the Moro of the Philippines to the other keris-bearing peoples of the archipelago, so that in their battles for independence they have put aside weapons of their ancestors like the kampilan, in favor of their own version of the keris, the kalis (also sundang), as well as their everyday weapons like the barong. While the typical Indonesian keris is an elegant gracile weapon, the Moro keris is really a battle-proven broad sword. They have blades that are about as long as the execution-type penjang keris, but are much broader and heavier. As with the keris, the blades may be wavy or straight, with curvy blades about twice as common as straight ones. Most are made of some sort of pattern-welded steel but usually not of the same type of pamor typical of Indonesian keris. Although usually they have spines (janggut) and other elaborations on the upper blade that are seen on typical keris, with the elephant's trunk being almost always present, most lack a true ganja (i.e. the piece of metal separate from the blade below the grip). However, most have a false ganja, indicted by an engraved line where the separation of a true ganja would have been, faintly visible on this example. Also, because of how they would have been used in battle more for cutting than just thrusting, they mostly have pommels to help hold the sword in the hand during strenuous use. The pommels are usually in the form of a stylized bird (cockatoo) head. The pommels were made out of ivory, or more often wood. The pommel on this sword is very typical. Occasionally the pommels are oversized but still suggestive of a cockatoo. Another feature seen on the Moro blades, and not present on normal keris, are the use of one or two clamps (baca-baca or sigi) holding the blade and handle together.

Some kalis have scabbards that are similar to those of typical keris, but often with smaller, less elegant sampirs. However, it seems that from hard use many of the Moro blades have lost their scabbards and scabbards without any sampirs are also common. The scabbards lacking sampirs have cutouts on the sides for the wide parts of the upper blade. It was clear from the pictures on eBay that the scabbard for this sword was not in the greatest shape. When it was arrived the sampir was basically detached, held loosely in place with an ill-fitting machine screw in one of the original peg holes. I re-glued the sampir and replaced the pegs (one replacement is not visible) and now the connection is firm. The blade appears to be plain steel and shows scratches from rough polishing.
