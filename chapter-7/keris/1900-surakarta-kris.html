Circa 1840/1940

Edward Frey, founder of an eponymous scientific equipment company that specialized in lab equipment for middle and high school science classes, was an avid collector of oriental weapons, especially Indonesian krises. Numerous items from his collection went up for auction on March 26, 2017. A friend drew my attention to the auction. She pointed out a gilded bhuj that was in such perfect condition I was suspicious of its authenticity until I checked the opinion of my friend Artzi at Oriental Arms. Online bidding preceded the live auction for a couple weeks. During that time, it was clear that certain items, including the Bhuj, were going to go for much more than the pre-auction estimates. After I was overbid on the Bhuj, with final bids likely to be over $4,000, I found an affordable bhuj on eBay and shifted my attention to a gilded chakra. The pre-auction estimate on the chakra suggested a range of $600-$900 and I was prepared to go up to $2,500. As it turns out, the chakra was the most expensive item sold, going for $19,000 (plus 22 percent premium)!

I did hit the bid button a few times during the live auction and the only time that I was successful was on was this kris. It appears in Figure of 8 of Frey's book, The Kris: Mystic Weapon of the Malay World.

There were a total of 26 krises in the auction, and as I was following the live bidding, I noted that they were not doing as well as other items. Ultimately, seven kris failed to even draw an opening bid and so were passed over. Overall, out of 142 items in the auction (not counting books), 126 sold. Only 73 percent of the krises sold compared to 92 percent of the other types of weapons.

Once I noticed that the krises were going relative cheaply, I went through the items that had not yet come up for bid in the live auction, going through the catalog on another device, so I could get ahead of the bidding. I picked this one to bid on when it came up in the live auction.

The blade, probably from the mid-19th century, is older than the hilt and according to Frey it was "mounted with a Surakarta style hilt, and matched to a scabbard decorated by the aging Solo (Surakarta) artist." Similar work has been associated with the artist, Mas Amien.

As noted above, the planar hilt is typical of krises from the Sunanate of Surakarta in southern Java. As is usually the case with hilts like these, there are a couple of carved spots, located always toward the top and bottom of the inside edge. These stylized mask-like faces are called cecekan and are believed to represent ancestors or deities.

The sheath style is known as sunggingan. While "sunggingan" simply means, "painted," the term is usually only applied to works done great skill and intricacy. Nonetheless, sunggingan scabbards vary significantly in the quality and the subject matter of the painting. Many sunggingan scabbards have scenes from the stories that are the common themes of Wayang Kulit (puppet) theater, and the Hindu epics, such as the Ramayana, that they draw upon. The story of the Ramayana is portrayed on the lontar panels shown along with the scribe's knife. In the Ramayana, Rama must rescue his wife Sita from the Demon King Ravana. The image on this sheath is that of a "raseksa" (giant's face). The face might be that Ravana, or any of a number of other Hindu demons.

The Sunanate of Surakarta is an Islamic monarchy in central Java, founded in 1745 and integrated into the Republic of Indonesia after its founding in 1946. Unlike other Indonesian Islamic monarchs, the rulers of Surakarta distained the title, Sultan, in favor of the title, Sunan. From the time of its founding, through the era of Dutch East Indian control, Surakarta was a largely autonomous Dutch protectorate. While the sister Yogyakarta Sultanate retains special status today within the modern nation of Indonesia, special status was revoked for the Sunanate of Surakarta shortly after the nation achieved independence. Although the Surakarta Kraton (residence of the former Sunan) is largely in decay, Pakubuwono XIII currently holds the title of the 13th Susuhunan (Sunan) of Surakarta.
