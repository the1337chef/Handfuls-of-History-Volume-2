Circa 1910

The pistol grip shape of the handle suggest that this keris originated with the Bugis people, an ethnic group living mostly on Sulawesi, third largest island of Indonesia. Historically, the Bugis were most well known as a sea going people; fishermen and traders moving spices and other goods on annual voyages among the islands. During the early colonial era they also had a formidable reputation as pirates preying on the Dutch trading ships. It is sometimes suggested that the term, "Boogieman" derived from the fear instilled by the Bugis pirates in the children of the Dutch colonists. However, while there might have been some transference of the term, the Boogieman of European folk tales is a much older phantom.

This is an attractive sword with a nice pamor blade and a well-defined ganja. The scabbard is also in excellent shape with engraved silver bands. One curious feature is the presence of an animal figure, probably a lion, carved into the steel on the outside surface of the dagu where the Belalai gajah (elephant's trunk) might usually be. The presence of a carved figure on a keris is not unknown but most common on Bali keris where the figure is usually supernatural. Outside of Bali, the most common carved figure (although still quite rare) is that of a seated lion, referred to as a "song" in the native terms. These figures are most common on the keris of particularly distinguished individuals.

There can be many chapters in the life story of a keris. Daggers are often broken and then repaired, sometimes reassembled with pieces of different origins. The lion carved on this piece is rather different from those on other blades, looking almost too realistic, suggesting that the lion on this blade may not be original to this dagger. Likewise, the handle may have once been mounted on another blade since the brass mendak is a somewhat poor fit to the handle, consistent with the idea that it may have originally been fit to another keris, or to this blade with a different handle. The combination of original and non-original features makes it possible that this keris was reconstructed and perhaps "dressed up" in such a way to make it appear more unique and therefore perhaps more valuable.

We are left then with several possibilities. This keris may have always been basically as it is now, intended by its maker to stand out. Alternatively, it may have been modified by request of its original owner (or one their decedents), perhaps to repair or disguise damage that was done to a now missing Belalai gajah. It is also possible that it was put together, dressed up with the carving and silver bands, to increase its value as an item for sale to tourists. Along that line, it may be noted that this was the first keris I purchased, years before the others in the collection. The carved lion and the silver bands were features that made it stand out to me.
