Circa 1887

A penjang keris is referred to as an execution sword, although there is no reason to suppose this sword was used for such a purpose. A penjang is significantly longer than a standard keris and would in fact be difficult to carry in some of the ways that an ordinary keris might be worn, such under a sash on the back.

As with all penjang, the blade on this sword is straight. The ganja and pamor pattern are distinct. There are Janggut but no elephant's trunk on the backside. The polished wooden handle is only slightly bent, making it useful for thrusting. The sheath is nicely polished wood with a bone or ivory end cap (buntut).

This keris is most likely from Sumatra, the large island north of Java and parallel to a long section of the Malaysian peninsula. Like most islands in the archipelago, prior to the 13th century, Sumatra was dominated by Hindu and/or Buddhist kingdoms that were subsequently converted to Islam. The powerful Aceh Sultanate in the North of the island survived to the 20th century. The Malacca Sultanate, centered at Palembang in the south of the island fell under Dutch control during the 1600s.

How effective would a penjang keris be as a weapon? The European weapon most brought to mind would be a rapier, a thrusting sword used for fencing. The penjang certainly could provide greater reach than would a normal keris. With the hot, humid climate of the region, soldiers were not commonly armored, so one can imagine that a penjang could indeed be an effective sword. I think another question would be how durable are the pamor steel blades. With hard use, would they hold up against a European rapier?
