Circa 1950

This is a very classically styled "Naga" kris, almost certainly from Bali, although probably not very old. The 17-inch blade is forged from very good high-contrast polished pamor steel with a Naga (snake god) chiseled into the blade. The cast brass handle is in the shape of a mythological creature set with colored stones. The ring mendak is also set with colored stones.

There is a proper ganga at the top of the blade and there are janggut (spines) on the upper edge of the wide side and the naga on the other side (the dagu), where the Belalai gajah (elephant's trunk) might otherwise be. The blade is of the wavy type (five lok), as fitting a Naga kris, and the serpent's body is carved down the center on both sides of the blade. The relatively plain dark wood scabbard is its least impressive feature.

In his book "The Kris," Edward Frey describes hilts from Bali that are like this one: "The most ornamental of the Bali type (hilts) is a large upright figure made of a carved wood or mastic effigy covered in gold foil or gold plated copper foil.... The mythical figure may take many forms. Most often found is Bayu, who carries in an ornate vase held waist high a holy fluid amrita (Sanskrit, 'immortal') that brings good and is exalted symbolically by a gem set into the vase. Usually also, there is a profusion of semi-precious stones or glass cabochons set into these gold figures."

Nagas appear frequently in Hindu legend, usually with negative connotations, but sometimes they are helpers of the chief protagonist. The following is a telling of the enmity between the Nagas and the god Garuda (from Wikipedia):

"The great nemesis of the nagas in the Mahabharata is the gigantic eagle-king Garuda. Garuda and the nagas began life as cousins. The sage Kashyapa had two wives (amongst his 13 wives, all prajapati Daksha's daughters), Kadru and Vinata, the former of whom desired many offspring, and the latter of whom desired few but powerful offspring. Each got her wish. Kadru laid 1000 eggs that hatched into snakes, and Vinata laid two, which hatched into the charioteer of Surya the sun god and Garuda. Through a foolish bet, Vinata became enslaved to her sister, and as a result Vinata's son Garuda was required to do the bidding of the snakes. Though compliant, he chafed and built up a grudge that he would never relinquish. When he asked the snakes what he would have to do in order to release his mother, Vinata, from her bondage, they told him he would have to bring them amrita, the elixir of immortality. Garuda stole the elixir from the gods and brought it to the serpents in fulfillment of their requirement, but through a ruse prevented them from partaking of it and achieving immortality. From that point onward, he regarded them as enemies and as food. (Book I: Adi Parva, Sections 16ff.)" This story may account for the association of the Naga with figure of Bayu used for the hilt.
