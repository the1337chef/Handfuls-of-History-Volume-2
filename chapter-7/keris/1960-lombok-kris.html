Circa 1960

This was an impulse buy, I was not really looking for another kris when I saw this on eBay. It was advertised as a "Naga" kris from Bali. I entered the opening bid and a few days later I had won the auction. I actually entered a maximum bid that was a couple hundred dollars higher, but no one bid against me. The pictures provided by the vendor were posted to an internet Kris forum and I was rather shocked with the negative reactions:

I'm sorry to say but I have noted a tendency of the self-anointed experts on sites like this to put people down and belittle the acquisitions of others. It also became clear that several people on this forum have a personal problem with the vendor who sold this item and that may have slanted their views.

Clearly this is not a very special piece, but I also think it is not such a bad piece either. It has all the traditional kris elements. There is a proper ganga, there are janggut (spines) on the outer upper edge and a sculpted image on the other side (the dagu). This could be taken to be a Naga (a mythical serpent demon), but the rendering is rather crude. Usually the Naga images on kris daggers are exceptionally well rendered, with the serpent's body insinuating down the length of the blade, which are usually of the wavy type, to complement the snake's body.

The blade of this kris is of pamor steel, though again not of any special or fancy pattern. The mendak is nice, set with colored stones. The wood of the scabbard and grip is in nice condition. The scabbard is somewhat unusual, without the pronounced sampir seen on the other Indonesian kris. In the 1930s this kind a scabbard was identified as the Madura type (G.B. Gardner in Kris and other Malay Weapons, Figure 13), but more recently (as per the forum "experts"), they are known as the kojongan or kekojongan style. These scabbards are sometimes used in Bali or Lombok, as well as Madura. The blade has been attributed most likely to Lombok. The island of Lombok neighbors Bali and at times it was control by Balinese kings. There is an ethnic Balinese minor population on Lombok who are predominately Hindu, however, as in most of Indonesia, the majority religion is Islam.

The Lombok Strait, that separates Lombok and Bali, is not very wide but is particularly deep. It marks the end of the chain of islands that were once probably connected by land bridges to mainland Malaysia. On the Bali side of the strait, the fauna are those of the mainland, and on the Lombok side, they are more related to the animals of Australia. This division was first noted by Alfred Russel Wallace in 1866, and is part of what has come to be known as the Wallace line.

The internet detractors not withstanding, I think this nice, if somewhat humble kris. It is hard to assign it an accurate date. Kris daggers are still being made in Indonesia both for the natives and for tourists. However, tourist pieces would tend to be fancier and more ostentatious. I purchased the frog holder to display this kris in my office. This ploncon was most likely made in Bali. It reminds me of the toilet paper holder that was in my bathroom in Bali that is shown in the last picture.
