Parang Nabur

Circa 1850

This style of sword is most associated with the Sultanate of Banjar (1526-1860) which was located in what is today the South Kalimantan province of Borneo. The Dutch East India Company valued Banjar as a source of pepper, and when the Sultan was threatened by a Bugis force from Sulawesi in 1757, he perhaps unwisely asked the Dutch for military help. Banjar subsequently became a Dutch protectorate and the later Sultans held their power at the discretion of their colonial overloads while the territory they controlled shrank around the capital of Banjarmasin. In 1857 Sultan Adam died and the Dutch tried to force the succession of the Sultan's illegitimate grandson Tamjied Illah, leading to a revolt and a religious war against the Dutch. The Dutch suppressed the revolt and put all the political control of the territory in the hands of their appointed president, although a figurehead sultan was appointed up until 1905 when any pretense of self governance was abolished.

Up until the Banjarmasin War of 1860, ships of the Banjar Sultanate plied the seas conducting trade with the Dutch and the other islands. Their early association with the Dutch led them to adopt the Parang Nabur as their native sword. The weapon has a remarkable blend of native/Islamic and European features. Except for the decorative features of the hilt, when viewed in its scabbard it could easily be mistaken for a European cutlass. Unsheathed, the blade shows its heritage with a curved klewang-like blade, ending not with the sharp point of a European or American cutlass but a floral motif. In spite of having a functional cross guard, like a typical klewang this is not a weapon for thrusting attacks.
