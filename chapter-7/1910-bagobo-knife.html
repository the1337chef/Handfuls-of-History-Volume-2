Circa 1910

The Bagobo are forest people of Mindanao, the southernmost island of the Philippines. The majority of the Bagobo still maintain their ancient tribal briefs, which are discussed in more detail in the entry on the Bagobo sword. The Bagobo practice subsistence "slash and burn" agriculture and take what they can from the forest, which, as in other parts of the world, is disappearing around them. Some of the Bagobo have moved into coastal towns and are being swept into the modern world, but for those in the forests, life is not so different from what it was a hundred or more years ago, although, at the urging of the central government, animal rather than human sacrifices are conducted to appease the malevolent spirits that they live amongst.

The Bagobo build simple houses but dress themselves in elaborate colorful costumes. Large earplugs are common, and it was a traditional beautification to have teeth filed down to stubs and then blackened.

There is always a lot of work to be done to glean subsistence from the forest, and women do their share of that work and probably more. Whether the job is to prepare food or process fibers and rattan from the forest plants, a knife is, of course, an essential tool. This distinctive piece is a Bagobo utility knife, often referred to as a woman's knife. Several similar knives are illustrated in the book by Fay-Cooper Cole, The Wild Tribes of Davao District, Mindanao. The rattan scabbard is skillfully made with slight ornamentation. However, it is likely that originally there were chains, bells, or other charms attached to the metal loops on the bottom of the scabbard.

It is common on single-edged knives that have a curved blade for the edge on the concave (inner) side to be the sharp one, as on the Chilean corvo and the Indonesian kerambit. This allows the knife to be used for draw cuts. Interestingly, on this knife it is the convex (outer) edge that is sharpened. It is not too hard to imagine how this would be more useful for the ordinary preparation of food on a cutting surface than to have only the inner edge sharpened.
