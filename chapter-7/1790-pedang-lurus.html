Pedang Lurus

Circa 1790

Pedang is the Indonesian word for sword and "lurus" simply means straight, therefore a pedang lurus is simply a straight pedang. Frequently, swords called by this name are quite elegant with silver handles and silver embossed scabbards, as is with this example. This sword is most likely from Java, but similar swords were used on other islands. The 21-inch blade is made from good quality pamor, the Indonesian version of pattern-welded steel. It is intended to be used primarily as a thrusting sword.

Of course, the keris is the most iconic and venerated of all Indonesian weapons and the person who owned this pedang quite likely would have also owned at least one keris. Why both? The keris is a sacred object to the Indonesian, with older heirloom ones having great spiritual power. A pedang lurus, even a beautiful one like this, is merely a weapon. In Zonneveld's encyclopedic book, Traditional Weapons of the Indonesian Archipelago, he shows a picture of a Javanese warrior with two keris and a pedang lurus. It was not uncommon for a warrior to carry as many as three keris in battle. By wielding the pedang the warrior might have protected a keris from being damaged, or they might have felt the pedang had a tactical advantage, used in one hand with a keris in the other as a parrying weapon.

It is possible that someone might have had a personal preference for a pedang over a keris, or would only carry a keris on more formal occasions. It is also possible that someone might not feel they had the cultural identity that would make a keris appropriate for them. Not everyone living in Texas wears a cowboy hat, at least not all the time.

Peacocks seem to be key decorative motifs in the silver covering of the scabbard. You see also a horse and other birds. To me this suggests Indian influence, although the pamor steel of the blade is typical of the islands. There is nothing that shows particular Islamic or tribal influences. Many of the Dutch took native wives and so there was also a substantial Eurasian population. Many of these people would have worked at the interface between the Dutch East India Company (VOC) and the native peoples. A pedang lurus similar to this one is known* which has a blade dated 1763 and is marked VOC.
