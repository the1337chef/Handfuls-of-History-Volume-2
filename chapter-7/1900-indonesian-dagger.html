Indonesian Dagger

Circa 1900

This unusual dagger most likely comes from Bali. In some ways it resembles a kris, the most well-known traditional weapon of Malaysia and Indonesia, but based on the blade it certainly is not a kris. The blade appears to be the repurposed use of an old spearhead. It is a pity to waste good steel, so it is not uncommon to see an old blade put to a new use. Other examples are the Chinese knife that was clearly made from an old spear, the massive Chinese sword that was perhaps cut down from a pole arm, and the Hungarian hunting sword made from a military saber. The blade may be Indonesian pamor pattern welded steel, or may just be so old and patinated that it gives that appearance.

The artwork of the hilt and the scabbard stand out and are strongly suggestive of Bali, one of the most unique and special places in Indonesia. I describe my own journey to Bali in the essay on the wedung. Like the hilt of the Balinese kris, the hilt of this dagger is a carved figure. However, the hilt of the Bali kris is more compact and seems easier to grip for use as a weapon, while the high relief of this carving makes it uncomfortable to grip strongly. The same can be said about the Ganesh dagger.

The figure carved into the hilt is both beast-like and human-like. This blending is very common in Balinese art. The scabbard is richly decorated and painted. There is a bird, something that looks like a snake confronting (almost kissing) a fish, and a dragon or naga at the bottom.

I don't often have occasion to use these weapons, but when I withdrew the blade to take picture, a wasp (or flying ant) crawled out of the scabbard. I quickly dispatched it with the dagger. I cannot say from whence this creature came, perhaps from Artzi's warehouse in Israel, or as far away as Bali.
