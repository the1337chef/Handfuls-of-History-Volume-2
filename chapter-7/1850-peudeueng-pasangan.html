Peudeueng Pasangan (Type I Pedang)

Circa 1850

This sword is from Aceh a region of Sumatra, in Indonesia. Aceh has long been known for its fervent dedication to Islam. The Acehnese fought a thirty-year war against Dutch colonialism, and after Indonesia gained its independence in 1948 a new period of Acehnese rebellion began that ended only after a terrible tsunami hit the region in 2004.

In Traditional Weapons of the Indonesian Archipelago, A.G. Van Zonneveld, classifies this type of sword as a Type I pedang. However, pedang is just an Indonesian word for sword and so there many "pedangs" in this Chapter, including the Type III, the Pedang Lurus, the Sumatran Pedang with Islamic dedications, as well as the Acehnese sikin. Classifying these various types by their hilt designs, this sword has a "hulu meu apet", which is an Indian-style basket hilt, like that of the Indian khanda. In this case, the Indian style hilt is mounted to a curved single-edged blade, most likely of European origin. In India, similar amalgams are known as firangi swords. This Indonesia adaptation of the Indian form has a shorter pommel spike than the Indian versions, and the basket is open at the top of hilt, rather than fused to the pommel as on the khanda and Indian firangi. There are distinctive short-spiked quillions and long pointed langets. The velvet-covered scabbard is newly made.

The grip of this style of pedang (or peudeueng) can be distinguished from that of an Indian Firangi by a characteristic bend which gives it a sort of pistol-style grip that is sometimes seen in European sabers, like the Prussian officer's sword. This style of grip may improve the transfer of energy for a forward thrust or the hold of the weapon for a slashing attack.

Zonneveld says that the Acehnese considered this style of sword as ceremonial and that they preferred the sikin as a weapon of war. He says this was because they favored weapons with the center of gravity further out on the blade. Certainly, while this sword could be used as a cut or thrust weapon, a sikin would have been wielded more as a machete, having essentially no point to the blade at all.

Many pictures of the native warriors from the Aceh war against the Dutch show them carrying a sikim pedangs, although this style pedang is also occasionally seen. Interestingly, this particular style of sword appears consistently in portraits of Keumalahayati* (1585-1604), an admiral of the Aceh Sultanate navy, considered the first woman admiral in the modern world. At the time, the Aceh Sultanate was a major power in the area and Keumalahayati's forces were a crucial factor in holding off both the Dutch and Portuguese navies. Remarkably for a Muslim people, her troops included other women, widows of Aceh and army officers. Her reputation as such an effective defender of the Sultanate persuaded Queen Elizabeth I to send a diplomatic mission to her directly to negotiate terms for English ships to be permitted into the Malacca Strait.


* A Google image search for Keumalahayati returns several similar portraits of a formidable woman wearing a red tunic and a shawl. She is looking forward, but with her body angled sometimes to the right and other times to the left. Some of the images are so similar you have to wonder if some are just the same image reversed, but others are different enough that it reasonable to think that they all draw from the same original source. I do not know how old the original portrait might be, or where these paintings are now located, but the consistency of these compositions, if it is an accurate attribution, would put this type of sword in the hand of important Sumatran woman 400 years ago.
