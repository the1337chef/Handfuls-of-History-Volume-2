Circa 1880

It is sometimes held that the history of the Philippines began with Magellan. This absurdly chauvinistic view can probably be attributed to the fervent efforts of the Spanish missionaries to not only convert the natives to Catholicism but to destroy pagan artifacts, including native documents and places of worship. This is quite in contrast to the work of Bernardino de Sahagún, the Spanish monk whose work provides us with some the best information of the pre-Columbian Aztec culture. In the western parts of the archipelago, the Dutch were mostly willing to let native cultures well enough alone, as long as they made their profits. In the Philippines, native cultures were only maintained at a cost by the Islamic (Moro) tribes and some of the remote hill tribes who retained their animistic beliefs.

In 1990 an ancient artifact known as the Laguna Copperplate came to light that proved that there were cultures in the Philippines over a thousand years ago that were very much like those that were thriving on Java at the same time. Current, more enlightened, scholarship suggests that prior to the incursions of the Spanish the Philippines had a history very much like that of the Indonesian islands. The original inhabitants were stone-age Austronesian peoples. On many of the islands these people were displaced by peoples from Southeast Asia and India. Some of the peoples of the Philippine islands are also believed to have emigrated from Borneo in the 12th century. There was an era when Hindu/Buddhist cultures flourished and these were then largely superseded by Islam over the period of a couple hundred years before Magellan and the later conquistadors and priests.

There are three main island groups that make up the Philippines: Luzon, Visayas, and Mindanao, going north to south. The Visayas constitute six large islands and numerous smaller one. The Visayans constitute the largest ethnic group on these islands but there are smaller groups including several tribes living on the island of Panay. These include the dark-skinned Ati people who are considered to be of the Negrito group related to the aboriginal Austronesians and have largely been converted to Christianity. Another group on Panay are the Suludnon people who maintain much of their pre-Hispanic culture and beliefs.

The tenegre style of sword comes from the Visayan Islands, and is usually attributed to Panay and quite possibly to the Suludnon people. The shape of the heavy blade makes it suitable as a serious weapon, as well as for use as a machete. Typical for a Visayan tenegre, the pommel is in the shape of demon head, something that of course would not have been on the sword of a devout Muslim or Catholic.
