Circa 1810

This sword most likely comes from the Palembang region in southern Sumatra. Palembang is the oldest city in Indonesia, and was the capital city of the powerful Malay Kingdom of Srivijaya. By the 1700s, Palembang was ruled by an Islamic dynasty and was a sultanate, like that of Aceh in Northern Sumatra. However, unlike the Acehnese, the rulers of Palembang tried to do business with the Dutch and were gradually absorbed into the Dutch commercial, if not political, sphere. The Dutch demanded a monopoly on the pepper trade and when the local resistance to this resulted in an attack on a Dutch ship in 1659, the Dutch retaliated by burning the city.

Although the Sultanate recovered from the Dutch attack, the situation remained tense between the locals and the Europeans. The Dutch hold on Sumatra was weakened during the time of the Napoleonic war when the English invaded Java in 1807, declaring themselves masters of the Dutch East Indies. The Palembang Sultan at that time, Mahmud Badaruddin II, rejected English claims on his realm and this prompted Thomas Stanford Raffles, the English governor of Java, to launch an invasion of Palembang. The English installed the Sultan's brother, Najamuddin, on the throne, and while the new ruler was acceptable to the British, he was unwilling to yield to the Dutch when they were returned to power. The Dutch invaded Palembang and deposed Najamuddin around 1820 and subsequently established direct colonial rule of the former sultanate.

Of course, some weapons from Palembang would have actually been used in these conflicts, while others would have survived as personal effects. It seems likely that this elegant sword would have been owned by a relatively well-to-do individual for personal defense and because it was the custom to be armed. This sword matches Van Zonneveld's classification of a Type III pedang, distinguished by the curved hilt with carved floral design and straight single-edged blade of typical Indonesian pamor pattern-welded steel. The blade, like that of the Bugis alamang and the Acehnese sikin panjang, is relatively unsuitable for thrusting attacks. There are still traces of gold on the carved hilt and a pattern of clear stones mounted near the mouth of the scabbard. A nicely carved wooden belt loop is attached to the front of the scabbard but the opening is rather small, more suitable for a cord or lanyard than a substantial belt.
