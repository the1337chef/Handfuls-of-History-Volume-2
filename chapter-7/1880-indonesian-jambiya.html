Indonesian "Jambiya"

Circa 1880

This is a rather unique piece since it is not identifiable as any usual Indonesian type. In shape and size the blade actually resembles those of Assib and Thouma Jambiya from Yemen, but while the Arab daggers are made of shiny steel with a raised central rib, this blade is diamond sectioned and made of typically Indonesian pattern welded pamor like the blade of a keris. The handle is also a distinctly non-Arab feature, as Arab jambiyas almost universally have an I-shaped handle, usually made of horn. Use of the carved bird head (probably a parrot) for a handle would go against the Islamic practice of avoiding figurative images.

Many Indonesian weapons, including pedangs, punals, and sewars have an abstractly shaped pommel that would lend itself to this sort of detailed carving. Indeed, some are seen with this sort of carving in wood or ivory. The style of the handle places the likely source for this dagger within Indonesia as an island where the older animistic beliefs are more tolerated, than among fervently Islamic peoples. This could include Bali or the Lesser Sunda Islands, such as Lombok and Sumbawa. There is, in fact, a sword from Sumbawa in the Van Zonneveld book with a similar hilt but a very different blade. It is therefore the shape of the blade combined with the handle that makes this dagger unique. Kerambit (Lawi Ayam) daggers have curved blades but these are usually narrower and of uniform width (not tapered). Another Indonesian knife with a curved blade is the beladau, which like a jambiya, is wider toward the hilt. The beladau is probably the closest fit for this dagger to any standard Indonesian type, however, most beladau are more like the Arab jambiya, with a handle as wide at the base as the blade is at the top.

Arab merchants began trading in the South Asian islands as long ago as the 1200s and established small communities shortly thereafter. Although the islands were spared outright colonization by the Arabs themselves, they were largely conquered by Islam. The Arab jambiya must have been a familiar weapon to the peoples of Sumatra and Java and probably was the inspiration for the beladau. This dagger however, I think is most properly viewed as a fusion of Arab influence and native art and custom.
