Sikin Panjang

Circa 1875

The Aceh sultanate of northern Sumatra is a fervently Muslim territory and from the 1600s until 1873 was considered a protectorate of the Ottoman Empire. Of all the peoples of Indonesia, the Acehnese provided the fiercest resistance to Dutch colonialism, fighting a war of resistance that lasted more than thirty years. The sikin panjang, a type of pasangan, was the most iconic fighting sword of the Aceh Sultanate.

Sikin pedangs appear frequently in photographs of native warriors taken during the thirty years of war between the Sultanate and the Dutch. The first Aceh War began in 1873, a time when the Dutch would have already been equipping their forces with relatively modern bolt-action Beaumont rifles. The first single-shot Beaumont rifles were introduced in 1871, and after 1878 they were upgraded to magazine-fed repeaters. Once the Dutch declared war, the Sultanate rapidly responded by modernizing their army. Modernizing would have taken two forms, firstly they would have had to source new firearms to replace antiquated flintlocks, and possibly even matchlocks, still in use. This was most likely accomplished with aid from England. In 1871 England began using Martini Henry rifles and by 1873 they were probably able to supply surplus Snider conversions of older Enfield rifles. The second form of modernization would have been social restructuring of the army with an established chain of command.

Traditionally, wars between the small princely states of India and other regions of South Asia were fought largely by the members of one kingdom's warrior class against those of their opponents. Modern wars involved larger armies with common soldiers armed with rifles rather than swords or other edged weapons. Sikin panjang were associated with the class of warlords who were elevated in rank by the sultan to lead his forces so that, as with European and American armies at this time, swords were becoming symbolic of authority.

The sikin panjang is a form of klewang, also similar to the balato of the Nias people, being a heavy single-edged weapon without a sharp point. It is by no means a thrusting weapon and has no cross-guard to prevent the hand from moving forward onto the blade if it were used for a thrusting attack. Rather than a traditional pommel, the grip broadens into a fork to keep the user's hand in place when delivering slashing attacks.

The 21.5-inch-long blade (wilah) of this sikin panjang is made of pattern-welded steel and is straight with a single edge and a 14-inch-long fuller just below the spine. The steel bolster is forged in a polygonal shape. The handle (hulu) is carved from honey-colored horn with a typical forked pommel. The scabbard (sarung) covered with twisted brass wire and mounted with chased brass mounts and a finely engraved ivory mouth.

A second kind of Acehnese pasangan is the firangi-type sword classified by Van Zonneveld as a Type 1 pedang. These usually have a European-style blade and a distinctive hilt in the style of an Indian khanda or tulwar known as a "hulu meu apet" (hilt with hand guard). The hilt style of the sikin is known as the hulu pasangan or sometimes hulu tumpang beunteung.

Although the thirty-year Aceh war ended in 1904 with the province largely pacified under Dutch control, following the independence of Indonesia in 1948 there was a reemergence of Acehnese nationalism that led to a second period of rebellion, this time against the central Indonesian government. In 2004, nature played a hand in the form of a devastating tsunami that killed 170,000 people in Aceh and left an additional 500,000 homeless. This led to a peace settlement, and now Aceh is governed as a special territory within Indonesia with a significant degree of autonomy.
