Circa 1918

The balato is the traditional sword of the native people of the Nias island, southwest of Sumatra. Their tribal culture is unique and celebrated today with war dances and music performed largely for tourist audiences. They remained out of the mainline of both the Islamic conversions and Dutch colonialism until the early 20th century. As in Borneo, with the Dutch suppression of the native headhunter culture, the people became the target of protestant missionaries so that now the majority of the natives identify themselves as Christians.

Balato swords show a fair degree of variation in blade shape and hilt design but, as on this example, most blades are single edged and have rounded points. Hilts usually have a carved pommel at the end of a sort of abbreviated pistol grip. The carvings are sometimes quite ornate but also sometimes quite simple. With some imagination, one can see a stylized beast in the carving of this sword. The construction and decoration of the scabbard for this sword is quite typical for a balato, with brass bands reinforcing wood. Sometimes scabbards are seen which retain an attached ball of woven rattan and sometimes boar's teeth, between the size of an orange and a grapefruit. The native would place within the ball various talismans such as bones, teeth, or other sacred objects.

As noted above, the Nias were a warlike people for most of their history but as is often the case with native swords, the balato could also give good service as a machete and a tool for daily life.
