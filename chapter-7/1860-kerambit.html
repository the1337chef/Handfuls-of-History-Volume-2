Circa 1860

The kerambit is believed to have originated with the Minangkabau people of West Sumatra, presumably inspired by the claw of a large cat. It is often considered a stealth weapon like the bagh nakh of India. It is traditionally used with an underhand grip that could aid in its concealment. It original uses were probably as an agricultural tool but it was ultimately weaponized. They are able to deliver viscous slashing or ripping wounds in close fighting. Its use was largely disdained by the upper class, as being a weapon used by peasants. However, a kerambit was frequently used by women, due to the relative ease with which it could be concealed in clothing or hair. The curved blade lends it some resemblance to jambiya, but unlike the Arab daggers kerambits are always plain, unadorned weapons.

The Minangkabau are one of the core ethnic groups of Indonesia and their trade and social contacts with other parts of South Asia led to the wider use of the kerambit. They were commonly used by the Bugis people and in Philippine martial arts. They are sometimes known by other names, for example as a lawi ayam (chicken's claw), which is how Gerald Gardner identifies it in his book. Variants are made with a semicircle to place the index finger rather than a closed loop.

This is a nice, authentic, old example, although the scabbard is a new replacement. The blade appears to be made of some form of pattern-welded steel, though not the pamor used for keris production. Modern versions are also produced by several well-known knife manufacturers including Spyderco and Cold Steel.
