Circa 1940

Traditionally, a dirk is a long thrusting dagger commonly associated with naval personal going back at least 500 years. They were often presented to junior officers in the British Navy as a token of their advancement. The term, dirk, is also used in reference to an unrelated style of Scottish dagger.

As part of their emulation of western styles, the Japanese adopted naval dirks for their officers during the Meiji era. The most common style was adopted in 1883 and technically is still an approved weapon today. They were very widely used by Japanese naval officers during their era of military expansion from the time of the Russo-Japanese war to the end of World War II. Because the style was substantially unchanged for so many years it is hard to date this piece with any real accuracy. There is no serial number or arsenal mark that can be seen without dismantling the piece. The wire wrapping is a bit out of alignment and the blade is imperfect, but otherwise it is in nice condition. The blades of these pieces were machine made and therefore never of very high quality, unlike some other Japanese swords. There were swords for Japanese Naval officers that were in the style of the Army Shin Guntōs, but a dirk must have been far more practical a weapon to carry on shipboard. The best reason for assigning this piece a World War II date is simply that most pieces of this sort in the United States would have been brought back as captured items.
