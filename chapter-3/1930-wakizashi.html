Circa 1930

Wakizashi can refer to any traditional Japanese sword of medium size, larger than a tanto and smaller than a katana. When the Samurai came to favor the katana over the longer taschi, they began to carry wakizashi as their second swords. One rationale for this is that if they were required to put aside their main sword when entering a house or fortress, the wakizashi left them with something that could still be used effectively for self-defense in confined spaces.

This particular piece was a lesson to me on bidding for items on auction from unfamiliar dealers, especially after a few glasses of wine. There were only three relatively low-resolution pictures provided. The dealer description read:


"Japanese, Attributed to Masanobu.

20-inch signed blade with fullers running to within 1-inch of the tip. Two holes in the squared-off tang. Engraved and hand-colored with leaf-pattern border. Multi-piece bone handle incised and hand-colored in foliate designs. Simple brass multi-piece bone saya ornately decorated and hand-colored. An old tag with pencil rubbing of the tang is attached to the sword and reads "Masa Nobu / 1390-" though this attribution is unverified at the time of this auction. Good. Blade scratched, stained and with some blemishes. Habaki loose, as is tsuba. Some repair to handle."


There was a picture of a signature on the tang. I have not been able to confirm that the signature is actually there because I have not been able to disassemble the sword. On a traditional Japanese sword, the blade is locked to the grip with a single bamboo peg. When I received this sword, the peg was taped to the top of the pommel, and it seems like the sword blade has been glued in place. The habaki is not just loose, it is detached and slides off the blade or gets stuck in the mouth of the scabbard. I wonder if this was part of some damage that occurred when the sword was improperly reassembled after the effort to photograph the signature, or if it has been like that for some time. One thing that is clear is that this is not a very old sword. It seems unlikely that the blade was hand forged and I would speculate that it might be a repurposed blade from a post Meiji era saber or kyu guntō. The statement that there are two holes in the tang should have been a real red flag, since there is no need for more than one hole in a proper nihonto (traditional sword). The presence of a second hole suggests that there was a need to create a new hole for mounting the blade into a different handle.

There is enough similarity in the upper portion of the auction picture to the visible base of the blade, with the large copper washer in particular, to accept that the signature is probably there. Does it read, Masa Nobu? Apparently it does. Does it date to 1390? Certainly not! A google search identified two Japanese sword smiths named Masa Nobu, and one search result* produced images of a beautiful Wakizashi dated to 1673-1681. Among the images was a picture of the signature with a translation. The lower two characters match those in the auction photograph and translate as Masa Nobu. However, this is no more likely to be 340 years old than it is to be 600 years old.

The other search result showed a sword smith by the name of Yoshihara Masanobu on a 1943 list of then current sword makers. They were ranked like Sumo warriors and he was placed at the modest rank of Maegashira. This Masanobu would have been active in pre-World War II Japan. That was a time when the Japanese were re-embracing their samurai heritage and getting away from their earlier efforts to copy European style swords. Kyu guntōs were being superseded by shin guntōs. The list of smiths goes on to say that some made non-traditional "showato guntō". The term "showato" is used by collectors to also refer to non-traditionally made swords of the Showa (post 1926) era. It also implies a lower grade of blade. The Masanobu on this list could indeed have been the maker of this very non-traditional and rather garish sword.
