Circa 1700

The tanto (short blade) is the smallest of the traditional samurai swords. They were usually paired with a taschi, an older style long sword that was somewhat longer than the katana. This tanto has a "hira-style" blade, flat without a ridgeline. A tanto was the knife traditionally used if a samurai was compelled by honor to commit seppuku (ritual suicide).

My friend Steve Bloom deemed the 10.5-inch blade, and general construction of this sword, as authentic old Japanese style. Unfortunately, the blade itself is unsigned, although the Kashira (end cap) and Fuchi (collar) are signed (see picture defining the sword parts). The person who consigned this knife to auction attributed signatures to Takase Eiju, (active 1775-1800), a retainer in the Matsudaira family, who became a kinkoshi (sword artist) under the great Taizan Motozane. However, a comparison between the signature on the Fuchi and signatures on other known works by Eiju does not support this attribution. They are nonetheless very nice works, and consistent with a period earlier than the work by Eiju.

The tanto has a traditional cloth-wrapped sharkskin handle, decorated tsuba and gilded menukis. When purchased there was a kogatana (small utility knife) in a pocket of the lacquered wooden saya. There is decoration on the kozuka (handle) of the side knife but it is not particularly in the style of the rest of the knife, so it may not be original. The blade of the kogatana is scratched with what may have been intended to be a signature.
