1944

As World War II progressed, the Japanese economy was stretched very thin, and although weapon production was still a priority, quality was sacrificed in many areas. This was especially evident in some of the late firearms, the so-called "last ditch" Arisaka rifles for example*. Economy measures are also evident in this late production officer's sword. Comparing it the earlier Type 94/98, all of the castings, including the menuki, are much plainer or absent entirely, and the wrapping of the grip is a simpler style. There is no pretense of ray skin under the wrappings on the grip. The scabbard is painted canvas over wood and has no hangers or an ishizuke protecting the end of the scabbard. For all of the economy measures it is still a nice sword. It is of a type referred to as a "two button sword," for the obvious reason that it has a button both on the sword and on the scabbard that can be pushed to allow the two to separate. The two-buttons allow for greater flexibility about how the sword is gripped when the blade is drawn. This might have been more important for this type of sword since it did not have a hanger loop.

My knife maker friend Steve Bloom thinks that it is a hand-forged blade. There is a "cloud temper" line relatively close to the edge.
