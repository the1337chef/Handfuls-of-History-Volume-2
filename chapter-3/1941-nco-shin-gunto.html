1941

The Type 95 shin guntō (new sword) was introduced in 1935 as part of the Empire's reverting from European style swords back to more traditional-style Japanese swords. The introduction of these new swords marked a change in the Japanese attitude toward the rest of the "modern world" and Europe in particular. They no longer felt they were as good as the Europeans, they knew they were better than the Europeans. By embracing the icons of the Samurai and giving them to the modern soldiers of the Empire, Japan was creating a mass army of invincible warriors. These common soldiers were spiritually empowered with the legendary strengths of the ancients. A sergeant of this new army was worthy of a sword of the Samurai.

The traditional swords of the Samurai were products of highly skilled craftsman, and each one was a worthy heirloom. The new army needed tens of thousands more swords than even could have been provided by all of the swords smiths of a hundred years earlier, and they needed them right away. The process of producing a traditional katana is long and involved, even down to how the hilt was wrapped. Making a new guntō, of a grade suitable for a noncommissioned officer, required making it look right even though it was product of mass production. The hilt of a Type 95 shin guntō was cast as a single piece of aluminum but treated to give it an appearance of age and intricacy, even down to false cherry blossom menuki cast into the simulated wrapping. The blades were sharp and certainly deadly, but not cast of the same steel as the legendary swords of the Samurai era. All the parts were there but they were not individualized to tell the story of a single warrior, but rather standardized to confirm the spiritual dedication of each soldier to his Emperor.

There are a set of marks under the hilt, on the band known as the habaki or fuchi, that tell some of the story of this sword. The mark on the left is identified in the Jim Dawson book "Swords of Imperial Japan 1868-1945 Cyclopedia Edition," page 190, as the Suya company logo. The one in the middle is the kanji TO as in Tokyo, as was used as the arsenal inspection mark of Tokyo First Arsenal. Plate 15-18 on the same page shows the fuchi of a copper-handled Type 95 NCO sword with exactly the same markings as on this sword with this notation: Dawson relates, "Together they indicate that the sword was made wholly or in part by the Suya Sword Shop for Tokyo First Arsenal during the time that Tokyo First produced swords under Kokura administrative control." Located in Tokyo, the Suya company was used by the Suikosha, or Navy Supply Center. The owner of the company was Mr. Shimada. This company started back in the Meiji era and had a long history of making high quality military swords, including those for members of the Imperial family and many high-ranking officers and diplomats.

Clearly though, this was a sword for a common soldier, and unfortunately it payed a price for years of poor storage. I purchased it through eBay and the vendor was not very forthcoming about the condition of the blade. It was almost certainly a "bring back" from the war and, when captured, I imagine that the blade was pristine. How it ever got so bad is hard to say, probably it was stored in a basement or other humid spot and sat ignored for decades.
