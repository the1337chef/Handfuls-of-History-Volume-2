1840

This was my first Japanese sword. By all indications, it is a high quality Katana and there is no indication that it was post-Meiji military shin guntō, as they have standardized fittings marked with the Imperial chrysanthemum. Since members of the Samurai were forbidden to wear swords after 1870, production of such swords fell off. These facts lead me to date this sword to before the Meiji restoration. None of the fittings carry a recognizable mon, so it would not have come from a particularly wealthy family. The scabbard, inlayed with mother-of-pearl, is attractive but rather unusual as katana scabbards are usually simply lacquered or have more pictorial inlays. The scabbard had a kozuka, a handle fitting for a kogatana (small utility knife) but it was empty. The kogatana shown was custom made for by Steve Bloom, the smith who wrote the introductory essay on steels. The grip is ray skin and menuki are simple sprays of leaves.
