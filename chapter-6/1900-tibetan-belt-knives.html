Tibetan belt knives

Circa 1900

These are a pair of traditional women's utility knives used by Tibetan nomads. They were acquired together. They each have elaborate silver scabbards housing strong steel blades. They were probably made in the region around Lhatse in the early 1900's. Worn on the belt as part of the native dress, they provided both ornate embellishments and ready tools.

The wider one combines symbolic images along with natural turquoise and red coral cabochons. The large turquoise stone in the center of the hilt may represent the sacred silver lotus blossom. It is paired with another large stone on the scabbard, as well as smaller stones. The raised stones are reminiscent of the large raise pommel stone on the Tibetan tsep-sa. The longer knife shares some of the same decorative features, with the raised coral cabochons, but with more elaborate work in the silver relief images of what appear to be dragons.

These are both attractive and very useful tools for daily life, not intended to serve as weapons. The blades of both pieces are strong and very sharp. As would be expected for belt knives, they have attachment loops on the back of the scabbards. While the backsides are not as elaborate as the outer side, they still are nicely engraved, with more attention to detail than is typically seen on the backside of other showy weapons such as Arab jambiyas or some Indonesian daggers like the sewar.
