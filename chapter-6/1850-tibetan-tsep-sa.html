Circa 1850

Throughout the entire 19th century Tibet was effectively isolated from the rest of the world, aside from its protector, China. When the British sent an expedition of 3,000 soldiers with modern weapons into Tibet in 1903 they were met with local soldiers outfitted with armor hundreds of years old and armed with swords, spears, and matchlock muskets. The Dalai Lama fled the country and the British, realizing that Tibet presented neither great opportunity nor threat, left soon after as the Chinese rattled their own sabers.

While the army of Tibet at the beginning of the 20th century was mostly symbolic, Tibetans held swords and other ancient weapons in high esteem. Prior to the occupation by Communist China, the great prayer festivals held in Lhasa featured symbolic battles between ornately clad warriors. The pantheon of Tibetan Buddhist deities feature guardian deities known as dharmapala and most monasteries in Tibet have special chapels known as gonkhang in which offerings of votive or captured weapons are stored for the spiritual use of the dharmapala.

In old Tibet a sword was also part of the expected dress for an upper class male. This Tibetan short sword, of a type known as tsep-sa, is of classic Tibetan design, featuring a trefoil pommel with a coral piece set in the center and a ray skin grip. The single-edged blade is also very typical. However, the plain steel, rather than pattern-welded, blade and the unadorned scabbard suggest that it was not the sword of a very wealthy man. Two of the pictures show this sword in comparison the short sword from neighboring Bhutan.
