Circa 1880

Tibetan Buddhism is rich with ritual and superstitions. There may have been but one Buddha, but have been many bodhisattvas, spiritual incarnations of the enlightened one, which may be a carryover from the many avatars of the Hindu deities. The spiritual world of Tibet was also rife with demons from which even your daily portion of rice had to be protected. These spirits were likely from a time before the Buddha, and so too were the rituals to cast them out. The phurba is an ancient style of sacred dagger that is believed to go back to the age when the Tibetan people were nomadic tent dwellers, since the name itself is a term for a stake or peg used to tether a tent. The use of a sacred phurba was a way to bless and purify the ground where the tent was to be pitched. phurbas were also used bless and purify offering of foods, but usually not flesh. Typically the blades of a phurba are not sharpened, as the object is not intended to be used as a weapon.

The three-fold design of the phurba is one of the sources for its spiritual power. The pommel has the faces of three deities and the blade is always of three vanes. Phurbas are not always made of metal, but those which are often have three types of metal. The body of this dagger is brass or bronze, and there is a central shaft of another sort of metal, while the blades are made of aged steel. It is quite possible that the blades are made of meteorite steel since that source of steel was believed to have special powers against spirits who were not of this earth.

There are many special legends and superstitions about the phurba. It may fly of its own power and volition. The heads of the pommel may animate and bite an unworthy holder. So far this phurba has stayed where I placed it and I remain unbitten.
