Circa 1870 to1920

The 100 years from the Taiping rebellion in 1850 to the Communist takeover in 1950 were amongst the bloodiest in all of Chinese history, with rebel armies fighting Imperial troops, Imperial troops fighting French and British armies in the Second Opium war, Chinese rebels facing European troops during the Boxer Rebellion and then 50 years of civil war between warlords, the Nationalists and the Communists and 10 years of fighting the invading Japanese. Through all of this, the Chinese peasants undoubtedly suffered most grievously. During this time, China was desperate for weapons, since the Europeans restricted arms sales to China, except for the purpose of fighting the Japanese during World War II. Although it was a century marked by great improvements in firearms, few of those modern weapons would have found their way to China, and virtually none would have been available outside the major armies. If guns were unavailable, a good edged weapon would have been better than nothing for a guerilla soldier or a peasant defending their home and family.

This very nice knife appears to be a conversion of an earlier pole arm, probably captured from the Imperial army sometime in the 19th century. The blade is marked with Latin numbers, which are not quite right, and may have been a later addition. The blade was very well made with decorative inlays. Conveniently, the dimensions of the socket work well for a handle. The simple wood scabbard is bound with thread and old fabric strips and is perfectly adequate for this repurposed weapon.
