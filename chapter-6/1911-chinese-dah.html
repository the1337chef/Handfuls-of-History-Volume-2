Circa 1911

This knife is in the style of a Burmese dah, although clearly it was made in China. The blade is 8.5 inches long and the handle is of elephant ivory with a finely incised rustic scene of a mountain, a forest and a boat on some lake or stream. The Chinese characters translate one at a time to something like "enter mountain wood mingle/closing/united." The pommel is a silver casting, perhaps with the image of a dragon. For as lovely as the handle is, the blade is rather crudely fashioned from an old file or rasp. Perhaps it replaces an older blade. It is likely that the blade and handle were joined in the early 20th century, when China was in chaos and weapons of any sort were valued. Such times call on people to be resourceful and adapt whatever may come to hand, whether a giant sword fashioned from a pole-arm, or knife made from a spear point.

In Burma, every man would have a dah, usually somewhat larger than this example. They could serve as weapons or to cut a path through the jungle. It is hard to imagine the intended use for this knife, except to speculate that it does come from two different times-one of tranquil reflection, the other of difficult necessity.
