Circa 1865

The ancestral home of the Viet people was the delta region of the Red River (also known as the Sông Hồng) in the far north of the modern country of Vietnam. There is a tradition of legendary kings of the Viet people, the Hồng Bàng, going back to nearly 3,000 BCE. However, the Viet were conquered by the Chinese in 111 BCE and were absorbed into the Chinese Empire for a thousand years. As the China's Tang dynasty was losing control of the Chinese empire, the Viet people of the province previously known as Annan, formed an autonomous tributary state, Giao Châu. In 938 the Southern Han, the new rulers of China, sent troops recover control of Annan but they were defeated by forces led by Ngô Quyền, who subsequently declared himself king of the Viet people.

The people who emerged from the centuries of Chinese domination were in many ways more like their Chinese overlord than their Hồng Bàng ancestors. The new kings instituted their own forms of Imperial examinations and a Chinese-style Mandarin administration. In some ways, the new nation of Vietnam remained in the shadow of the Chinese Empire, and for a brief period (1407-1427), under the Ming Dynasty, they were reabsorbed by the Empire.

Vietnam experienced its own spats of dynastic transitions and civil wars. The victors in these conflicts would seek to be acknowledged by the rulers of China as the rightful kings. The Chinese Emperors would grant them titles and in return, the new Viet Emperors would send tributes to China.

The Emperors of Vietnam accomplished something few other leaders of that era did, they successfully fought off Mongol invasions, not just once but twice. The Viet Emperors also progressively expanded the country to the south, fighting a series of wars against the Islamic Cham people. By 1802 the country had expanded to the size we associate with its current boundaries, and Nguyễn Ánh established the Nguyễn dynasty, the last dynasty to rule Vietnam.

In settling his dynastic accession, the new Emperor Nguyễn had received some help from the French, who were exploring the colonial opportunities in Southeast Asia. This deal with the devil was ultimately the undoing of the Empire of Vietnam. Due to the assistance he received from French navy, the new Emperor Nguyễn was inclined to show a tolerance toward a French presence in his new court and the Catholic missionaries in his country. This was something that his successors could not approve. However, as the later emperors tried to reverse the trend and expel the missionaries, the French were quick to take the excuse of protecting French citizens to invade and expand their colony of Cochinchina to encompass Vietnam. By 1885 the Vietnamese people had lost control of their country, and it became a protectorate of France. The French maintained the Nguyen kings as figureheads up until 1946, but kept them under close watch, deposing three of them who were then sent into exile in Algeria.

The Guom is a traditional sword of the Vietnamese courts. It is thought to be styled after the legendary Thuận Thiên, the sword of the Vietnamese King Lê Lợi who freed Vietnam from the last Ming domination in 1427. This particular sword is from the Nguyen period of the mid-19th century. The Guoms of this period are especially lovely swords, always with beautiful delicate inlays on the scabbard. Just looking at the sword can give you a false impression of the size. The ivory grip is larger than expected for a single-hand, making it effectively a hand and a half or even two-hand sword. The blade shows European influence and may have been imported or adapted from a European saber. The pommel shows what is likely Chinese influence, since it appears to be in the image of a traditional Chinese dog-faced guardian lion. It is sharp and would make a formidable weapon, but was more likely the sword of a courtier than a warrior.
