Circa 1890

Trousses like this are usually eating sets with a knife, a pair of chopsticks, and sometimes other accessories such as tweezers or a pickle spear. Such a set would be part of a man's formal dress, and they were common during the late Qing dynasty. This one however is a bit odd, because in place of chopsticks there appear to be a pair of bone knitting needles. I have tried to see if they could be used as chopsticks, and I think that with a bit of practice they could be, at least to my limited level of skill. It has been suggested that this kit was intended for a woman, rather than for a man. We have several female friends who take knitting with them everywhere but I rather doubt that this was a custom among the Chinese upper class.

The decorations appear to be painted (enamel) rather than inlays. They show a bit of wear in several areas. The designs are a mix of flowers and animals. The needles are a good fit to their compartment. The channels built for them are far too small to accommodate normal sized chopsticks. The knife is solidly made, with a full tang. The blade is sharpened but not as sharp as it might be. I would not want to try to cut a steak with it.

The set is presumably from China, but similar sets were used in various countries around China, especially in tributary nations like Korea. They came out of an auction wear most of the items were Tibetan, such as the small belt knives.
