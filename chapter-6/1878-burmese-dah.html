Burmese Dahs

The word "dah" (also dha) is simply Chinese for sword, so that term maybe applied to long knives or swords from various Asian countries including China and Thailand. Swords identified as dah have round handles with slender, single-edged blades and no cross guard or pommel. They can be applied to many purposes, from cutting paths through the jungle to warfare. Most are of rather simple construction, like the Thai examples, others were the swords of rich men and can be quite beautiful. The style of these two dahs is Burmese and they probably date from the time of the first two Anglo-Burmese wars.

At the beginning of the Common Era, the region of the Irrawaddy River Valley which constitutes the heart of the region we know as Burma (Myanmar), was occupied Tibeto-Burman-speaking people known as the Pyu. Mostly Buddhist, they were primarily a sedentary-agrarian people, living in numerous small city-states. During the 9th century there was a large migration of Mranma (Burmans) from the present day Yunnan region of China. These more warlike people came to dominate the area, founding a series of militant Empires, expanding or contracting their borders between the Khmers to the east and the Kingdoms of India to the west in a long sequence of wars. The rulers of Burma established their courts on the style of the Chinese Emperor, but the country was home to numerous minority tribes as well, such as the Kachin and the Mon.

The Konbaung Dynasty ruled Burma from 1752 to 1885, when the territorial ambitions of the last kings brought about the country's demise at the hands of the British. While the first two Anglo-Burmese Wars merely shrank back the country's borders, the third war resulted in the expulsion of the last king, Thibaw Min, and the presentation of a new province to Queen Victoria by the English Parliament in 1886.

Burma did not acquiesce easily to British rule and resistance was suppressed with a heavy hand, resorting to the systematic destruction of entire villages in the north. The economy grew, but only for the profit of the Anglo-Burmese. While literacy increased, by the early 20th century, nationalism and independence movements became widespread. World War II brought Japanese occupation and the Japanese sought to further the anti-British movements by granting Burma nominal independence from Great Britain in 1943. The Japanese-bestowed "independence" was, of course, not recognized by the Allies after the defeat of Japan, but the independence movements were too strong to hold back for long. In 1948 Burma was granted independence. Unlike India and Pakistan, Burma chose not to join the British Commonwealth. Burma has been wracked with internal conflicts, and occasional military dictatorships ever since gaining independence. In 1989 the military government declared that the name of the country in English should be Myanmar rather than Burma.

Circa 1878

The blade of this sword is very well forged and 21.5 inches long. There is almost a resemblance to Japanese swords. The grip is nice old ivory with silver mounts. The scabbard is very basic, just wood with fiber bands. It is unlikely that the red cord is original. Overall, the piece is more functional than ornamental, a real fighting sword from a nation struggling against European imperialism. The owner of this sword was neither very rich nor very poor.


Silver Inlaid Dah

Circa 1878

This sword is a true work of art and as such was probably owned by someone of noble birth. It would also serve, however, as a very effective weapon. The heavy up-curving blade is 28 inches long. The center panel of the blade on both sides is very richly decorated with silver inlay showing various animals in a floral background. Although somewhat fanciful, it is ornamented with birds, crocodiles, lions, monkeys, dogs, buffalo, ordinary elephants and elephants with wings, a monkey with a beak, and a wolf with horns. The handle has a solid wooden core and is wrapped in silver and engraved with the figures of half a dozen dancers. Over time the scabbard has gone missing, probably put aside to better display the blade.
